Option 2 · red-team (v0.2)

Pressure-Test — Option 2

Re-tested as the determined destination — right shape, not yet sound. Build-time retired; the danger moves to a built-but-wrong Core.

Option 236 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-pressure-test-option-2-v0.2.md
In brief

A hostile re-test of the selected destination. Build-time is retired, but that sharpens the real risk: a Core that can be built will compile a wrong specification just as readily as a right one — and FTLAB’s own load-bearing primitives are still defined inconsistently across the corpus.

Key takeaways
  • Critical: buildable ≠ correctly specified — the verifier checks against the spec, so a wrong spec propagates as authority.
  • Critical: dashboard oversight buys bandwidth, not independence — a conflicted Guardian just rubber-stamps faster.
  • Critical: Oligarch’s Temptation — the Core concentrates infra + capital + charters + rules; Strategy-as-Code makes it more durable, not less.
  • Verdict: right destination shape; four conditions first (freeze + red-team the spec; external Guardian halt-right; external accountability terminus; move origination out of the Core).

This is a hostile review. Option 2 is no longer one option among four — the determination selects it as the destination, so the standard rises: a destination must survive attack on its chosen primitive, not merely out-argue the alternatives. This pass does not relitigate the two-jobs-of-hierarchy split (conceded, and still the strongest idea in the corpus) and does not relitigate buildability or build-duration of the Core (retired by the determination §8, and the retirement is accepted in full). It attacks something the retirement makes sharper, not softer: a Core that can be built is not the same object as a Core that is correctly specified. A built-but-misspecified Core is more dangerous than an unbuilt one, because it runs at compute speed, wears the legitimacy of "the substrate," and propagates its misspecification to every Engine that reads it before a human is in the loop. The findings below concentrate there, and on the one Critical the determination itself names and defers: independent Guardianship at 2–5 humans. Evidence is graded strong or contested. The verdict is at the end.

A note on standard. The determination is right that the prior analyses hedged toward Option 1 on build-time grounds, and right to retire those grounds. But the head-to-head v0.2 (31 May) reached its Option-2 findings on a different basis: it rated Option 2's hardest problems architectural — "not carpentry" — and recommended Option-1-led precisely because those architectural problems are not refactorable. The determination (1 June) overturns the recommendation by retiring build-time. That move is valid against the build-time hedge. It does not touch the architectural findings, which were never about build-time. This pass tests whether those findings survive — and finds that the most serious of them do.

1. What is being tested

The determination makes four load-bearing claims this pass attacks:

  1. That the Core is "a concrete, buildable artefact with a known specification" (determination §3b), and that because it can be built, a design depending on it is sound. Tested: whether "known specification" is true — whether FTLAB can author and maintain a correct Strategy-as-Code repository, given that its own foundational primitives are defined incompatibly across its live corpus and have not been reconciled.
  2. That FTLAB is a Mothership and can meet the Five Conditions for Mothership Success (determination §3c, model §4). Tested: the two conditions FTLAB's size makes hardest — multi-year platform commitment, and founder-level talent/comp per venture — against a 2–5 human pool and the attention-scarcity the founders themselves name binding.
  3. That the Core·Engines·Edge boundary is stable — Core routes, Engines produce, Edge governs. Tested: whether, in the actual staged charters, the Engines and the strategic-judgment half of the Edge collapse into the Core, reconstituting a single point of control.
  4. That dashboard-based oversight (evaluator-agents, statistical sampling, the weekly random audit) delivers governance at small scale. Tested: whether it delivers independence or merely bandwidth — the inherited Critical the determination §7 concedes and defers.

The model answers the named Book-1 shadows in a summary table (model §8). This pass treats that table as a target: a mechanism named is not a mechanism shown correct, and several of the answers presuppose exactly the capacity (a correct spec, an independent Guardian, an un-colonised Cortex) the findings below dispute.

2. Findings

CRITICAL 1 — "Buildable" is not "correctly specified," and FTLAB cannot yet author a correct Core spec from a corpus that contradicts itself

The claim under attack. Determination §3b: the Core is "a concrete, buildable artefact with a known specification… the book is, in effect, the build-spec for the Core." Model §2.1: Strategy-as-Code, hardConstraints, the Five Planes, VP-Agents — "an engineering programme, not a research gamble."

Steelman. The component list is genuinely specified: anyone reading Book 1 Ch 5–6 and the framework can name the parts (charters, repo, IPRE, Governance Loop). And the retirement of build-time is correct — none of what follows is a build-duration objection.

Where it breaks. A specification of what components exist is not a specification of what they should contain. The Core is only as correct as the Strategy-as-Code, the hardConstraints, the Boundary Layer (C2a) decision-classes, and the Agent Charters that get written into it. FTLAB's own evidence says it cannot yet author those correctly, because it cannot yet keep its own primitives straight:

  1. The institution's load-bearing terms are defined two or three incompatible ways, and — as of the canonical-terms doc — "nothing canonical has been rewritten yet." Per ftlab-canonical-terms-resolution-v0.1.md: the ratio is 5:100 and 1:500 and 1:100–1:10,000 across live documents; "Five Planes" means the technical stack and the org-layers; "the New Triumvirate" is Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian and Human-Judgment/Machine-Intelligence/Institutional-Design; "VP-Agent" is Values-and-Preferences and Virtual-Professional. The doc states plainly: "If one reads 'Five Planes' as a technical stack and another as organisational layers, they will build incompatible mental models of the same institution. Coordination-through-memory fails silently exactly here." These are proposed resolutions awaiting founder confirmation — not applied. The Core's repository would be authored now, from this corpus.
  2. A built Core compiles whichever contradictory definition it happened to ingest — and then runs it at compute speed. Strategy-as-Code's entire value proposition is that it makes strategy "precise, testable, reversible." But precision over a contradictory source does not produce correctness; it produces one arbitrary branch of the contradiction, hardened into executable form and propagated to every Engine that reads it. If the hardConstraints are derived from a Values definition that itself collides with a Preferences definition (Resolution 4), the architecturally-prior penalty fires on the wrong actions, or fails to fire — and it does so silently, because the repo is the ground truth against which everything else is checked. There is no higher authority in the system to detect that the authority is wrong.
  3. The verifier cannot catch a misspecified spec — it checks against it. The staged verification-layer.md is explicit: C5 checks artifacts "against the Boundary Layer (C2a) to confirm the decision class is correctly classified and the correct authority is exercising it," and the human Verification function "authors the hard-check thresholds and the Boundary Layer (C2a) classification table that C5 applies." So the spec is the verifier's yardstick. A gamed output is detectable; a wrong yardstick is not, because the instrument built to detect deviation defines deviation by reference to the thing that is wrong. Specification gaming (Book 1's named shadow, MAST's 42% specification-failure share cited in the model) is not merely an agent behaviour here — it is what the whole Core does to a mis-authored spec, faithfully and fast.

Why it bites. This is the precise object the new ground rule directs attention to, and it is worse than the retired build-time concern. A slow build delays value; a fast, confident, misspecified Core produces wrong action with the full authority of "the substrate" and the full reach of "everything flows through the Core" (model §2, diagram). The determination treats "known specification" as settled because the parts are named. The parts are named; the content is, by FTLAB's own audit, internally contradictory and unwritten. Buildable, yes. Correctly specifiable today, no.

Severity: Critical. Evidence: strong — the contradictions, their unresolved status, and the silent-coordination-failure diagnosis are FTLAB's own document, not an external claim.

Concrete fix.

  1. Gate the Core build on a frozen, founder-confirmed canonical lexicon. Make the application of ftlab-canonical-terms-resolution-v0.1.md (all six resolutions, plus the framework v1→v1.1 pass) a hard Phase-0 precondition — before any hardConstraints or Strategy-as-Code is authored. No repo entry may reference a term that still has two live definitions.
  2. Treat the Strategy-as-Code repository as a specification under adversarial review, not as documentation. Stand up a standing "spec red-team" whose only job is to find clauses whose meaning is ambiguous, whose hardConstraints admit a gaming path, or whose Values/Preferences boundary is contestable — and to do so against a held-out set of hard cases the spec did not anticipate. Correctness of the spec is a measured, contested property, not an authoring assumption.
  3. Build a spec-drift instrument distinct from output-drift. Alignment Debt as specified (model §2.1f) measures the gap between stated values and agent behaviour. Add a second gap: between intended values and encoded values — periodically re-derive a sample of hardConstraints from first principles and diff against the repo. The repo being wrong must be a detectable event, not an undetectable premise.

CRITICAL 2 — Dashboard oversight solves bandwidth, not independence; the staged charters show two of three powers in one founder-pair and the third leg vacant

The claim under attack. Determination §3f and model §7: Book 1 Ch 8–9 "dissolve the bandwidth half" of the Guardian problem (evaluator-agents, statistical sampling, override-rate sensor, weekly random audit), so "the Guardian governs by dashboard, not by reviewing every decision." The independence half is conceded as the inherited Critical, to be solved by structural protections + an external check.

Steelman. The bandwidth claim is true and is a real achievement: a single human genuinely cannot review thousands of agent actions, and statistical oversight over an immutable trace is the only pattern that makes substantive oversight achievable at all. The determination is also honest that independence is unsolved.

Where it breaks. The model and determination treat bandwidth and independence as two halves of one problem, with the bandwidth half "substantially solved by engineering." That framing obscures that the engineering solves only bandwidth, and bandwidth without independence is the more dangerous configuration — a Guardian with perfect instrumentation and no standing to act on it ratifies faster and more confidently. The staged operational layer makes the deficit concrete, not hypothetical:

  1. In the actual charters, the separation of powers is not separated. intelligence-router.md §Governance and verification-layer.md §Governance both specify the interim Edge as: Direction → Shayan (Managing Partner); Governance, Values & Coherence + the legal/fiduciary terminus → "Mehran + founders jointly"; Verification & Quality → "Shared / independent reviewer as team grows." So today: the capital-and-attention allocator and the values-halt-right-holder are the same two-person founding pair, and the third leg — the one that reviews the verifier and holds the circuit-breaker — is explicitly unstaffed. A "separation of powers" with two of three powers in one pair and the third vacant is not a separation; it is a single locus of control with three labels.
  2. A Guardian who holds the halt-right and the legal terminus and shares the founders' P&L cannot halt a revenue-driving venture against himself. Book 1's own prescription ("no P&L, does not report to the CEO") is violated structurally, not by oversight. The dashboard tells this Guardian, in 95%-confidence detail, that a venture is drifting; nothing in the architecture gives him standing to act when acting is financially or socially costly to the founding group. Better dashboards make this worse: they remove every excuse of not knowing, leaving only the conflict of not wanting to.
  3. The model's own "fix" presupposes the thing in dispute. Model §7 says Guardianship becomes "a property of the substrate that survives any single human." But the substrate's outputs are read and acted upon by the Edge, and the Edge's independent leg is vacant. A property of the substrate that no independent human has standing to enforce is telemetry, not governance. The external check ("an independent director… earlier than is comfortable") is named but undated and uncommitted — the same "as the team grows" deferral the head-to-head v0.2 §5 flagged as the recurring unsolved Critical and the one a regulator "will not let you defer."

Why it bites. This is the determination's own §7 Critical, and it does not weaken under the new ground rule — it is capability-independent and build-independent. The danger is that the bandwidth solution reads as progress on the whole problem. It is not. Independence is the binding half, it is unsolved in the live charters, and the model's substrate-based answer to it is circular.

Severity: Critical. Evidence: strong — the Edge composition is quoted verbatim from the staged charters; the structural conflict is Book 1's own prescription inverted.

Concrete fix.

  1. Externalise the third leg now, with a real exercisable veto, as a Phase-0 gate — not "as the team grows." An independent director or external Guardian-auditor with read access to the immutable trace and a standing, logged halt-right on values/irreversibility/novelty classes. Until that person exists and has acted at least once, the Core does not run autonomously on any externally-consequential class.
  2. Make the founder-Guardian's "never" rules discretion-free and pre-committed. Bright-line prohibitions that bind without requiring the conflicted human to judge against his own P&L (adopt the head-to-head v0.2 §8 fix verbatim). A rule that requires the conflicted party to choose against his own interest in the moment is not a control.
  3. Instrument independence as a measured quantity, not an assumed one. Track and publish: number of Guardian overrides exercised against revenue interest; time-to-act on dashboard-flagged drift; the override-rate of the external check on the internal Guardian. If those numbers are zero, "independent Guardianship" is a title, and the dashboard is a rubber-stamp with good resolution (the exact failure GDPR Art. 22 and EU AI Act Art. 14, cited approvingly in the model, treat as no oversight at all).

CRITICAL 3 — The Oligarch's Temptation: a Core that holds infrastructure, capital, charters, and the rules-repository is the FTX/WeWork structural pattern, with no external accountability terminus

The claim under attack. Determination §6f names "the Oligarch's Temptation" as a shadow every re-run must answer. The model does not answer it — it appears in neither the §8 shadow table nor §7. This is a gap, and it is the most consequential one.

Steelman. The model's intent is the opposite of oligarchic: separation of powers, Strategy-as-Code for transparency, an external check. The instinct is right.

Where it breaks. Michels' Iron Law of Oligarchy is not defeated by intent; it is a structural prediction — that any organisation concentrating the means of coordination, resource, and rule-making in a small group will trend toward control by that group regardless of its democratic or stewardship commitments. Option 2, as determined, concentrates every such means in the same 2–5 humans and their Core:

  1. Single locus of: infrastructure + capital + charters + the rules-as-code repository. The Core is the coordination infrastructure ("coordination flows through the Core"). Direction holds capital allocation and attention (model §2.3). The Edge authors the Agent Charters and the Strategy-as-Code that bound every agent. The same Edge authors the Boundary Layer that decides what is even escalated to a human. There is no layer of the institution — operational, financial, or constitutional — that does not terminate in the founding pair. This is precisely the concentration Michels predicts will self-perpetuate, and it is the structural signature common to FTX (founder-controlled infra + capital + the rules, no independent check that could act) and WeWork (founder-controlled narrative + capital + governance, board captured). The pattern is not about fraud; it is about the absence of any node with both the information and the standing to say no.
  2. Strategy-as-Code makes the concentration more durable, not less. A constitution in a version-controlled repo controlled by the same people who allocate capital is not a check on those people — it is their preferences rendered authoritative and difficult to contest, with a commit history that looks like governance. "If your governance isn't in the repository, it isn't governance" cuts both ways: governance that is only in a repository the founders control is governance the founders can rewrite. The model never specifies who, outside the founding pair, can refuse a Strategy-as-Code change.
  3. The external-accountability gap is total. Risk Twins (10× adversarial simulation) gate Strategy-as-Code changes (model §2.1f) — but Risk Twins are themselves part of the Core the founders author. The weekly random audit is performed by the Core for the Edge. Nothing in the architecture answers to anyone outside the founding group. For a studio that intends to take sovereign capital and operate regulated ventures (Adaptic in finance), an institution with no external accountability terminus is not merely a governance weakness — it is an un-investable and likely un-licensable structure.

Why it bites. The determination flagged this shadow as mandatory to answer and the model left it unanswered, while building the exact concentration the shadow warns of. Combined with Critical 2 (no independent leg) this is the FTX/WeWork structural pattern stated in AI-born vocabulary: a benevolent founding group, total control of infra + capital + rules, and no node that can act against them. Benevolence is not a control. The Iron Law is indifferent to it.

Severity: Critical. Evidence: strong on the structural concentration (it is the architecture's own design); contested only on whether the analogy to specific failures (FTX/WeWork) is fair — but the structural property (concentration without external terminus) is undisputed and is the load-bearing point.

Concrete fix.

  1. Install an external accountability terminus with teeth before scale. Not advisory — an independent board with the power to remove a founder from the Guardian role, veto a Strategy-as-Code change, and compel disclosure. The studio's own framework (§ on sovereign engagement) insists on "alignment without capture"; the institution must hold itself to the same standard it sells.
  2. Separate the rule-making authority from the capital authority in the repo's permissions, literally. Strategy-as-Code changes touching Values/hardConstraints/escalation thresholds require sign-off from a role that holds no P&L and is not the Intent-Setter. Encode the separation in the repository's own access control, not in prose.
  3. Add the Oligarch's Temptation to the standing risk register and the Alignment Debt instrument as a named metric — e.g., concentration of revocable authority in the founding pair, tracked over time and reported externally. A shadow the determination named cannot be left absent from the model that the determination selects.

MAJOR 1 — The Core·Engines·Edge boundary is not stable: in the staged charters the Core absorbs deal-origination (an Engine function) and reduces the Intent-Setter to a yes/no on Core-framed candidates

The claim under attack. Determination §4 and model §2: the boundary is clean — "the Core does the routing… the Engines localise accountability… the Edge governs; it does not route." Three rings, three jobs.

Steelman. The conceptual split is sound, and "Engines produce, Core coordinates, Edge governs" is the right target. Keeping coordination out of the Engines and out of the Edge is exactly the two-jobs insight applied.

Where it breaks. The staged intelligence-router.md — the Core's coordinator (C4) — does not stay inside the routing job. It performs work the model assigns to the Generation Engine (E1) and to the Intent-Setter (Edge), collapsing two of the three rings into the centre:

  1. The Core originates ventures. The router's "Defining Behaviour B" makes it "the studio's deal-origination engine": every composition-failure is logged as "a candidate venture thesis," and "the studio's comparative advantage… is that its operating architecture generates thesis candidates." But the model §2.2 assigns hypothesis production to E1 — Research & Intelligence ("framework + venture-hypothesis production"). Origination is therefore performed in both the Core and an Engine, and the Core's version is upstream — it decides what reaches E1's queue. The "Core only routes" boundary is breached by the Core's own flagship feature.
  2. The Intent-Setter is reduced to ratifying Core-framed candidates. The router "packages the candidate entry… and surfaces it for Direction's decision," and its stated job is "to make that judgment easy and well-informed — not to make it." This is the classic framing-controls-the-decision move: whoever sets the option set holds more power than whoever picks from it. The Core decides which gaps become candidates, frames each as a problem statement, and presents a curated queue; the Intent-Setter says yes or no. That is not "the Edge directs"; it is the Edge ratifying the Core's strategic agenda — epistemic colonisation (the determination §6 shadow) operating through the deal pipeline, not just the dashboard.
  3. One agent holds composition + topology + world-model writes + origination — a harder, more central singleton than the thing Option 2 charges the alternatives with. The router composes capabilities, selects topology per task (a judgment the model itself rates load-bearing, citing a 58–515% overhead tax for getting it wrong), is "one of the heaviest readers and writers of the world model," and originates theses. The prior pressure-test (v0.1 Findings 4, 9) named this; the staged charter confirms it verbatim. The determination claims Option 2 escapes the central coordinator; the operational layer shows a more loaded one, now also holding the origination agenda.

Why it bites. Boundary stability is what makes the three-ring claim more than a diagram. If the Core both coordinates and originates, and the Edge's strategic leg only ratifies, then "Engines produce / Edge governs / Core routes" is not the operating reality — the Core is the institution's executive, and the humans are its approvers. That is the single-point-of-control the architecture is sold as dissolving.

Severity: Major (escalates toward Critical if the router's origination role is retained at scale without an Engine-side or Edge-side counterweight, because it makes the strategic agenda a Core output). Evidence: strong — quoted from the staged intelligence-router.md.

Concrete fix.

  1. Move origination out of the Core. The router may detect and log a composition-failure as a capability-roadmap item (a coordination fact); the framing of any gap as a candidate venture thesis belongs to E1 (Generation), and the option set presented to Direction must be authored by E1, not by C4. The Core surfaces gaps; an Engine frames theses; the Edge directs. Restore the three rings.
  2. Give the Intent-Setter an independent origination input. Direction must be able to put theses into the pipeline that did not originate in a Core composition-failure, and must see the gaps the Core did not surface (the model's own §2.1i "the model cannot do the editorial function" applies here). Otherwise the strategic agenda is whatever the Core happens to fail at.
  3. Split the router's roles or cap its autonomy. Topology-selection and world-model writes can stay with C4 under measured-reliability gating; origination-framing cannot. Do not let one Core agent hold coordination and the deal agenda.

MAJOR 2 — FTLAB cannot meet two of the Five Conditions at its size: founder-level comp per venture, and a credible multi-year platform commitment (the platform-starved-at-month-18 failure)

The claim under attack. Determination §6 and model §4: the Five Conditions for Mothership Success are "design requirements… not optional," and the architecture meets them. Two of the five are the ones FTLAB's size makes hardest.

Steelman. FTLAB clearly intends to honour these — ventures get their own brand, their own Edge, single-reporting lines. The fractal model is a genuine attempt to make the conditions structural.

Where it breaks. Two conditions are not design choices FTLAB can grant by architecture; they are resource facts its size contradicts:

  1. Founder-level talent and compensation per venture, from a 2–5 human pool. The model §4 condition 3 requires "talent at the venture Edge… commensurate with what a standalone venture would attract," and Book 1 marks violating this "a leading cause of Mothership failure." But the founders state (Q13) "each venture is envisioned to have its own small human core… not all part of FTLAB headcount," and the model itself names the talent paradox (model §6: automating juniors starves the senior-human pipeline) and apprenticeship as "the one human function that must grow." These collide. Founder-level humans are exactly the scarce, expensive, high-judgement people the studio cannot mass-produce and the AI-born thesis displaces the training pipeline for. The fractal model multiplies the requirement (N ventures × a separated, founder-calibre Edge) against a pool that does not scale with N. The model asserts the condition is met; the founders' own constraints say the supply does not exist.
  2. Multi-year platform commitment, against the founders' stated need to show sovereign traction now. Model §4 condition 4 requires committing to the Core "as a multi-year engineering programme, resisting the pressure to treat it as infrastructure that can be deferred." But the founders state (Q8) FTLAB "must be actively building and doing things in the region for sovereigns to take the company seriously." This is the platform-starved-at-month-18 failure: the studio commits to a multi-year platform build, sovereign pressure demands visible ventures, attention (the framework's named-scarcest resource) is pulled from the platform to the ventures, and the platform is quietly under-resourced precisely when the second and third ventures need it to be mature. The retirement of build-time as an objection to soundness does not retire the commitment risk: the model concedes (§4.4) "the determination doc §8 retires build-time as an objection… but does not pretend the build is trivial." A non-trivial multi-year commitment held by an attention-starved 2–5 person team facing sovereign-traction pressure is the condition most likely to be violated in practice — and Book 1 says violating it is a leading failure cause.

Why it bites. The determination leans the entire Mothership claim on the Five Conditions being met. Two of the five are not architecture-grantable at FTLAB's size — one is a talent-supply fact the thesis itself worsens, the other a commitment FTLAB's own go-to-market pressure undermines. This is not build-time; it is whether the destination is reachable with the people and attention FTLAB actually has.

Severity: Major. Evidence: strong — the conditions are the model's own, the constraints are the founders' own (Q8, Q13), and the talent paradox is the model's own §6.

Concrete fix.

  1. State a venture-count ceiling tied to founder-calibre Edge supply. Do not instantiate a venture until a separated, founder-calibre Edge for it exists (or is externalised). Publish the human-supply math: N ventures require how many separated role-holders, drawn from where. If the math does not close, the portfolio size is capped — say so.
  2. Ring-fence platform attention against sovereign-traction pull. A pre-committed minimum of Cortex attention reserved for the Core build, protected from venture firefighting, with the protection itself a governed (and externally visible) commitment. The month-18 failure is an attention-allocation failure; make the allocation a hard constraint, not an intention.
  3. Make "founder-level comp per venture" an explicit go/no-go on each new venture, and treat apprenticeship/steward-continuity (model §6) as the funded supply line for it from day one — not a function that grows on its own.

MAJOR 3 — "Verify out-of-band" is asserted but its independence is unspecified; the verifier inherits the founders' values and the misspecified spec

The claim under attack. Model §2.1h, §8 and the staged verification-layer.md: specification gaming and escalation suppression are caught by "immutable decision tracing," "meta-evaluators oversee the evaluators," and verification that "checks the artifact against sources and ground truth that are independent of the pipeline that produced it."

Steelman. Separating verification from production is correct and the staged charter is rigorous about it: C5 is out-of-band, adversarial, not throughput-tunable, and a Critical finding is an un-overridable BLOCK. This is genuinely good and should be kept.

Where it breaks. "Out-of-band" is named as a property but never specified as a mechanism, and the things it must be independent of are precisely the things it is not:

  1. Independence from what? The charter says C5 "checks independently" and "does not ask the producing agent." But it does not specify what makes the check independent — different model family? adversarial objective? human-seeded ground truth the verifier cannot see in advance? Without a specified independence mechanism, the verifier and the produced output are correlated: trained on related data, evaluated against related signals, drifting under the same pressures the charter elsewhere says are "a structural equilibrium under finite evaluation." "AI overseeing AI" with both AIs of the same kind stacks a second instance of the same failure; it does not introduce an independent error source (prior pressure-test v0.1 Finding 5, unresolved in the staged charter).
  2. The verifier checks against a spec authored by the conflicted/absent human (links to Critical 1 and 2). C5 verifies against C2a, and C2a is authored by the Verification function — the vacant Edge leg. So the independence chain is: a possibly-correlated verifier agent, checking against a spec authored by an unstaffed human function, on behalf of a founding pair that authored the values the verifier encodes. At no point does an independent judgment enter. Gaming the spec (as opposed to gaming an output) is therefore undetectable, because the spec is the verifier's definition of correct.
  3. The floor-integrity sample assumes a human to sample. The charter's floor-integrity requirement ("confirm a human actually judged, not just approved") is excellent — and it samples escalations returned from the Edge's Verification function, which is vacant. A floor-integrity check on an unstaffed function checks nothing.

Why it bites. Specification gaming and escalation suppression are two of the determination's named shadows, and the model's answers to them all route through C5. If C5's independence is unspecified and its yardstick is a founder-authored spec checked by a vacant human leg, then the answers are nominal. The autonomy-by-measured-reliability mechanism (model §6) promotes task classes to autonomous "when the verifiers demonstrably hold" — so a correlated verifier on a wrong spec widens autonomy on a false signal. This is the failure that compounds silently and is hardest to see precisely because the instrument built to see it is part of what is wrong.

Severity: Major. Evidence: strong — the independence gap is analytic and confirmed by the staged charter's silence; the spec-authorship chain is the charters' own.

Concrete fix.

  1. Specify verifier independence as an implementation requirement in C5's charter, not a value. Mechanically: a different model family from the producer; an adversarial objective; and, for high-stakes/novel classes, human-seeded ground-truth probes the verifier cannot see in advance. State it concretely or the property does not exist.
  2. Add an independent-of-the-spec check. For a sampled set of high-stakes verdicts, have a human (the externalised Guardian-auditor from Critical 2) re-derive the correct answer without reference to C2a, and diff against both the output and the spec. A rising disagreement between "what the spec says" and "what an independent human derives" halts further autonomy promotion. This is the only way gaming-the-spec becomes detectable.
  3. Do not promote any class to autonomous while the Edge Verification leg is vacant. Measured reliability measured by an unwatched verifier is not measured reliability.

MAJOR 4 — "Substitutability is an architectural property" is asserted against a Core that is, in the staged charters, model-pinned — the fragility cascade is not actually mitigated

The claim under attack. Model §8: the fragility cascade from model-vendor dependence is answered by a "provider-agnostic interface at the Core's Actuation plane," and "substitutability is an architectural property, not a procurement aspiration."

Steelman. Owning the orchestration layer and treating models as inputs behind a stable interface is the correct design intent, and is more than most operators do.

Where it breaks. The assertion is contradicted by the institution's own staged configuration and is under-specified as an architectural claim:

  1. The Core is pinned to a single vendor in the actual charters. Both intelligence-router.md and verification-layer.md specify model: opus for the Core's coordinator and verifier. The most capability-sensitive, judgment-heavy components — the ones whose behaviour defines the institution — are configured to one frontier model from one vendor. "Substitutability is architectural" is asserted in the model while the staged Core is single-sourced at exactly the nodes where model behaviour matters most. The interface may be provider-agnostic; the behaviour the institution depends on is not, because a different model behind the same interface is a different coordinator and a different verifier.
  2. Substitutability of interface is not substitutability of behaviour. A stable Actuation-plane API means you can call a different model; it does not mean the topology-selection judgment, the composition reasoning, or the adversarial-verification quality survive the swap. The model conflates "can route to another vendor" with "another vendor produces equivalent governance behaviour." The latter is the fragility that matters, and it is unaddressed.
  3. Same-vendor coordinator and verifier is a correlated-failure risk (links to Major 3). If both C4 and C5 are the same model family, a vendor-side change, a capability regression, or a shared failure mode hits the producer and its checker simultaneously — defeating the out-of-band separation the verifier depends on.

Why it bites. The fragility cascade is a named determination shadow, and the model's answer is contradicted by the institution's own staged files. An architecture whose two most load-bearing agents are single-sourced has a vendor as a single point of failure regardless of how agnostic the interface is.

Severity: Major. Evidence: strong — the model: opus pinning is in the staged charters; the interface-vs-behaviour distinction is analytic.

Concrete fix.

  1. Make the coordinator and the verifier different model families wherever capability allows — at minimum, C5 must not be the same family as C4, to preserve out-of-band independence under a vendor-side failure.
  2. Define substitutability as a tested property: maintain a fallback model behind the interface and periodically run the Core on it on a held-out workload, measuring behavioural drift. Substitutability you have never exercised is procurement aspiration wearing an architectural label.
  3. State the vendor-dependence honestly in the risk register until behavioural substitutability is demonstrated, rather than asserting it is solved.

MAJOR 5 — Cortex atrophy and epistemic colonisation: the model's answers presuppose a Cortex capacity that the Core's own competence and the small pool erode

The claim under attack. Model §8: Cortex atrophy is answered by "the apprenticeship function… named and funded," and epistemic colonisation by "the IPRE Plan stage as the last human checkpoint" and "the Cortex reads dashboards and governs by exception."

Steelman. Naming apprenticeship as the one growing function is genuinely the right instinct, and pricing Cortex attention (Cognitive Overhead Index) is a real diagnostic.

Where it breaks. Both answers presuppose the capacity they are meant to protect:

  1. "The Plan stage is the last human checkpoint" is itself a colonisation vector if the Plan is Core-authored. The IPRE Plan is produced through the Core (the router composes it, the world model informs it). A human "checkpoint" on a plan the Core authored, framed in the Core's terms, supported by the Core's evidence, is exactly the editorial-capture the model elsewhere warns the world model cannot be allowed to perform (model §2.1i). The checkpoint is where colonisation operates, not where it is prevented — the human ratifies the Core's framing because the human has no independent basis from which to reframe it. (This is the same mechanism as Major 1's deal-origination capture, applied to operating plans.)
  2. Governing by dashboard is itself an atrophy driver. The more the Cortex governs by reading the Core's dashboards and approving the Core's exceptions, the less it does the first-hand work that built its judgment — and the less able it becomes to know when the dashboard is wrong (Critical 1's spec-error, Major 3's verifier-error). Atrophy is not a risk the architecture incidentally runs; it is the intended steady state (the Cortex thins, the Core widens), and apprenticeship is asked to counter a force the whole design accelerates. The model does not show that a 2–5 person Cortex, governing increasingly by exception, retains enough first-hand competence to author a correct spec (Critical 1) or to catch a colonising frame.
  3. The apprenticeship supply is the same scarce pool as the Edge (links to Major 2). The function asked to grow is drawn from the talent pool the thesis displaces and the studio cannot mass-produce. "Named and funded" does not produce founder-calibre judgement on demand.

Why it bites. Two named shadows are answered by mechanisms that presuppose Cortex capacity, while the architecture's intended trajectory and FTLAB's pool both erode that capacity. The answers are directionally right and quantitatively unfunded — and they interlock with Critical 1 (a colonised, atrophied Cortex is exactly the Cortex that authors a wrong spec without noticing).

Severity: Major. Evidence: strong on the colonisation-via-the-checkpoint mechanism (it is the model's own structure); contested on the rate of atrophy (no FTLAB operating data yet).

Concrete fix.

  1. Require the Cortex to author the Plan's intent before seeing the Core's composition on high-stakes classes — an independent first-pass that the Core then serves, rather than a checkpoint on a Core-authored plan. Preserve a human-originated frame the Core must answer to.
  2. Instrument Cortex decision-quality as an independent output (the model gestures at this via IPRE Evaluate) — periodically have the Cortex decide a sampled case without the dashboard and compare to the dashboard-assisted decision. A widening gap is the atrophy signal.
  3. Fund apprenticeship against the Major-2 supply math — it cannot both supply the growing-judgement function and the separated-Edge function from the same 2–5 people without a real external pipeline.

MINOR 1 — The determination selects Option 2 by retiring a premise the head-to-head's Option-2 findings never rested on

The finding. The head-to-head v0.2 (31 May) rated Option 2's hardest problems "architectural — not carpentry" (singleton Core, unmeetable substrate precondition, unstaffable separation-of-powers, renamed central coordinator) and recommended Option-1-led for that reason. The determination (1 June) overturns the recommendation by retiring build-time. But build-time was the basis for Option 1's selection, not the basis for Option 2's architectural Criticals. Retiring build-time correctly removes Option 1's distinctive advantage; it does not engage — or dissolve — Option 2's architectural problems, which are build-time-independent. The determination's §5 table lists Option 2's dropped caveat as only "its old 'long pre-value build' caveat," silently treating the architectural Criticals as if they were the build-time hedge. They were not.

Why it bites. The determination presents Option 2's selection as following from a clean retirement, when in fact the architectural Criticals (this pass's Criticals 1–3, Major 1) were never about build-time and survive the retirement untouched. The selection is sound as a destination-shape choice; it is over-claimed as a resolution of the red-team findings. Minor, because it is a framing/provenance defect rather than a structural one — but it matters, because it is the mechanism by which the most serious findings risk being treated as already-handled.

Severity: Minor. Evidence: strong — both documents are in the corpus and say what they say.

Concrete fix. In the determination, separate the two moves explicitly: (a) build-time is retired, which removes Option 1's advantage; (b) Option 2's architectural Criticals are independent of build-time and remain open, carried forward to this pass. Do not let (a) appear to discharge (b).

MINOR 2 — The "Five Planes" naming collision is still unresolved in the determination itself

The finding. The determination §3b uses "Five Planes" for the technical stack (Data→Model→Agent→Orchestration→Actuation); the framework and CLAUDE.md use it for org-layers (Strategy/Architecture/Governance/Operations/Intelligence). The canonical-terms doc (Resolution 2) flags this as a coordination-breaking ambiguity, and the model §11 lists resolving it as a founder decision — yet the governing determination uses the contested term without flagging the collision. A determination authored in ambiguous terms is a small instance of Critical 1.

Severity: Minor. Evidence: strong (the collision is documented). Concrete fix: apply Resolution 2 (reserve "Five Planes" for the technical stack; org layers are Core/Engines/Edge) to the determination before it governs further work.

3. What v0.1 findings are RETIRED by the new ground rule

The new ground rule (build difficulty/duration is not a valid objection) retires the following, in whole or in part:

  • v0.1 Finding 3 — "'Build the Core first, deploy nothing' is a long pre-value sunk-cost gate" — RETIRED in full. This was explicitly a build-duration / time-to-value objection ("no deployed value until phase 5," "pre-value gap," "the studio cannot spend its scarcest resource… on an unshippable substrate for an indeterminate period"). The determination §8 retires exactly this, and the prior pressure-test's own §3 fix (thin vertical slice, dependency-rule-not-calendar-gate) is now a sequencing preference, not a soundness finding. Not carried forward. (Note: the legitimate residue — that the Core delivers little until the spec is correct, and that attention pulled to ventures starves the platform — is not a build-time point and reappears as Major 2's commitment risk and Critical 1's correctness gap.)
  • v0.1 Finding 8 — the cold-start ("the Core can coordinate almost nothing meaningful early") — PARTIALLY RETIRED. The portion arguing this is a time-to-value / warm-up duration problem ("the value proposition is back-loaded to a maturity the institution has not reached," "a long warm-up the design does not cost") is retired as a build-time variant. What survives and is re-homed: sub-point 3 — choosing a venture's "honest signal" is a high-stakes, un-owned judgment that bakes in institutional Goodhart from day one. That is a specification-correctness defect, not a build-time defect, and it now sits under Critical 1 (a misspecified proxy is a misspecified spec) and Major 5.
  • The "long pre-value Core-first build [Major]" caveat that the determination §8 names directly — RETIRED, consistent with the determination.

No other v0.1 finding was a build-time finding. Critically, the v0.1 Criticals were not build-time objections and are therefore not retired (next section).

4. What survives the new ground rule

The new ground rule sharpens rather than softens the most serious findings, because "buildable" was never what they rested on.

  • Buildable ≠ correctly specified (this pass, Critical 1) — SURVIVES and is STRENGTHENED. The ground rule grants that the Core can be built; it says nothing about whether it can be built correctly. FTLAB's own canonical-terms audit shows the spec source is internally contradictory and unwritten. A built-but-misspecified Core is the more dangerous object precisely because build-time is no longer a delay protecting against it — it ships fast, confident, and wrong. This is the single finding the new ground rule most amplifies.
  • Guardian independence at 2–5 humans (this pass, Critical 2; v0.1 Finding 6; determination §7) — SURVIVES UNTOUCHED. It is capability-independent and build-independent. The determination concedes it. The staged charters confirm it is unsolved in the live operating layer (two powers in one founder-pair; third leg vacant). Dashboard oversight delivers bandwidth, not independence — and bandwidth without independence is the worse configuration. This is the inherited Critical, and it is still the one to solve first.
  • The Oligarch's Temptation / Michels (this pass, Critical 3; determination §6 named shadow) — SURVIVES and is UNANSWERED in the model. Concentration of infra + capital + charters + the rules-repo in 2–5 humans with no external terminus is a structural prediction indifferent to build-time and to founder benevolence. The determination flagged it as mandatory to answer; the model does not answer it.
  • The Core·Engines·Edge boundary instability / "renamed central coordinator" (this pass, Major 1; v0.1 Findings 4, 9) — SURVIVES. The staged intelligence-router.md shows the Core absorbing origination and the Intent-Setter reduced to ratification — a more loaded singleton, not a dissolved one. Build-time is irrelevant to this; it is a property of the chosen primitive.
  • Verifier independence unspecified (this pass, Major 3; v0.1 Finding 5) — SURVIVES. "Out-of-band" remains a named property without a specified mechanism, and the verifier checks against a founder-authored spec via a vacant human leg. Not a build-time matter.
  • Specification gaming, escalation suppression, epistemic colonisation, Cortex atrophy, fragility cascade (this pass, Criticals 1/3, Majors 3/4/5; determination §6 shadows) — SURVIVE as correctness/independence/concentration defects, not as build defects. The model answers them in a table by naming mechanisms; this pass finds the mechanisms presuppose the very capacities (a correct spec, an independent Guardian, an un-colonised Cortex, a multi-vendor Core) in dispute.

The genuinely robust elements from v0.1 still hold and should be kept: the two-jobs-of-hierarchy split; verification as a first-class separate function (now: specify its independence); "parallelise reads, single-thread writes" as the fixed default; "legitimacy not capability" as the permanent floor; measured-reliability promotion to autonomy (now: gate it on a watched verifier and a correct spec); and apprenticeship as the one human function that must grow (now: fund it against the Major-2 supply math).

5. Verdict

Option 2 is the right destination shape and is not sound as currently determined — because the determination treats "buildable" as if it settled "correctly specified, independently governed, and structurally accountable," and it settles none of them. The two-jobs split is correct, the Mothership mapping is genuine, and retiring build-time is the right call. But the new ground rule does not rescue Option 2; it relocates the danger from a slow build to a fast, confident, misspecified Core — and on that terrain Option 2 carries three Criticals that are properties of its chosen primitive, not unfinished work: (1) FTLAB cannot yet author a correct Strategy-as-Code spec from a corpus that defines its own primitives incompatibly and has not reconciled them, and a built Core compiles the contradiction and propagates it as authoritative; (2) dashboard oversight delivers bandwidth but not independence, and the staged charters show two of three powers in one founder-pair with the third leg vacant — better instrumentation makes a conflicted Guardian a faster rubber-stamp, not an independent one; (3) the architecture concentrates infrastructure, capital, charters, and the rules-repository in the same 2–5 humans with no external accountability terminus, which is the Iron-Law / FTX-WeWork structural pattern that benevolence does not cure. Beneath these, the Core·Engines·Edge boundary does not hold in practice (the Core absorbs origination; the Intent-Setter ratifies Core-framed candidates), two of the Five Conditions are unmeetable at FTLAB's size, and the named shadows are answered by mechanisms that presuppose the capacities in dispute. For Option 2 to hold as the determined destination, four things must be true before the Core runs autonomously on anything consequential: the canonical lexicon is frozen and the spec is adversarially red-teamed for correctness (not just built); an external Guardian-auditor with a real, exercised halt-right holds the third power (independence demonstrated, not titled); an external accountability terminus with teeth exists (the Oligarch's Temptation answered, not omitted); and origination is moved out of the Core so the humans direct rather than ratify. Until then, the soundest reading remains the head-to-head v0.2's, which the determination did not refute: build and red-team the Core on a separate track, run something legible and accountable the whole time, and migrate per task-class only once the spec is verified, the verifier is independent, and the Guardian is — demonstrably — not the founder grading himself.
