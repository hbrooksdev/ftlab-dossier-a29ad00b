Org Architecture v0.1 (superseded)

The first draft — over-relied on the AI-Born book; retained for the record.

33 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-organisational-architecture-v0.1.md
In brief

Superseded by v0.3. The original “Federated Cortex” draft, before the founder feedback to reground in the framework and treat the book as directional.

This document proposes a total organisational architecture for FTLAB itself: the complete set of functions, the roles that own them, the division of labour between the human team and the agentic workforce, the governance that holds it together, and the trajectory by which authority devolves from humans to agents over time. It is written to be coherent with FTLAB's conceptual framework, to build on the agent roster already drafted in the repository, and to be pressure-tested against the strongest external thinking on AI-era organisational design — principally Block's "From Hierarchy to Intelligence." It is a draft. It names its own open questions and the decisions that only the founders can make.

0. Provenance and how to read this

This proposal integrates five bodies of source material: (a) FTLAB's conceptual framework v1; (b) the founder responses to open questions; (c) the AI-Born book that is the institution's intellectual foundation; (d) the 28-agent roster already drafted in .claude/agents/; and (e) external evidence on AI-era organisational design (Block, McKinsey, Coase-and-after, and the empirical record of flat-organisation experiments).

Claims are classified where it matters: [Founder] = grounded in founder statements or the business plan; [Framework] = derived from FTLAB's own conceptual framework or AI-Born; [External] = from outside evidence; [Synthesis] = my proposal, not yet anyone's stated position; [Speculative] = forward-looking, low evidence. The institution's own review guide notes that several operating-model elements (temporal rhythms, success metrics, the "what we will not do" list) are invented and not yet founder-validated; this document treats those as raw material, not doctrine.

One reading instruction: the visible artifact at the end of this document does look like a chart of "Heads of major functions with teams underneath" — which is what was asked for. The argument in Parts 2–4 is that what those heads are, and what the lines between them mean, is categorically different from a management hierarchy. Read the model before reading the chart, or the chart will be misread as the very thing it is designed to replace.

1. Executive summary

FTLAB's organisational form must itself be the first proof of FTLAB's thesis. An institution that researches how AI transforms organisations, while organising itself the way organisations were built in 1950, is incoherent — and coherence is a load-bearing value here, not a slogan.

The design problem is sharper than "draw an org chart of AI agents." It is the institution's own stated open question: how do you scale governance across a growing portfolio without rebuilding the hierarchical bureaucracy the AI-born thesis exists to transcend? The naive answers both fail. Rebuilding a management pyramid with agents in the boxes reproduces the bottleneck the thesis rejects. Going fully flat — the Block "intelligence in the system, people on the edge" model — is an unproven bet at scale and discards the legible accountability that every flat-organisation experiment in the historical record eventually had to reinstate.

The resolution rests on one distinction. Hierarchy historically did two separable jobs: it routed information, and it located accountability and judgment. Autonomous systems can now do the first — coordination collapses into a continuously-maintained world model, FTLAB's institutional memory and Knowledge Flywheel. They cannot do the second, and the reason is not capability but legitimacy: an agent cannot be held to account, sued, or trusted by a sovereign partner. AI-Born states this plainly — if AI matches human judgment, "the capability argument for those roles weakens; the governance argument does not."

So FTLAB should automate the routing into a system and keep a deliberately small, legible, accountable human Cortex at the judgment, accountability, and relationship boundaries. The organisation is built from one repeating unit — a Domain: a major function owned by a VP-Agent (the "Head"), staffed by a three-tier agent team, governed (not managed) by an assigned human Triumvirate (Architect, Intent-Setter, Guardian), and bounded by a machine-readable Charter. The whole institution is a federation of Domains under a thin human Cortex and a shared world model — what this document calls the Federated Cortex. It is neither a pyramid nor a flat collective. The only hierarchy that remains is the agent-orchestration topology inside each Domain — a coordination necessity (centralised agent control degrades past 500–1,000 agents), not a structure of human power.

This model answers the scale question by fractal recursion: every venture, and eventually every sub-function, is its own Domain with its own cortex, coordinated through the world model and a single shared governance language (the Triumvirate diagnostic), not through a chain of managers. It accommodates FTLAB's founder-sanctioned end-state — evolving "into something more like an autonomous methodology or framework" — as a continuous devolution, not a cliff, with a permanent human accountability floor that never fully automates.

The rest of this document specifies the model, instantiates FTLAB's ~10 Domains by elevating the existing agent roster and filling its genuine gaps, maps the human Cortex onto the founders and near-term hires, lays out the devolution roadmap across FTLAB's temporal horizons, specifies the governance mechanics, and ends with the contradictions it resolves and the decisions it leaves to the founders.

2. The question, sharpened

The brief contained three positions in tension, and the value of this exercise is in resolving the tension rather than choosing a side.

Position A — the founder instinct. A structure of high-altitude heads — "Head of Agentic Systems, with engineers underneath" — each owning a huge function with a team beneath them, fully empowered, with key responsibility and agency devolved to them. Mapped to human org roles. (I read the brief's "genetic systems" as "agentic systems"; the example is otherwise unambiguous and I have designed to it. If that reading is wrong, one Domain below changes name, nothing else.)

Position B — Block's challenge. "From Hierarchy to Intelligence" argues the opposite: do not recreate heads-with-teams. Hierarchy existed only to route information under the span-of-control limit; a machine-maintained world model now does that routing; therefore eliminate permanent middle management, put "the intelligence in the system" and "the people on the edge," and keep only three roles — individual contributors, directly-responsible-individuals, and player-coaches.

Position C — FTLAB's own framework. Already anti-hierarchical, and more subtle than either. Machine Core + Human Cortex is "not hierarchical but symbiotic." The master diagram insists the worldview "radiates, it does not descend… a learning architecture, not a command-and-control hierarchy." And Part V concedes the institution does not yet know how to scale governance "without creating the hierarchical bureaucracy that the AI-born thesis seeks to transcend."

These do not reconcile by compromise ("a bit of hierarchy, a bit of flatness"). They reconcile by making a distinction none of them quite states on its own.

3. The central insight: hierarchy did two jobs

For a century, the org chart fused two functions that we can now separate.

Job 1 — information routing and coordination. Aggregating what is happening upward, distributing decisions downward, synchronising effort across units. This is the job Coase identified the firm as existing to do cheaply, and the job the span-of-control limit (one human attends to 3–8 others) forced into layers. Block's argument is exactly and only about this job: a continuously-updated world model "can maintain a model of an entire business and use it to coordinate work in ways that previously required humans." [External/Framework]

Job 2 — accountability and judgment. Locating who is answerable when something goes wrong; exercising taste, moral reasoning, and discernment in novel, ambiguous, high-stakes, or relational situations; holding the line on values as the system adapts. This is the job that survives.

The decisive evidence that these separate cleanly — and that only Job 1 automates — comes from three directions that rarely agree and here all do:

  • FTLAB's own book. The case for keeping human roles "does not rest primarily on current capability gaps. It rests on structural necessity: accountability, democratic legitimacy, and the irreducibility of moral responsibility require human agents regardless of AI capability… legitimacy cannot be delegated." The hard stop on full autonomy is therefore permanent and capability-independent. [Framework]
  • Block, read precisely. Even the most aggressive flat-org advocate fences off "ethical decisions, novel situations, high-stakes moments where the cost of being wrong is existential" and reserves "intuition, opinionated direction, cultural context" for humans. Block did not abolish judgment-hierarchy; it abolished routing-hierarchy and renamed the judgment roles (DRI, player-coach). [External]
  • The outcomes record. Every deliberate de-hierarchy experiment that has run long enough to evaluate — Zappos/Holacracy, GitHub, Medium, Buffer, Valve at scale, the "Spotify model" — reverted or hybridised, and the mechanism was always the same: removing legible authority does not remove authority, it replaces it with illegible authority (loudest voice, longest tenure, or — in an agent org — whoever quietly writes the agents' instructions). Klarna ran the cleanest natural experiment in the automate-Job-2 direction, replaced ~700 human agents with AI, and reversed: "we went too far… lower quality… not sustainable." The functions it had to bring humans back for — empathy, trust, complex judgment — are precisely Job 2. [External]

A crucial asymmetry follows, and FTLAB should internalise it as a discipline: the exciting pro-flat claims (Block, the one-person unicorn, Agentforce) are announcements and predictions; the cautionary claims (Zappos, GitHub, Klarna) are outcomes. Weight outcomes over announcements. Design into that tension, not around it.

The resolution. Automate Job 1 into a system; keep Job 2 in a small, legible, accountable human Cortex. The "heads of function with teams" the brief asked for are real — but each head is a VP-Agent doing coordinated execution (Job 1 territory), and the humans relate to it as governors of Job 2, not as managers in a routing chain. The hierarchy the brief pictured (humans over humans, routing work down) is the part that dissolves. The hierarchy that remains is the agent orchestration tree inside each function — which FTLAB's own book shows is a coordination necessity, not a power structure, and which must itself federate (not stack) past ~200–300 agents or it bottlenecks.

4. The model: the Federated Cortex

4.1 The repeating unit — the Domain

The institution is built from one unit, repeated and federated. A Domain is a major function rendered as four coupled things:

  1. A Head (a VP-Agent). An orchestrator-level master agent that owns the function end to end — receives objectives, decomposes them, delegates to its team, monitors, reallocates, reports. In AI-Born's terms, "analogous to a Vice President but operating at machine speed with perfect information." This is the "head of a huge function" the brief wanted. It is not a tool and not a human substitute; it is "a new category of institutional participant with specific capabilities, defined authority, explicit constraints, and a governance relationship with the humans who set its intent." [Framework]
  1. A team (a three-tier agent population). Beneath the Head: mid-tier session managers owning projects/workflows, and a larger base of specialist workers doing granular execution. This is the "engineers underneath them" of the brief — rendered as an agent swarm with model-tiering for cost and capability (high-capability models at the Head, efficient models at the base). The team is sized by the function's leverage ratio, which is sector- and risk-dependent, not a fixed number (see §10 on the ratio). [Framework]
  1. A governance triple (the Triumvirate, assigned). Three governance functions — Architect (designs how the Domain works), Intent-Setter (defines what it is for and tunes its priorities), Guardian (ensures it stays within values and can halt it) — held by the human Cortex, or progressively by machine capability under verification. These are functions, not job titles, and "the absence of any one creates a specific governance failure: no Intent-Setter produces purposeless agents; no Guardian produces unmonitored agents; no Architect produces agents that cannot be maintained, explained, or improved." [Framework]
  1. A Charter (the bounded authority envelope). A machine-readable specification — simultaneously job description, performance contract, and governance document — defining the Head's identity, objectives, and authority in three tiers: autonomous (act without approval), dual-control (prepare, but a human authorises before execution), and forbidden (hard constraints, inviolable). Authority is set on two axes — decision type × consequence magnitude — and bounded by Values (non-negotiable, Guardian-enforced) versus Preferences (tunable by the Intent-Setter) versus Structural Preferences (important enough to need elevated care, short of values). [Framework]

The Domain is the answer to "what is a head of function, concretely?" It is a VP-Agent + its swarm + an assigned Triumvirate + a Charter. Everything in FTLAB is built from this unit, including FTLAB itself and including every venture.

4.2 What a "Head of Function" is — and what the lines mean

This reframing is the whole point, so it is worth stating flatly what changes relative to the conventional reading of the brief:

Conventional orgThe Federated Cortex
Head = a senior human who manages peopleHead = a VP-Agent that orchestrates an agent swarm
Line down = command/reporting (routes work)Line down = orchestration topology (a coordination tree the world model, not a manager, keeps coherent)
Line up = "reports to my boss"Line up = governance (a human Triumvirate sets the charter, can halt, redesigns) — not a boss assigning tasks
Coordination across heads = meetings, alignment, the manager chainCoordination across Domains = the shared world model / institutional memory + the Knowledge Flywheel
Authority = positional, tenuredAuthority = chartered, evidence-earned, revocable, and altitude-gated
Growth = add headcount and layersGrowth = add compute and federate (new Domains/cortexes), not layers

The humans are not at the top of this. They are, in Block's phrase, "on the edge" — but more precisely, they are the Cortex: the thin layer that sets intent, holds values, exercises judgment on what the system escalates, and carries accountability. The world model is in the middle, doing what middle management used to do. The agent Domains do the work.

4.3 The human Cortex — thin, legible, accountable

The human team is small by design and stays small — FTLAB's founders commit that "FTLAB itself will always maintain a ratio of a small human team to a large agentic workforce." This is not only a thesis statement; it is FTLAB's strongest evidentiary position: flat, legible coordination has always worked in the <150–300-human regime and always failed above it, so a 5–30-human core is precisely where the structure is supported by the record — whereas Block's bet is that AI breaks that regularity at 6,000 people, which is unproven. FTLAB should claim the regime that is supported, not the bet that is not. [Synthesis/External]

The Cortex holds the three Triumvirate functions across the Domains. It does not hold them uniformly, and the division is a real design choice (Part 6). Three properties are non-negotiable:

  • Legibility. For every Domain, it must be answerable in one sentence who performs Architect, Intent-Setter, and Guardian. This is the single question FTLAB already applies to portfolio ventures; turned inward, it becomes the org's accountability map. The failure mode of flatness — invisible power — is defeated by making the governance triple explicit for every function.
  • Guardian independence. The Guardian function must be structurally independent of profit-and-loss — "no P&L, not reporting to whoever owns revenue," with un-overridable authority to halt and escalate. Financial pressure and values judgment are incompatible over time. This has an immediate consequence for FTLAB: Shayan currently holds Compliance/Guardian-type responsibilities and operating responsibilities; the model says those should not sit in the same person indefinitely. [Framework]
  • Designed tension. Architect (wants capability), Intent-Setter (wants purpose), Guardian (wants safety) must be in productive tension; no one dominates. The cortex "succeeds or fails as a unit," and an odd-numbered arrangement gives built-in arbitration (any two can check the third).

4.4 The coordination substrate — what replaces routing

The model only works if something genuinely does Job 1. That something is a world model — for FTLAB, this is its institutional memory plus the Knowledge Flywheel plus the IPRE/PRAL coordination spine, made continuous and machine-readable.

This is the part the external evidence is most emphatic about, and the part organisations most often skip: the world model is a prerequisite, not a payoff. Block's model works only because everything the company does — "decisions, discussions, code, designs, plans, problems, progress" — becomes a machine-readable artifact. No artifact substrate, no world model; and an org that removes its routing layer without first building the substrate has simply deleted its coordination and kept the chaos. [External]

Concretely, FTLAB's coordination substrate is:

  • The artifact discipline + institutional memory (the Data plane, Plane 1, made total): every research finding, decision, venture signal, consulting insight, and agent action is captured as structured, queryable artifact. This is the literal world model.
  • The Knowledge Flywheel as the circulatory system: research → ventures → consulting → licensing → research, with each Domain reading from and writing to the shared memory rather than reporting up a chain.
  • IPRE (Intent → Policy → Routing → Execution) as the authority spine — how intent flows from Cortex to agents — and PRAL (Perception → Reasoning → Action → Learning) as the learning spine — how experience flows back, at individual, orchestration, and institutional levels.

When a Head needs to coordinate with another Domain, it does so through the shared model — not by escalating to a common manager. Coordination becomes a read/write against shared state, which is exactly why it can scale without layers.

4.5 Why not flat, why not pyramid, why not pure-Block

To be explicit about what is rejected and why:

  • Not a management pyramid. It rebuilds the routing bottleneck the thesis rejects, raises Cognitive Overhead, and contradicts FTLAB's own "radiates, does not descend." Rejected. [Framework]
  • Not a flat collective (which is what the current 28-agent draft actually is — peers with no accountable heads). Flatness produces invisible hierarchy and a 7-way veto with no single owner; it fails the legibility test. Rejected. [External/Synthesis]
  • Not pure-Block. FTLAB is not Block. It is two orders of magnitude smaller in humans, it must govern a portfolio (not one company), and it deals with sovereign partners for whom a named, accountable human counterpart is the relationship. Block's elimination of named function-heads suits a 6,000-person single company betting it can hold flatness at scale; FTLAB's advantage is the opposite — staying small enough that legible Domains are cheap and the flat-zone evidence is on its side. Adopt Block's world-model mechanism and its time-boxed DRI accountability primitive; reject its claim that named function-ownership disappears. [Synthesis]

The Federated Cortex keeps the one thing all the evidence says you must keep — legible human accountability at the judgment boundary — while automating the one thing all the evidence says you now can — routing — and federating rather than stacking as it grows.

5. FTLAB's Domains — the concrete organisation

What follows instantiates the model for FTLAB. It is built by elevating the 28 drafted agents into ~10 high-altitude Domains (each Head owning a function, with the former agents becoming its team or sub-orchestrators) and by adding the functions the audit found missing for an institution that builds and operates ventures rather than only researching them.

A note on the existing draft: it is currently a flat collective of 28 equal peers in six "planes," with no real heads, a two-headed coordinator (chief-of-staff + agent-orchestrator), a seven-way publication veto, registry drift (phantom "Deal Scout" and "Platform Architect" agents referenced but never created), and four missing functions (venture builder/operator, capital/finance, platform/product engineering, go-to-market). The "Platform Architect" that several agents report to but which does not exist is a tell: a build/platform head was intended and dropped. The elevation below reinstates it and fixes the drift as step zero.

5.2 Selected Domains in detail

Four Domains carry most of the model's novelty and deserve detail; the rest follow the same pattern.

Domain 2 — Knowledge & World Model (the keystone). This is the most important and most under-appreciated Domain, because it is the coordination substrate that makes the whole model work. Its mandate is to make FTLAB's institutional memory total, machine-readable, and continuously queried — so that every other Domain coordinates by reading and writing shared state rather than by reporting up a chain. It owns the artifact discipline (nothing happens that is not captured), the framework architecture, and the cross-Domain knowledge graph. If FTLAB builds only one thing first, it is this — the external evidence is unanimous that removing routing without first building the world model is the dominant failure mode. Its human governance is Architect-led and is among the earliest to devolve to an agent Architect, because the work is high-volume, high-artifact-density, and low-stakes per action.

Domain 4 — Quality, Rigour & Guardianship (the consolidated gate). Today seven agents independently hold blocking publication vetoes. That is not governance; it is diffusion. This Domain consolidates rigour into one accountable function and houses the institutional Guardian. It must be structurally independent (no P&L), with authority to halt any Domain that drifts from its values, and it operates by statistical oversight, not total inspection: high-stakes outputs reviewed fully, medium sampled, low handled by peer-agent consensus with periodic audit; meta-evaluators watch the evaluators; anomaly dashboards rather than per-decision review. It enforces the five escalation thresholds (§9). This is the last function to devolve, because it is the human accountability floor.

Domain 3 — Venture Architecture & Building (the gap that matters most). The current roster designs ventures on paper and stops; there is no agent that builds or runs one, despite "FTLAB builds and operates AI-born ventures" appearing throughout. This Domain owns the full pipeline (Research Thesis → Prototype → Named → Growth) and, critically, the incubation of each venture as its own nested Domain-lattice (see Part 7). It is where FTLAB's two tiers meet: the studio Domain that spins out venture cortexes.

Domain 10 — People, Culture & Apprenticeship (the function the brief's framing omits). The external evidence surfaces a risk neither the founder instinct nor Block addresses: an org of "senior humans on the edge + agents, no juniors" has no mechanism to produce future senior humans — and the entry-level pipeline is collapsing industry-wide. For a studio whose job is to seed many ventures with capable human cortexes, this is existential, not cosmetic. This Domain therefore explicitly owns an apprenticeship pipeline ("decision shadows": humans who observe Triumvirate decisions to learn the protocols and become the next cortex members and the next ventures' founders). It also owns the four documented psychological failure modes of cortex roles — isolation, the "flattening effect" (humans converging on algorithmic taste), context-switching overload, and identity loss — as architectural requirements (rotation, escalation caps, mandatory hands-on work), because the model "succeeds only if humans thrive within it." This Domain never fully devolves: it is the part of the institution that makes humans.

The remaining Domains (1, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9) follow the same four-part structure (Head + team + Triumvirate + Charter) and are summarised in the table; each can be specified to the same depth when the founders confirm the overall shape.

6. The human Cortex — who holds what

The model needs the human "5" to be defined as Triumvirate functions across Domains, not as a management ladder. Mapping onto the actual founders and the business plan's named near-term hires:

  • Shayan Kargarian — Managing Partner — lead Intent-Setter. Sets institutional intent, direction, priorities, and charters across Domains; brings structured-finance and programme-governance depth (natural fit for Capital & Operations and Venture governance). The framework already positions him as "driving much of the thinking and direction." [Founder]
  • Mehran Granfar — General Partner — Intent-Setter (thesis) + initial Architect. Owns the thesis and the AI-Born intellectual foundation (the source of Values), and the initial system/architecture design across Research, Knowledge, and Platform. [Founder]
  • The Guardian (key early hire or independent director). The model's hard requirement that the Guardian be P&L-independent means it should not remain bundled with Shayan's operating role. This is the most important near-term human decision (Part 11). Candidate: an independent director with AI-governance standing, or a dedicated Chief Guardian.
  • The Architect (CTO / "Principal Solutions Architect" — the business plan's Q2 hire). Designs the Platform & Agentic Systems Domain and the Five-Planes stack; the human counterpart most able to devolve to an agent Architect over time.
  • Operations/Capital lead (the business plan's "Operations Lead"). Anchors Capital & Operations until that Domain's VP-Agent matures.

That is a 4–5 human Cortex — consistent with the business plan's Year-1 "2–4 people" and Year-2 "4–10," but reframed: these people are not a management chain, they are the holders of Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian across the Domain federation. The business plan's traditional titles (Co-CEO, VP Engineering, Venture Analyst) are human-role equivalents useful for external/DIFC legibility; internally, the operative identities are Triumvirate functions.

Concentration risk is the dominant risk of a small Cortex — "losing one Intent-Setter or Architect can be more consequential than losing one of forty employees." Mitigations are design requirements, not afterthoughts: documented intent and decisions (the world model doubles as succession insurance), redundancy in governance roles (more than one human able to hold each function), decision-shadows as a succession pipeline (Domain 10), and the explicit acknowledgement that the founders intend, as the portfolio grows, for "each founder to develop their own governance apparatus" — which in this model means cloning governance capacity via agents and apprentices, not hiring management.

7. The two tiers and fractal recursion — the answer to "governance at scale"

FTLAB is not one organisation; it is a studio plus a growing portfolio of ventures, and the founders are explicit that venture teams are not FTLAB headcount — each venture has "its own small human core." This is the structural fact that lets FTLAB answer its own open question.

The Federated Cortex recurses. The same unit — Domain (Head + team + Triumvirate + Charter) — is the building block at three scales:

  1. The studio (FTLAB itself): the ~10 Domains of Part 5 under the founder Cortex.
  2. Each venture: spun out by Domain 3 as its own Domain-lattice with its own small human cortex (Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian for that venture) and its own agent population — sized by that sector's leverage ratio (regulated finance ≈ tighter human oversight; high-autonomy domains ≈ larger swarms).
  3. Inside a large Domain or venture: sub-functions become nested Domains as they grow, federating rather than stacking.

This is the Mothership pattern: a shared Platform Core (Domains 2 and 9 — shared data, models, governance-as-service) supports autonomous ventures (3–10 humans + agents, separate P&L, own charters) under a shared strategic Cortex. Real-world existence proof that this scales: Haier's Rendanheyi — ~4,700 microenterprises of 10–15 people, ~$38B revenue — is a federation of small cells, not a pyramid.

Why this transcends hierarchy at scale. Governance scales not by adding management layers above the ventures, but by:

  • Replicating the pattern, not the chain: every unit is self-similar, governed by the same Triumvirate triple and Charter grammar.
  • Coordinating through the world model, not through managers: FTLAB's studio reads venture state from shared memory; it does not route venture work.
  • Governing by altitude of change (§9): operational decisions are agent-autonomous and fast; structural decisions need more evidence; foundational ones (thesis, values) are human-reserved and slow. This "appropriate friction" is the governor that prevents both rigidity and runaway.
  • Auditing with one question, fractally: "Who holds Architect / Intent-Setter / Guardian here, are they resourced, and are they in tension?" — asked of every Domain and every venture.

This is the concrete, founder-grounded answer to "how does governance scale across a portfolio without hierarchical bureaucracy": federate self-similar cortexes coordinated by a shared world model and one governance grammar, governed by altitude-of-change friction — not by a management chain. [Synthesis, grounded in Framework]

8. The devolution roadmap — evolving toward a fully autonomous institution

The brief's deepest requirement is that this be a trajectory, not a static chart: FTLAB "constantly evolving into an AI-born fully autonomous company," with key responsibility and agency progressively devolved to agents. The framework sanctions this end-state explicitly — FTLAB may "evolve beyond being an institution into something more like an autonomous methodology or framework." The roadmap must therefore be continuous, and it must be honest about its one permanent limit.

8.1 The mechanism of devolution

Devolution is not a vibe; it is three concrete, simultaneous movements:

  • Per agent: widen the Charter — move decisions from dual-control toward autonomous, never touching forbidden/Values. Expansion is "triggered by evidence, not time" — verified performance within a progressively wider authority envelope. The warning: do not mistake the absence of observed problems for trustworthiness — that is abandonment, not trust.
  • Per human role: the personal shift from operator → delegator → intender — humans stop doing, then stop directing, and end up only setting intent and judging.
  • Per organisation: the staged path Augmentation → Delegation → Architectural Redesign.

8.2 The sequence — what devolves when

The order is set by two principles: devolve where artifact density is highest and stakes-per-action lowest first; devolve trust-bearing and accountability functions last. The A.G.E.N.T. defensibility stack gives the ordering by difficulty — Architecture and Governance build (and devolve) in months; Network and Trust take years and "no technology stack can replicate" them.

8.3 The permanent floor — what "fully autonomous" does and does not mean

This is the honest limit, and stating it clearly is itself coherent with FTLAB's values. "Fully autonomous" can be true of doing and of coordination. It cannot be true of accountability — not because agents will not be capable, but because accountability requires a nameable, questionable, liable human, and "legitimacy cannot be delegated." So the permanent human floor is:

  • Legitimacy and liability — a human answerable to regulators, sovereigns, courts, and stakeholders.
  • Novel, ethical, existential calls — decisions outside any charter's trained domain or where the cost of being wrong is irreversible.
  • Values evolution — deciding what the institution values as it learns; agents implement values, humans author them.
  • Trust-bearing relationships — sovereign engagement and the deepest partnerships, where the relationship is a human one.

The framing horizon for the capability-based parts of this floor is the book's stated 10–15 year window; the legitimacy-based parts are not time-limited. FTLAB's end-state is therefore best described not as "no humans" but as "the smallest possible human cortex holding the irreducible accountability floor, governing a near-fully-autonomous agent institution" — which is exactly the founder-sanctioned "autonomous methodology with human stewardship at its core," made precise.

8.4 The instruments that tell you it is working

Three diagnostics, read continuously, tell FTLAB whether devolution is succeeding or whether it is quietly drifting back toward a conventional structure:

  • Cognitive Overhead — the share of the human cortex's capacity spent on operational management versus judgment and creation. Rising overhead is the early signal that "the machine core is not adequately handling its responsibilities and the enterprise is drifting toward a conventional structure." This is the devolution KPI. Target trend: down.
  • Alignment Debt — the accumulated gap between stated values and actual agent behaviour. The goal is not zero (impossible) but tracked, acknowledged, deliberately managed — visible debt is institutional maturity; hidden debt is decay.
  • Iteration Half-Life — the time for a full learn cycle. Lengthening is an early warning of calcification.

9. Governance and coordination mechanics

The model's governance is not a committee calendar; it is encoded into the Charters and the coordination substrate. The load-bearing mechanics:

  • The Charter grammar. Every Head and sub-agent has a machine-readable charter with autonomousAuthority, escalationTriggers (dual-control), and hardConstraints (forbidden), plus reward weights and an owning Triumvirate. Changing strategy = updating charters, not reorganising — "organisational pivots in hours, not months."
  • The five escalation thresholds (any one triggers a human): irreversibility (not reversible within ~24h without disproportionate cost), novelty (outside trained domain / no precedent), stakeholder impact (exceeds a defined threshold of revenue/customers/affected third parties), values conflict (a values trade-off the agent cannot resolve), and regulatory ambiguity (legally uncertain regardless of the agent's confidence).
  • The four Guardian responses, each logged: approve / approve-with-modifications / defer / veto (→ post-mortem → charter revision). This is the audit trail that makes accountability legible.
  • Statistical oversight, not total inspection. Stratified by risk; meta-evaluators; calibration checks (does claimed confidence match actual accuracy?); anomaly dashboards; rapid detect→fix→validate cycles. Cap escalations per human Guardian (~50/day) to prevent oversight degrading into theatre; federate with Local Guardians per venture and a Chief Guardian setting principles.
  • Governance by altitude of change. Operational changes: fast, cheap, agent-autonomous. Structural changes: more evidence, slower. Foundational changes (thesis, values, worldview): most evidence, human-reserved. This single rule decides what an agent may do unilaterally versus what escalates.
  • Consultation as an architected organ. Drawn from FTLAB's intellectual heritage and secularised: structured, disciplined consultation that surfaces stakeholder knowledge (not seeks approval, not dilutes vision) — built into the architecture via stakeholder forums and the odd-numbered Triumvirate's built-in arbitration, not bolted on.
  • The artifact-substrate prerequisite. None of the above works without the world model (Domain 2). Sequencing rule: build the artifact/observability substrate before widening any charter.

10. Assumptions challenged and contradictions resolved

The brief asked to have its assumptions challenged. Here, explicitly, is what this proposal overturns or corrects — including in FTLAB's own corpus.

Challenged — "heads of function with teams underneath" (the founder instinct). Kept in form, overturned in substance: the heads are VP-Agents, the humans are governors not managers, and the only real hierarchy is the agent-orchestration tree inside each Domain. The picture survives; its meaning is inverted.

Challenged — "go flat / adopt Block wholesale." Rejected for FTLAB: Block's no-named-heads model is an unproven scale bet, discards the legible accountability the outcomes-record says you must keep, and ignores that FTLAB's advantage is staying in the small-human regime where flatness actually works. Adopt Block's world-model mechanism and DRI primitive; reject its abolition of function-ownership.

Challenged — the talent factory. Neither the founder instinct nor Block addresses how an org of seniors-plus-agents produces its next seniors. Added as a first-class Domain (10), because a studio that seeds ventures with human cortexes cannot outsource the manufacture of those humans.

Contradiction resolved — the ratio. The corpus says three different things: 5:100 (business plan, framework, whitepapers), 1:500 / 5:2,500 (the website's CLAUDE.md), and 1:100–1:10,000, sector-dependent (the book). Recommendation: abandon the fixed-number slogan and treat the leverage ratio as a per-Domain, per-sector design parameter — tighter human oversight for regulated/trust-bearing functions (≈1:100), larger swarms for high-autonomy functions (≈1:1,000+) — while holding the human count in the 5–30 range. The "5:100" is useful externally as an evocative figure; internally it should not be load-bearing. [Synthesis]

Contradiction resolved — two "Five Planes." The framework defines them as a technical stack (Data/Model/Agent/Orchestration/Actuation); the CLAUDE.md defines them as org layers (Strategy/Architecture/Governance/Operations/Intelligence); the agent roster uses a third six-plane taxonomy. Recommendation: keep "Five Planes" for the technical stack only (it is the canonical, founder-grounded meaning) and rename the organisational grouping to avoid the collision — "Domains" (this document) replaces the org-planes entirely. [Synthesis]

Contradiction resolved — two "New Triumvirates" and two "VP-Agents." The framework's Triumvirate is Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian (governance roles); the CLAUDE.md's is Human Judgment/Machine Intelligence/Institutional Design (capability sources). The framework's VP-Agent is Values & Preferences (a governance-encoding model); the CLAUDE.md's is Virtual Professional (an agent-as-staff metaphor). Recommendation: canonicalise the framework definitions (Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian; Values & Preferences) and treat "Virtual Professional" as the name for the Head agent and "Values & Preferences" as how it is bounded — they are complementary, not competing, once named carefully. [Synthesis]

Contradiction resolved — leadership titles. Business plan: Co-CEO/Co-CEO. Founder responses: Managing Partner / General Partner. Recommendation: adopt Managing Partner / General Partner internally — "CEO" connotes the command hierarchy the model rejects; "Partner + Triumvirate function" fits. Keep CEO-equivalents only where external/DIFC legibility requires. [Synthesis, founder decision]

Corrected — registry hygiene (step zero). Before any elevation, reconcile the phantom agents ("Deal Scout," "Platform Architect") and the dangling reports-to lines. A redesign inherited on top of broken references propagates them.

11. Open decisions for the founders

These are genuine forks the documents do not settle and that this proposal should not pre-empt:

  1. Where does the Guardian sit? The model requires the institutional Guardian to be P&L-independent. Today Shayan holds both Guardian-type and operating responsibilities. Options: (a) hire/appoint an independent Chief Guardian early; (b) assign Guardian to Mehran while Shayan holds Intent-Setter + Operations; (c) use an independent director. This is the most consequential near-term human decision.
  1. How aggressive is the devolution timeline? The roadmap (Part 8) is paced to FTLAB's four horizons but the speed of moving Domains from dual-control to autonomous is a risk appetite the founders must set — especially for Venture-building and Capital.
  1. The ratio doctrine. Confirm the recommendation to retire the fixed "5:100" in favour of a per-Domain leverage parameter, and resolve which external figure (if any) FTLAB publishes.
  1. Scope of "total organisation." This document designs the studio and shows the fractal venture pattern. If the founders want the canonical venture-org template specified to the same depth (a reusable Domain-lattice every venture inherits), that is a natural next deliverable.
  1. Naming. "The Federated Cortex," "Domain," and the reframed "Head" are proposals. If they earn their place, they extend the existing vocabulary (Machine Core + Human Cortex, Triumvirate, VP-Agent); if not, they are cheap to rename.
  1. Build order. The proposal's strong recommendation is to build Domain 2 (Knowledge & World Model) first — the artifact substrate — before widening any charter. Confirm this sequencing.

12. What to do next

If this shape is right, the immediate, concrete follow-ons are:

  • Specify each Domain's Charter (the YAML grammar: autonomous/dual-control/forbidden, reward weights, owning Triumvirate) — turning the ten Domains into deployable agent definitions, and elevating/retiring the 28 existing agents into them.
  • Stand up the world model (Domain 2): the artifact-capture discipline and institutional-memory substrate, as the precondition for everything else.
  • Write the canonical venture-org template (decision 4) if the founders want the fractal pattern made reusable.
  • Fix the registry (step zero) and reconcile the corpus contradictions (Part 10) into the next version of the conceptual framework, so the org architecture and the framework stay coherent.

This document is v0.1 and deliberately names its uncertainties. It is offered in the institution's own spirit: a framework held with conviction but not rigidity, written to be tested in action and revised by what reality teaches.
