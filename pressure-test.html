Option 1 · red-team (v0.3)

Pressure-Test — Option 1 (re-run)

Hostile re-test of the v0.4 re-run — it caught the count (five should be ~3) and a regression (capital fused with the Guardian), both now fixed in v0.4.

Option 136 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-org-architecture-pressure-test-v0.3.md
In brief

A red-team of the re-run. Verdict: sound as the face, but it reported the wrong number — the work yields ~3 Engines, and naming Coordination & Memory and Quality & Guardianship as departments repeats the seven’s second over-count. It also flagged that folding Capital into the Guardian’s Edge worsens the inherited Critical.

Key takeaways
  • Critical: FIVE is ~3 + the Core + the Edge relabelled — the work yields three value-creating Engines (corrected in v0.4).
  • Critical: the keystone survives — Coordination & Memory as a human-supervised department, not an engineered Core (Option 1’s honest limit).
  • Critical: capital must not fuse with the Guardian (Ch 9) — corrected; the halt-right is externalised first.
  • Confirms Option 1 is not the destination: it is the legible face, ~3 Engines wearing department labels.

This is a hostile re-test, commissioned to find where the re-run breaks — not to confirm it. v0.4 is an improvement on v0.3 (it un-splits Research+Venture and matches the business plan's headcount far better), and that is graded honestly. But the re-run makes one move it must be made to answer for: having correctly identified — via its own cited source, the role-derivation — that the seven-Domain count over-constructed in two ways, it fixes the first error and repeats the second. The test's central charge is that v0.4 derives THREE from the work and then re-inflates to FIVE by counting the Core and the Edge as Heads, which is the precise over-count the role-derivation names. Everything else (the keystone, the Guardian Critical, the singleton charter, blast radius) follows from, or compounds, that move. Findings are graded Critical / Major / Minor, each with the finding, why it bites, severity, and a concrete fix. §3 retires/changes the v0.2 findings the re-run genuinely answers; §4 states what survives; §5 is the verdict.

1. What is being tested

The subject. Option 1 as re-derived in ftlab-organisational-architecture-v0.4.md: the seven v0.3 Domains collapsed to five named AI Heads — Venture Engine; Platform & Agentic Systems; Knowledge & Ecosystem; Coordination & Memory; Quality & Guardianship — each an agent under a three-tier charter (alone / escalate / never), governed by a 2–3 person human Cortex (the Edge) running Architect / Intent-Setter / Guardian, with Capital & Operations held by the founder Edge until it earns a sixth seat. The collapse is justified by two founder intentions ("the ventures are the research," Q1/Q13; "advisory is always tied to venture-building," Q7) and one architectural fact (single-owner charters). Coordination runs through a named Coordination & Memory Head whose "integrator" routes work and sequences hand-offs; v0.4 is explicit that this is "a department, which is the honest limit of Option 1" (§3-Head-4), and that, mounted on a real Core, the model reduces to ~3 Engines (§5).

The frame. Three constraints govern this test:

  1. Option 1 has no Core to build, so build-time is not in play at all. The determination's ground rule (§8) — "as long as the Core can be built, it is valid" — does not rescue or wound Option 1 directly, because Option 1 builds no Core. The relevant critique, per the brief, is therefore what the re-run gives up by not building one, and whether the no-Core stance is honestly costed.
  1. The determination has already fixed the work-grounded count at ~3 (...determination... §10, locked: "the count is ~3 Engines, not seven… This supersedes any 'seven faces / seven Domains' language"), and the role-derivation (...role-derivation... §4) names the two over-construction errors precisely: the seven-Domain model (a) split the one venture-research engine into two, and (b) counted the substrate and the governance layer as departments. This test holds v0.4 to its own source on both.
  1. The re-run claims to be "leaner and more honest" than v0.3 (§5–§6). The test grades that claim directly — does FIVE represent the work, or does it preserve the second over-count under a candour framing?

Method. Each finding states the attack, steelmans the design's best reply, says where it breaks anyway, rates severity, and gives a fix. Where v0.2 already found something, the finding marks whether the re-run retires, changes, or leaves it standing. Shared Book 1 shadows are tested for whether they bite the five-Head face specifically — not merely as generic risks.

2. Findings

CRITICAL

C1 — FIVE is not derived from the work; it is THREE plus the two seats the re-run's own source says are not departments. The second over-count was never retired

The finding. v0.4's headline is "seven Domains collapse to FIVE… derived from the work" (§0, §2). But the document it cites as its own derivation — ftlab-role-derivation-from-work-v0.1.md — runs the same work and the same founder intent and lands at ~3 value-creating Engines (Venture Engine · Platform · Knowledge & Ecosystem) over a Core and an Edge (role-derivation §4; Level B). That source names the seven-Domain error as two distinct over-counts (role-derivation §4):

  1. it "split the one venture-research engine into two" (D1 Research + D3 Venture), and
  2. it "counted the substrate and the governance layer as departments… rather than as the Core and the Edge."

v0.4 fixes (1) and repeats (2). It correctly merges Research+Venture+Consulting into the Venture Engine — retiring the first over-count. But it then keeps Coordination & Memory as a Head (that is the substrate — the role-derivation's "Core") and Quality & Guardianship as a Head (that is the governance layer — the role-derivation's "Edge / Guardian function"). Those are exactly the two elements role-derivation §4 says are not departments. Strip them back to where the work places them and v0.4's five Heads are: Venture Engine, Platform, Knowledge & Ecosystem (= the three Engines) + Coordination & Memory (= the Core) + Quality & Guardianship (= part of the Edge). That is 3 Engines + Core + Edge — the determined shape — re-labelled as "five Heads." The number 5 is not a finding from the work; it is an artefact of refusing to name two of the three primitives (Core, Edge) as what the work says they are.

Steelman. v0.4's best reply is its own §5: "Standalone, the department model needs five seats; mounted on a real Core, three of them are faces and two have become substrate and governance." The claim is that because Option 1 declines the Core, coordination and guardianship have nowhere else to live, so they must be named as departments — five is the honest count for a design with no Core and no separated Edge. This is a real argument and it is to v0.4's credit that it states it plainly rather than hiding it.

Where it breaks anyway. The steelman proves the finding rather than rebutting it. Concede the premise — that with no Core, coordination must be a department, and with no separated Edge, guardianship must be a department — and what you have conceded is that two of the five "Heads" exist only because Option 1 refuses to build the Core and refuses to separate the Edge. They are not seats the work demands; they are seats the architecture's omissions demand. The role-derivation is explicit that the work points to Level B (~3), and that counting substrate + governance as departments is an over-construction regardless of whether you build the Core — it is a category error about what kind of thing coordination and guardianship are. v0.4 has not answered that; it has restated the category error as a necessity and called the restatement honesty. A candid over-count is still an over-count. The determination's §10 ("this supersedes any 'seven faces' language") was a ruling about the work-grounded number, and the work-grounded number is 3 — v0.4 cites the very document that says so while reporting 5.

Why it bites. The entire stated purpose of the re-run (§0) is to "derive the seats from the work rather than from theory." If the work yields 3 and the document reports 5, the re-run has not achieved its own stated method — it has achieved it for one of the two errors and stopped. Worse, it does so while citing as authority the source that completes the derivation to 3. This is not a cosmetic mismatch: it means the v0.4 model presents the Core and the Edge to a reader (and to a regulator, and to the org's own memory) as operating departments with single-owner charters and agent teams, when the determination has ruled they are substrate and governance. A reader who takes v0.4 at face value will build five chartered Head-agents — including a chartered "Coordination & Memory" Head and a chartered "Quality & Guardianship" Head — and will have hard-coded the second over-count into the org's accountability ontology.

Severity: Critical. It is the re-run's load-bearing claim (FIVE, from the work) and it fails against the re-run's own cited derivation and the determination's locked count. The number that should anchor the face is 3, not 5.

Fix. Report the work-grounded count as the determination locks it: ~3 Engines (Venture Engine · Platform · Knowledge & Ecosystem) as the face, with coordination/memory named as the Core (substrate, to be engineered — Option 3) and guardianship+capital named as the Edge (governance, founder-held). If the document's purpose is specifically to describe the standalone department model that refuses the Core, then say so in the title and the count: "five departments because there is no Core and no separated Edge — two of which the determined architecture absorbs." Do not present that as "derived from the work"; present it as derived from Option 1's refusal of two primitives. The honest headline is "3 from the work; 5 only if you decline to build the Core and decline to separate the Edge."

C2 — The keystone Critical survives intact: Coordination & Memory is a human-supervised department, not an engineered Core — and the re-run concedes it

The finding. v0.2's C3 (the keystone) held that Option 1's coordination mechanism — memory-as-coordinator — was "asserted, not engineered." v0.4 keeps coordination as a named Head ("Coordination & Memory") whose "integrator" agent "routes work and sequences hand-offs across the other Heads" and which escalates to a human for "any contested cross-Head routing" and "resolving conflicts between authoritative documents" (§3-Head-4 "escalate" / "never" lines). It is "built first, devolved last" (correct for safety) — which means coordination remains a human-overseen department indefinitely. v0.4 states the limit itself: "in Option 2/3 this Head becomes the engineered Core; here it is a department, which is the honest limit of Option 1" (§3-Head-4). The keystone is not addressed; it is conceded and frozen.

Steelman. "v0.4 is maximally honest — it explicitly names this as the seam, the 'honest limit of Option 1,' and routes the architecture toward the Core (Option 2/3) for the real fix. Naming the limit is the most a face-model can do." Correct, and credited — this is more candid than v0.3.

Where it breaks anyway. Honesty about the limit does not remove the limit, and the brief's framing is decisive here: Option 1 by definition doesn't build the Core, so the v0.2/v0.1 "memory-as-coordinator is asserted, not engineered" Critical should still bite — and it does. The highest-value coordination events (novel, cross-Head, contested document conflicts) are also the judgment events, and v0.4 routes them to the human Cortex — so the management/routing function the thesis says autonomous coordination dissolves is renamed "escalation" and seated in two founders (the v0.2 C3 / v0.1 Finding-1 point, restated). The single-coordination-path fragility also returns: Coordination & Memory is the one integrator with no second path (v0.2's single-point-of-failure point, still unanswered). Naming a department "Coordination & Memory" and giving its integrator the job of routing and sequencing hand-offs is the routing hierarchy AI is supposed to dissolve, with a human adjudicator at the top of it — governance theater by the framework's own definition, and v0.4's Cognitive Overhead Index (human attention spent on coordination) is the tell.

Why it bites. This is the precise reason the determination says coordination belongs in the Core and that "this is not Option 1; that is Option 3" (§3d). The re-run's own §5 agrees ("in Option 2/3 this Head becomes the engineered Core"). So v0.4 stands as a correct diagnosis of why Option 1 is only a face — but as a destination it freezes the keystone, and as a face it must not be mistaken for the thing that resolves the keystone. The danger is that "five named Heads, derived from the work" reads as a finished architecture rather than as the face over an unbuilt Core.

Severity: Critical. It is v0.2's keystone Critical, untouched in substance by the re-run, and explicitly conceded by it. The collapse to five does not address coordination's category; it preserves it.

Fix. State, at the head of the document, that Coordination & Memory is not a fifth department but the Core seen through Option 1's refusal to build it — and that the resolution is to engineer it (Option 3), at which point it leaves the Head-count entirely. As an Option-1-only interim: charter the integrator to detect and flag contradictory cross-Head action (not merely route), surface a coordination-health signal, pre-commit which Head's premise wins in named recurring conflicts (so resolution is not always a two-founder escalation), and provide a second coordination path so the integrator is not a singleton. Each of those is a step toward the Core; the fix is self-erasing, which is the point.

C3 — Independent Guardianship at 2–5 humans is structurally worse in v0.4 than in v0.3: Capital & Operations now sits in the same Edge that holds the Guardian, violating the Ch 9 "no P&L" rule by construction

The finding. The determination's true unsolved Critical (§7) is independent Guardianship at 2–5 humans — the founding-team capture problem, untouched by the ground rule. v0.4 inherits it and makes one specific change that sharpens it: it moves Capital & Operations into the founder Edge (§2 row D7; §4) — the same Edge that holds the Guardian. Ch 9's independence prescription, which the determination quotes as canonical (§3f), is exactly: "the Guardian should not carry P&L responsibility… should not report to the CEO." In v0.4, the Guardian is held jointly by the two founders, and the same two-founder Edge now explicitly holds capital allocation, venture finance, and operations (§4: Shayan "holder of Capital & Operations"). So the body that guards values is the body that owns the P&L. This is not a deferred or interim imperfection — it is written into the structure: v0.4 fuses the capital function and the Guardian function in the same 2-person Edge.

Steelman. "v0.4 is the most honest version yet — it logs the Guardian gap as Alignment Debt, names it the inherited Critical (determination §7), commits to externalising the halt-right to an independent director, and explicitly says the mutual check 'does not satisfy the P&L-independence the model wants' (§4)." Correct, and credited. v0.4's posture is the right one.

Where it breaks anyway. Honesty about the gap does not close it, and v0.4 has widened it relative to v0.3 in one concrete respect: by placing Capital & Operations in the Edge, it has made the Guardian a capital-holder structurally, not merely socially. In v0.3 the founders were conflicted because they shared equity; in v0.4 they are conflicted and the governance body literally owns the capital-allocation function — the precise thing Ch 9 says the Guardian must not hold. A regulator, sovereign counterpart, or court will read "the people who can halt on values are the people who allocate the capital" as the definition of a non-independent guardian (head-to-head v0.2 §5.1: "a values-judge who carries P&L is not independent"). The "Quality & Guardianship Head, independent of P&L" (§3-Head-5) is independence of the agent Head from P&L — but the human Guardian above it is not, and in an AI-born org the human Guardian is the accountability terminus. v0.4 has given the agent Head P&L-independence while removing it from the human who matters. This is the Oligarch's Temptation (Michels' Iron Law) at n=2 with capital and guardianship fused.

Why it bites. This is the one Critical the determination says must be solved first, before the shape choice matters (§7; head-to-head v0.3 gating-sequence #1). v0.4 not only inherits it but takes a structural step in the wrong direction by co-locating capital and Guardianship in the same two people. Because FTLAB's primary audiences are sovereign and regulated (DIFC/DFSA, GCC sovereigns), the gap between "logged as Alignment Debt" and "an independent natural person with an exercised halt-right who does not hold the P&L" is the whole legitimacy question.

Severity: Critical. The system's designated first-to-solve Critical, inherited and structurally sharpened by moving Capital into the Guardian-holding Edge.

Fix. Two moves, both ahead of the shape choice: (1) Separate Capital from the Guardian within the Edge — the founder holding capital allocation must not be the one holding the Guardian function for any given decision, and the conflict-of-interest protocol must name capital decisions as the canonical case where the non-capital founder guards. (2) Implement the determination §7 / head-to-head v0.3 #1 answer on a dated trigger, not "as the studio grows": a Guardian with no P&L, a charter clause the other founder cannot revoke unilaterally, and an external Guardian-auditor with a real, exercised halt-right and read access to the audit trail. Until both hold, the model should say plainly that it does not yet meet the independence its own §3-Head-5 asserts.

MAJOR

M1 — The Venture Engine is a charter that cannot compile: one single-owner Head spanning thesis-origination → SPV build → live operation → sovereign co-ventures violates the rule the re-run invokes to justify it

The finding. v0.4 invokes the single-owner-charter rule ("an agent's charter must compile," §1) as a reason to collapse seats. But the collapse produces a Venture Engine whose mandate is "the whole learning-in-action loop — originate theses, design ventures plane-by-plane, prototype, build, and operate them through the pipeline… and run incumbent transformations as co-ventures" (§3-Head-1), staffed by "research, venture-design, build, and operator sub-agents." That is one chartered Head owning: (a) speculative research and thesis origination; (b) venture design across the Five Planes; (c) prototyping; (d) operating live ventures (SPVs) in-market; and (e) running co-ventures with sovereign incumbents. These have incompatible hardConstraints and decision-tier profiles. Research's autonomous tier is wide (explore freely); a live SPV's operating tier is narrow and fiduciary; a sovereign co-venture's tier is dominated by the founders' non-negotiable disclosure rule (Q7) and counterparty obligations. A single charter cannot set one coherent "decides alone / must escalate / never" boundary across "draft a speculative thesis" and "operate a regulated venture's capital in-market" — the autonomy that is correct for the first is reckless for the second. The rule v0.4 cites to merge is the rule the merge breaks.

Steelman. "The Venture Engine is FTLAB's methodology — the whole point of 'the ventures are the research' (Q1/Q13) is that these are one coupled activity, so one Head is the faithful representation; sub-agents carry the differing constraint profiles." Fair, and it correctly honours the founder intent that research and venture-building are not two functions.

Where it breaks anyway. "The ventures are the research" is a claim about methodological coupling (you learn by building), not about accountability scope (one owner for everything from a whiteboard thesis to a live regulated SPV's operations). The determination itself draws this line: its §9 gating-item #5 is "move origination out of the Core / keep it with the human Intent-Setter, so the Cortex directs rather than ratifies" — i.e., origination and operation must not sit in the same autonomous unit. v0.4 collapses origination, build, and operation into a single Head and its charter, which is the opposite move. The "sub-agents carry the constraints" reply concedes the point: if the real constraint boundaries live at the sub-agent level (research-agent vs operator-agent), then the accountable seat is below the Head, and the Head is a label spanning genuinely different fiduciary regimes — a charter that "compiles" only by being vague enough to be unenforceable. The determination's §9 Correction-1 is the deep version: "buildable ≠ correctly specified… the verifier cannot catch a wrong specification because it checks against it." A too-broad charter is a wrong specification that compiles.

Why it bites. The single-owner-charter rule is one of v0.4's three stated derivation inputs (§1, "the framework"). If the rule, applied honestly, forbids the Venture Engine's scope, then the re-run has used the rule selectively — invoking it to cut seats while violating it inside the seat it created. And the failure is consequential: operating live SPV capital and running sovereign co-ventures under the same autonomous envelope as speculative research is precisely where an over-broad autonomous tier becomes a fiduciary and counterparty hazard.

Severity: Major. Real and structural — the keystone seat is mis-chartered by the re-run's own rule — but it is a specification defect fixable by splitting the charter's operating tier from its origination tier without necessarily splitting the Head's label, so it ranks below the Criticals.

Fix. Keep the Venture Engine as a legible label if desired, but split its charter by life-stage: an origination/research tier (wide autonomous exploration, no capital, no external commitment), a build tier (prototype within budget, escalate stage-gates), and an operate tier (narrow, fiduciary, per-SPV, with the SPV's own governance binding). Pull origination to the human Intent-Setter per determination §9 #5. Bind sovereign co-ventures to the Q7 disclosure non-negotiable as a hardConstraint at the membrane, not as a charter line. If those tiers cannot share one enforceable boundary — and they cannot — then the "single Head" is a face over distinct accountable units, which again points to a substrate (the Core) beneath the label.

M2 — Blast radius: the five-Head model concentrates more in fewer agents and two founders than the seven-Domain version did — the leaner shape raises, not lowers, single-point exposure

The finding. The brief asks whether the leaner 5-head model concentrates too much in too few agents. It does, on two axes. Agent axis: the Venture Engine now carries four formerly-separate functions (research + venture-design + build + operate + consulting), so a single mis-specified or captured Head-agent now compromises the entire value-creating core of FTLAB rather than one-seventh of it. The Platform Head carries the licensed framework (~70% of revenue) and the infrastructure every other Head runs on — a single Head that is simultaneously the main revenue product and the substrate, so its failure is both a commercial and an operational single point. Human axis: the 2-person Edge is more loaded than v0.3's 3–5-person Cortex. Per §4, Shayan alone is: lead Intent-Setter + governs the Venture Engine + governs Knowledge & Ecosystem + holds Capital & Operations + co-Guardian. Mehran alone is: lead Architect + governs Platform + governs Coordination & Memory + co-Guardian. That is two people holding five governance relationships plus capital plus joint Guardianship — a thinner human floor under a more concentrated agent layer than the seven-Domain model had.

Steelman. "Concentration is the correct reading of the work and the business plan — the Year-1 team is ~5 people and the founders genuinely do hold these functions; pretending otherwise (seven departments, 3–5 governors) was the less honest model. Fewer seats with clear owners is exactly 'named accountability.'" Fair — and v0.4 is right that the seven-domain spread was partly fictional.

Where it breaks anyway. Honesty about concentration does not mitigate the blast radius concentration creates; it just states it. The determination's whole safety architecture (substrate-anchored oversight, evaluator-agents, the override-rate sensor, the weekly random audit — §6, §3f) exists precisely to make oversight survive the failure of any single human or agent. v0.4 concentrates the work into fewer agents and keeps oversight prose-anchored (no Core — see C2 and M3), so the larger blast radius meets a weaker containment layer. The human axis is the sharper problem: with Shayan governing two Engines + capital and Mehran governing the Platform + the coordination substrate, the "mutual check" Guardian model (C3) is being run by two people who are each already overloaded as Intent-Setter/Architect and capital-holder — exactly the condition under which a Guardian wave-through is most likely and least likely to be noticed. Concentration raises the value of each containment mechanism at the same moment the model under-supplies them.

Why it bites. AI-born safety is a claim that small teams can govern large agent populations without the failure modes of concentration — and the framework's own Cognitive Overhead Index and 5:100 ratio assume the human floor is not individually overloaded. A model where two founders hold five governance lines, capital, and Guardianship, over agent-Heads each carrying a large fraction of the whole, is the maximum-blast-radius configuration consistent with the headcount, not the safest one.

Severity: Major. Real and structural; partially an unavoidable consequence of FTLAB's actual scale (hence not Critical), but the model amplifies it where it could distribute it.

Fix. (1) Within the Platform Head, ringfence the substrate/Core responsibility from the licensed-product responsibility so a commercial failure and an infrastructure failure are not the same single point. (2) On the human axis, do not co-locate capital-allocation and Guardianship in the same founder (C3 fix), and name a dated trigger to bring a third governance principal (external director / external Guardian-auditor) before the agent-Heads are trusted with consequential autonomous action — the human floor is the binding constraint, not the agent count. (3) Build the substrate-anchored oversight (evaluator-agents, override-rate sensor, weekly audit) so containment survives any single Head or founder — which is, again, the Core.

M3 — Substrate-less oversight: with no Core, the five Heads' charters are prose an agent can be prompted around, so the shared shadows (charter gaming, escalation suppression, drift-with-no-dashboard) bite the face harder than the determined path

The finding. v0.2's C4 held that Option 1 has no engineered substrate to anchor the Book 1 oversight mechanisms — hardConstraints prior to reward, Values at the membrane, evaluator-agents reading shared state. v0.4 changes nothing here: each Head has a prose charter ("alone / escalate / never," §3) and Quality & Guardianship is a reviewing Head, but there is no membrane — no engineered protocol boundary between agents and the world, only charters and a downstream reviewer the gaming is optimised against. The collapse to five does not add a substrate; it re-labels the same prose-charter-plus-reviewer arrangement with fewer seats. So specification gaming (phrasing actions to dodge a charter's "escalate" trigger) and escalation suppression (under-reporting the signals Quality & Guardianship samples for) have no architectural floor — exactly the Alignment-Debt metrics (escalation-suppression, reward-hacking) the determination requires every shape to answer (§8, §6).

Steelman. "The statistical mechanisms — sampling, the weekly random audit, the override-rate sensor — can run inside Quality & Guardianship without a Core; measurement is a Head function." Genuinely true for the measurement half, which is why this is Major not Critical, and why the fix has an Option-1-compatible part.

Where it breaks anyway. Measurement can live in a Head; enforcement cannot. The difference between an agent that cannot take a prohibited action (constraint compiled prior to reward at the membrane) and one that is graded down after the fact is the difference between a floor and a tripwire. v0.4 has the tripwire (Quality & Guardianship reviews and can halt) but no floor — and the brief's point that Option 1 by definition does not build the Core means it cannot acquire the floor without becoming Option 3. The five-Head version is, if anything, marginally worse than seven on this axis: with the Venture Engine now spanning research-through-operation (M1), the surface an agent could game spans a wider fiduciary range under one reviewer. The shadows are shared, but they are not equal: the face faces them with prose and a reviewer; the determined path faces them with an enforced invariant.

Why it bites. The thesis's central safety claim — stays within bounds without bureaucracy — depends on substrate-anchored enforcement. The face supplies prose-anchored enforcement and is strictly weaker than the architecture the determination selects. Under the no-Core stance this is the cost the re-run pays and must state: it is choosing the design that declines the very substrate the shadows' countermeasures require.

Severity: Major. Strictly weaker oversight than the determined path on the enforcement half; partially mitigable on the measurement half within a Head.

Fix. Stand up the measurement mechanisms immediately in Quality & Guardianship regardless of shape (override-rate sensor, weekly audit of non-escalated decisions, the five Alignment-Debt metrics) — these are genuinely Option-1-compatible and should not wait. For enforcement (hardConstraints prior to reward, Values at the membrane, evaluator-agents reading shared state) there is no Option-1 substitute: building it is building the Core (Option 3). The re-run should name this as the second concrete thing the no-Core stance gives up (the first being the keystone, C2).

M4 — Knowledge & Ecosystem repeats the over-broad-seat pattern at the second seat, and re-opens the v0.2 standalone-advisory and represented-vs-actual gaps

The finding. The same too-broad-charter pattern that hits the Venture Engine (M1) recurs at Knowledge & Ecosystem, which v0.4 widens to own publications + executive education + business development + partnerships + GCC/sovereign/MNC relationships + brand (§2 row D6; §3-Head-3). That fuses two genuinely different fiduciary regimes: low-stakes content/education (publish, teach) and high-stakes sovereign relationship management (partnership terms, sovereign commitments, the Q7 non-negotiable). Its charter tries to span both with one "never mislead or conceal against a partner's wishes" line — correct for the relationship half, irrelevant to the publishing half, and silent on the founder-sanctioned standalone-advisory exception (Q7: rare cases "where the impact could be great for society but the work does not lend itself to being a venture"), which v0.4 again leaves without a named home (v0.2's m2, unretired). Separately, v0.4 asserts the face matches the real governance but, lacking a substrate to audit against (M3), can assert the represented-vs-actual match but not demonstrate it — v0.2's M4 external-accountability gap, carried forward, and sharpest for exactly the sovereign/DFSA audiences §3-Head-3 targets.

Steelman. "Knowledge & Ecosystem as one Head matches the business plan (Business Development is one near-term hire) and the founder intent that publications double as lead-gen (Q5); one owner for 'how FTLAB is known and related-to' is coherent." Fair at the labelling level.

Where it breaks anyway. Coherent as a label, incoherent as a single charter: the autonomous tier correct for "publish a quarterly insight brief" is reckless for "agree a sovereign partnership term," and the Q7 disclosure non-negotiable is a relationship-half constraint that a publishing-half autonomous tier will not naturally respect. As with M1, the real accountable boundaries are at the sub-agent level (content-agent vs partnership-agent), so the Head is a label over distinct regimes — and sovereign-relationship work is exactly what v0.4 elsewhere flags as "devolve last" (by analogy to §3-Head-4/5 timing), confirming it does not fit the autonomous-department model.

Why it bites. Two of the three Engines (Venture Engine, Knowledge & Ecosystem) are over-broad seats whose charters fuse incompatible fiduciary regimes. That is a systematic feature of the collapse-to-five, not a one-off — the re-run reduced seat count without reconciling charter coherence, so the seats it kept absorbed the merged scope without the constraint structure to govern it.

Severity: Major. Structural and recurring (second instance of M1's pattern), plus it re-opens two standing v0.2 findings (standalone-advisory home; represented-vs-actual gap).

Fix. Split the Knowledge & Ecosystem charter into a content/education tier (wide autonomy) and a relationship/partnership tier (narrow, Q7-non-negotiable as a hardConstraint, sovereign commitments escalated). Add the explicit (rare, Guardian-gated) standalone-advisory path under this Head's charter so the Q7 exception has a home. Institute the floor-integrity test now (sample decisions the Cortex "approved" and require reconstruction without the agent's summary) and keep a structured, queryable decision log to support external reconstruction — the Option-1-compatible half of closing the represented-vs-actual gap.

M5 — Limits of the small-team thesis: crisis / out-of-distribution / complex-novel-regulation land on the five-Head face with no command-mode and a thinner Edge than before

The finding. The founders' own open question (Q13; framework §5.3) asks how the small-team model performs "under crisis conditions… beyond training distributions." v0.2's M5 found a memory-coordinated, agent-run org is least able to coordinate exactly when reality goes off-distribution and the memory's precedents stop applying — and has "no command mode for crisis." v0.4 changes the exposure for the worse on the human side: its only coordination mechanism is still the precedent-laden Coordination & Memory department (C2), and when precedents fail it falls back to the two-founder escalation path that C3 shows is its weakest and most overloaded point (M2) — now thinner than v0.3's 3–5-person Cortex. There is no engineered "unknown decision-class → human-decided" fail-safe (that is a Core boundary-layer component Option 1 does not build), and the cases v0.4 marks "devolve last" (sovereign relationships, values judgment) are precisely the off-distribution cases — flagged, but met with the weakest mechanism under maximum load.

Steelman. "v0.4 keeps sovereign relationships and values-judgment human and 'devolve last,' so it does keep humans where out-of-distribution judgment lives." True, and the right instinct.

Where it breaks anyway. Keeping a function human is not the same as having a crisis coordination mode. In a genuine off-distribution event the question is how five agent-Heads and their sub-agent swarms coordinate when the shared memory's precedents no longer apply and the integrator surfaces conflicts faster than two founders — already holding five governance lines each (M2) — can adjudicate. v0.4 has no designed answer; it falls back to the path that is simultaneously its weakest (C3) and its most overloaded (M2). The determined path at least supplies a substrate-level fail-safe (unknown class → human-decided, by construction) and inspectable state to reason over; the face has prose charters and an overwhelmed two-person adjudication path.

Why it bites. This is the small-team thesis's hardest unsolved edge, named by the founders themselves, and the five-Head face meets it with the mechanism (memory-coordination + two-founder escalation) that is structurally weakest exactly when stakes are highest — and the move to a 2-person Edge plus Capital-in-the-Edge makes the fallback thinner and more conflicted than the version v0.2 tested.

Severity: Major.

Fix. Pre-write a crisis / out-of-distribution command-mode into the charters now: a pre-agreed trigger that collapses coordination to a single named human decision-maker, bright-line "novel / irreversible → halt and escalate externally" triggers, and a pre-committed external-escalation path for complex novel regulation and high-stakes relational events. The fail-safe-by-construction version ("unknown class → human-decided") needs a substrate (the Core); the Option-1-only version is the runbook plus the bright-line charter triggers.

M6 — Naming "Coordination & Memory" as a department re-creates the routing hierarchy the thesis dissolves — governance theater at the structural, not rhetorical, level

The finding. The brief asks directly: does naming coordination as a department re-create the routing hierarchy AI dissolves? It does. Ch 3 (which v0.4 cites in its own method, §1) is explicit that hierarchical departments exist to solve information routing, and routing is the job autonomous coordination dissolves — so named seats must localise accountability, never re-create a routing hierarchy (v0.4 quotes this intent at §1, Ch 3 row). Yet v0.4 then creates a department, Coordination & Memory, whose integrator's explicit job is to "route work and sequence hand-offs across the other Heads" (§3-Head-4) — i.e., a named seat whose function is routing, with a human adjudicator above it for the hard cases. That is the routing hierarchy, re-created and named, in direct tension with the chapter v0.4 invokes to forbid it.

Steelman. "The integrator only routes the routine and escalates the contested — so the human is not in the routine routing path; this is accountability-localisation (someone owns coordination quality), not a management chain." Partly fair: routine routing is automated.

Where it breaks anyway. The framework's own diagnostic settles it: the Cognitive Overhead Index catches an organisation that spends human attention on coordination, and v0.4 routes every contested/novel/cross-Head decision — the high-value coordination events — to two humans (C2). "Localising accountability for coordination" and "seating the coordination-management function in a department with a human at its apex" are the same structure described two ways; the thesis's claim is that the function itself dissolves into the substrate, not that it gets a tidy owner. A department named for routing, with a human resolving the routing conflicts that matter, is AI-enabled by the framework's measure whatever it is labelled — the determination's "AI-born vocabulary on an AI-enabled skeleton" (§3d). v0.4's candour ("the honest limit of Option 1," §3-Head-4) is the tell that it knows this.

Why it bites. It is the determination's structural charge against Option 1-as-destination, now located at the precise seat (Coordination & Memory) that v0.4 keeps. It does not condemn v0.4 as a face — a face is allowed to be AI-enabled underneath while the Core is built — but it does forbid reading the five-Head model as a destination, and it warns that giving routing a permanent named departmental home is the thing most likely to calcify into the management chain the thesis exists to retire.

Severity: Major. A structural (not merely rhetorical) re-creation of routing at the kept coordination seat; bounded to Major because it is a correct description of Option 1's face limit rather than a defect unique to v0.4's execution — but it is the seam the determination names.

Fix. Do not give routing a permanent departmental identity. Frame Coordination & Memory as the Core-in-waiting (substrate to be engineered) rather than a standing department, and charter its integrator to detect and flag conflict and surface coordination-health signals rather than to own routing as a function. The destination resolution is to dissolve routing into the engineered substrate (Option 3) so no named human or department sits at the apex of the routing path.

MINOR

m1 — Capital & Operations as a "future sixth Head" mislabels the Edge as a deferred department

The finding. v0.4 says Capital & Operations is "held by the founder Edge until it earns its own Head" (§0, §2, §4) — i.e., it frames the Edge's capital/governance function as a department-in-waiting that will graduate to a sixth Head. But per the role-derivation (§4) and the determination (§10), capital allocation and Guardianship are the Edge — they are governance held by humans, not a value-creating Engine that scales into a chartered agent-Head. Framing the Edge as a future Head repeats C1's category error in miniature: it treats a primitive (the Edge) as a deferred department. (Operations proper — the ops-manager function — can devolve to agents under the Platform/Core; capital allocation and Guardianship cannot, by the model's own logic.)

Severity: Minor. A labelling error consistent with C1, low operational consequence, easily corrected.

Fix. Split the line: Operations (execution) devolves into the Core/Platform substrate over time; Capital allocation and Guardianship remain Edge functions and do not become a Head. Drop the "earns its own Head" framing for the capital/governance half.

m2 — Single model-vendor fragility is real but option-neutral, so it is not an argument against the five-Head face specifically

The finding. Single frontier-model-vendor dependence is a genuine fragility (determination §8). It bites every shape that runs agents on frontier models. The face's only specific edge is the membrane point already captured under M3: lacking a membrane, charter-level constraints must be re-validated by hand against any new model. Recorded as Minor because it does not distinguish the five-Head face from the determined path.

Severity: Minor.

Fix. Maintain a vendor-independent charter test-suite re-run against any model change; define the model/vendor contract surface so any layer can be swapped without dissolving governance. (Largely subsumed by M3's fix — enforce Values at a membrane, which is a Core property.)

3. What v0.2 findings are RETIRED or CHANGED by the re-run

The re-run is a genuine improvement on two specific points and changes the framing of a third. Held honestly, the list of what changes is short, because the re-run kept Option 1's primitive (named accountability + a memory department, no Core), and that primitive is what most of the v0.2 critique attacked.

  1. RETIRED — the "split the venture-research engine in two" over-construction. v0.2 and v0.1 carried the overlap/seam problem between D1 Research and D3 Venture Building (consulting-becomes-venture sitting on the D3/D6 boundary; v0.2 M1, v0.1 Finding 4). The merge into a single Venture Engine retires this specific over-count, in line with the role-derivation's first correction. Note the asymmetry: retiring it does not shrink the model to the work's count, because the second over-count (Core + Edge as departments) was kept — so the merge fixes the seam but not the number (C1). And the merge introduces a new defect at the seat it created (the un-compilable charter, M1) — the over-count is retired, a charter-coherence problem replaces it.
  1. CHANGED — the seven-vs-ten "calcification" finding is overtaken by the collapse, but the number is still wrong in the other direction. v0.2's M1 attacked the seven-fixed / ten-end-state ontology as calcification-prone. v0.4 abandons the seven/ten scaling story entirely and goes the other way (five, trending toward ~3 mounted on a Core). So the specific "splits into more seats over time" calcification mechanism is changed/retired. But the underlying finding inverts rather than disappears: the model is now over-collapsed at the charter level (two seats absorb incompatible fiduciary regimes, M1/M4) even as it is over-counted at the primitive level (Core + Edge as Heads, C1). Calcification risk moves from "too many seats added over time" to "two over-broad seats whose stable single-owner charters resist the split they need."
  1. CHANGED (in standing, not substance) — the "deployable now / strictly dominated" framing. v0.2's C1/C2 were framed around the ground rule converting Option 1's "deployable now" moat into strict dominance by Option 3. v0.4 does not contest its standing as a face (it agrees: §5–§6, "the legible face FTLAB wears over the Core it should build"). So the adversarial C1/C2 of v0.2 are no longer contested findings — they are now common ground between the re-run and the determination. They are not retired (the substance — no Core, prose-anchored oversight — still holds, see §4); their status changes from "charge Option 1 resists" to "premise Option 1 accepts."

What is explicitly NOT retired (guarding against over-retirement): the keystone (coordination-as-department, C2); the founding-team-capture Guardian Critical (C3, now sharpened by Capital-in-the-Edge); substrate-less oversight (M3); the represented-vs-actual / external-accountability gap (M4); the small-team-thesis edge (M5); the routing-rebuilt structural charge (M6); and the standalone-advisory home (M4). The re-run's collapse to five touches the count and the seam, not the category of coordination or guardianship — so the category-level findings stand.

4. What survives the re-run

The collapse to five changed the seat count and retired one over-count; it did not change Option 1's primitive (named accountability + a memory department, no engineered Core, founder-held Guardian), and the primitive is what the surviving findings attack. What stands:

  • The keystone — Coordination & Memory as a human-supervised department, not an engineered Core (C2). Survives wholly and is conceded by v0.4 ("the honest limit of Option 1," §3-Head-4; "in Option 2/3 this Head becomes the engineered Core," §5). The brief's expectation holds: because Option 1 by definition does not build the Core, the v0.2/v0.1 "asserted, not engineered" Critical bites the re-run unchanged.
  • The inherited Guardian Critical — independent Guardianship at 2–5 humans (C3). Survives by explicit ringfence (determination §7–§8) and is structurally sharpened by v0.4's move of Capital & Operations into the same Edge that holds the Guardian — fusing P&L and Guardianship in two people, against Ch 9's express rule. The determination's "solve first" Critical is, if anything, harder in v0.4 than in v0.3.
  • Substrate-less oversight (M3). Survives — hardConstraints-prior-to-reward and Values-at-the-membrane are enforcement engineering the face cannot supply without becoming the Core; the five-Head version re-labels the same prose-charter-plus-reviewer arrangement.
  • Charter incoherence at the merged seats (M1, M4). New under the re-run (it is a consequence of the collapse) and survives as a standing defect: the single-owner-charter rule v0.4 invokes to merge is violated inside the Venture Engine and Knowledge & Ecosystem.
  • Blast radius / concentration (M2). Survives and worsens: fewer agents carrying more, and a 2-person Edge holding five governance lines plus capital plus joint Guardianship — a thinner human floor under a more concentrated agent layer than the seven-domain model.
  • Routing rebuilt as a named department / governance theater (M6). Survives at the precise kept seat (Coordination & Memory), in tension with the Ch 3 intent v0.4 itself cites.
  • Small-team-thesis edge — crisis / OOD / complex regulation (M5). Survives — a thesis-edge defect the founders named; met with the weakest, now-thinner mechanism.
  • Standalone-advisory home (M4) and model-vendor fragility (m2). Survive as carried-forward Minor/Major items.
  • Genuine strengths that also survive (graded honestly): the un-splitting of Research+Venture is the correct read of "the ventures are the research"; matching the business plan's Year-1 headcount is a real gain over seven departments; the intellectual honesty of naming its own limit ("the honest limit of Option 1," logging the Guardian gap as Alignment Debt, naming the reduction-to-3-on-a-Core). These survive untouched — and, as in v0.2, every one of them is inherited wholesale by Option 3 / the determined ~3-Engine face, which is exactly why they do not constitute a case for FIVE as the right number or for the five-Head model as a destination.

5. Verdict

The five-Head re-run is sound as a face but reports the wrong number, and FIVE is not the right count — the work yields ~3. v0.4 is a real improvement on v0.3: it correctly un-splits Research+Venture into a single Venture Engine ("the ventures are the research"), folds consulting in (Q7), and matches the business plan's Year-1 headcount far better than seven departments did — and it is admirably candid about being a face over a Core it declines to build. But it fixes only one of the two over-construction errors its own cited source (ftlab-role-derivation-from-work-v0.1.md §4) identifies: it un-splits the venture engine, then keeps the Core (Coordination & Memory) and the Edge (Quality & Guardianship) counted as departments — the exact second over-count the role-derivation and the determination (§10, locked at "~3 Engines, not seven") rule out. Strip those two back to the primitives the work names and v0.4's "five Heads" are 3 Engines + Core + Edge — the determined shape under a different label — which v0.4 itself concedes in §5 ("mounted on a real Core, three of them are faces and two have become substrate and governance"). So the re-run's number is an artefact of refusing two primitives, not a finding from the work; the honest count is three faces over a Core and an Edge. Every category-level v0.2 finding survives the collapse intact, two of them sharpened: the keystone (coordination as a supervised department, not an engineered Core) is conceded and frozen, and the inherited Guardian Critical is made structurally worse by fusing Capital & Operations into the same two-person Edge that holds the Guardian — directly against Ch 9's "no P&L" rule and ahead of every other decision in the gating sequence. The collapse also creates two new charter-coherence defects (the Venture Engine and Knowledge & Ecosystem each fuse incompatible fiduciary regimes under a single charter that cannot compile — the very rule v0.4 invokes to merge) and raises, rather than lowers, blast radius. The re-run therefore confirms the determination on the only question that matters for it: Option 1 is not the destination. It is the legible accountability face — and as a face it is load-bearing and should be carried — but the correct face-count is ~3, the coordination seat is the Core-in-waiting (not a fifth department), the Guardian-plus-Capital fusion must be separated and externally anchored before anything consequential runs autonomously, and the two over-broad charters must be split by life-stage. FIVE is the count Option 1 produces only by declining to build the Core and declining to separate the Edge; the work, read straight, produces three.
