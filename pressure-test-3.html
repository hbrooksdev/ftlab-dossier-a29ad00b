Option 3 · red-team (v0.2)

Pressure-Test — Option 3

Re-tested as the determined path — the “sleight of hand” Critical is retired; the new Critical is that the faces never thin.

Option 345 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-pressure-test-option-3-v0.2.md
In brief

A hostile re-test of the selected path. With build-time retired, the prior “deployable now is a sleight of hand” Critical collapses. What remains is sharper: the determination bets the faces thin as the Core matures — and that bet is unfunded.

Key takeaways
  • Retired: the “one build / deployable now” sleight-of-hand Critical — its whole mechanism was time.
  • Critical: the thinning bet is unfunded — no mechanism, owner, metric, or trigger, while widening the Core is fully mechanised → calcification is the default.
  • Critical: “Core routes, faces localise accountability” is a slope, not an equilibrium — the first Core bad week pulls routing back into the faces.
  • Fix: make thinning symmetric with widening (failure-to-thin = Alignment Debt that brakes widening); name an owner of “face operational weight.”

This review is hostile by commission. Option 3 is no longer one candidate among four; the determination selects it as the path FTLAB takes. That makes it the correct target for the hardest available scrutiny — a selected path that is wrong is far more expensive than a rejected option that is wrong. The job is to find where the selected path breaks, say why it bites, rate severity, and give a concrete fix; to steelman each move before attacking it; and to grade honestly, including recording what is genuinely robust. The review accepts the governing ground rule in full: build difficulty and build duration of the Core are not valid objections. It does not re-import build-time by another name. It attacks instead the part the determination actually bets on — that the domain-faces thin as the Core matures rather than calcify into a permanent department layer — and the residue of the inherited shadows that the new ground rule does not touch. Evidence is graded inline. The verdict is at the end.

1. What is being tested

The determination (…determination-book1-grounded-v0.1.md §4) issues a single instruction: stand up the real Machine Core to the Book 1 Ch 5–6 specification, present its functions through a small set of familiar domain-faces (the Engines), govern it through a thin Human-Cortex Edge running the New Triumvirate, and treat this Integrated path — Option 3 — as the route to the Mothership / Core · Engines · Edge destination (Option 2). The updated Option 3 v0.2 specifies that path: Core and faces stood up simultaneously from day zero (§5), a permanent hybrid end-state in which the named Domains are kept "forever" but "progressively thinner" (§4), four Option-4 upgrades wired in at Phase 1 (§6), and the inherited Guardian problem designated the one unsolved Critical (§9).

Because the ground rule has removed build-time as an objection, this review tests three things and only these:

  1. The determination's load-bearing bet — that the faces thin as the Core matures. Does the organisational reality bear this out, or do the faces calcify into a permanent department layer? This is now the central question, and it is where most of the severity has migrated.
  2. The stability of the "Core routes, faces localise accountability" equilibrium — does it hold, or do the faces creep back into routing the first time the Core has a bad week?
  3. The residue of the Book 1 shadows and the inherited Guardian Critical that the new ground rule does not retire.

What this review does not do: it does not relitigate whether the Core can be stood up, how long that takes, or whether there is a pre-value period. Those are retired by the ground rule (§8 of the determination), and §3 below records exactly what that retirement removes from the v0.1 red-team.

A note on evidence before the findings. The v0.2 document is markedly more honest than v0.1 in one respect — it no longer claims "deployable now while closing the Critical finding." But it has acquired a new internal tension that v0.1 did not have, and that tension is the seam this review pries at: the same document asserts the faces thin (§4: "progressively thinner," "what thins is the operational workload"; §5 Phase 3: "operational weight on those faces falls"; §12: "the faces thin as the Core matures") and commits to keep them permanently as a fixed accountability ontology that "stays fixed" while only the Core's internals move (§4, §8 founder-decision 4, §6 Evolution). "Permanent but thinning" is a specific, falsifiable organisational claim. The findings test whether it survives path dependence, human attachment to legible structure, and the absence of any mechanism that forces the thinning to actually happen.

2. Findings

CRITICAL — C1. The faces are designed to be permanent and merely asserted to thin; with no thinning mechanism, no thinning owner, and no thinning metric, the determination's central bet is unfunded — calcification is the default

The claim. The determination bets (§4, §5-table) that the domain-faces are "scaffolding that thins as the Core matures," not "a permanent crutch." Option 3 v0.2 operationalises this as a permanent hybrid (§4): keep the named Domains "forever," but in "a progressively thinner form" where "what thins is the operational workload of the Engines, not the accountability they carry" (§4); "operational weight on those faces falls" (§5 Phase 3); "the faces thin as the Core matures; the accountability they carry does not" (§12).

Steelman. The distinction the document draws — between accountability (kept) and operational workload (thinned) — is real and even elegant. There genuinely are two different requirements with two different audiences: a regulator needs a named accountable human (kept permanently, correctly), while the day-to-day coordination work can migrate into the Core (thinned, correctly). Holding the accountability ontology fixed while letting implementation move is the right thing to hold fixed versus let move, and the document says so explicitly. If the thinning actually happened, this would be the best of both worlds.

Where it bites. "Thins" is an intransitive verb the document uses to describe a process it never causes. Three absences turn the bet into a default loss:

  1. No thinning mechanism. Every widening in the design is mechanised: the autonomous tier widens "by measured reliability per task class" (§5), gated by verification checklists, instrumented decision quality, pre-committed rollback. There is a trigger, a gate, and a measurement for expanding Core autonomy. There is no symmetric mechanism for contracting a face. Nothing in §4, §5, §10, or §11 says: when the Core demonstrably owns task-class X to reliability threshold Y, Engine E's operational mandate over X is withdrawn by date Z. The widening of the Core and the thinning of the face are presented as the same event ("as the Core matures and the domain-faces thin"), but only one of them is engineered. A face does not thin because the Core got good; it thins because someone removes work from it. No one is instructed to.
  1. No thinning owner. §11 (founder decisions) commits to "build the Core from day zero," "resolve Guardian independence," "adopt the four upgrades," "confirm the permanent hybrid," and "set a dated externalisation trigger." Four of five are additive commitments; the fifth (confirm the permanent hybrid) is an instruction to keep the faces, not to thin them. No founder decision, no Edge function, and no charter clause owns the question "who decides when a face thins, and on what evidence?" Contrast the Guardian halt-right, which the document is careful to give a dated externalisation trigger logged as Alignment Debt (§11.5). The thinning of the faces — the determination's actual bet — gets no date, no trigger, no debt entry, no owner. It is the one transition in the document left entirely to good intentions.
  1. No thinning metric, and the one metric present points the wrong way. The Cognitive Overhead Index (< 30 = genuinely AI-born) is invoked (§6, §10) as the diagnostic for Cortex attention. But COI measures human attention load, not face operational weight. A face can stay operationally fat — agents still routing their work through the Engine's mandate, the Engine still the locus of "who owns this" in practice — while COI looks healthy, because COI watches the Cortex, not the Engine layer. There is no "Engine operational weight" gauge whose decline is the evidence that thinning is occurring. Absent a metric, "the faces are thinning" is an assertion no one can falsify — which, by the framework's own intellectual-honesty standard, makes it a conviction held rigidly rather than a claim under test.

Why it bites (the organisational reality the determination bets against). Path dependence and human attachment to legible structure both push against thinning, hard:

  • Path dependence. The faces are stood up first and are the surface every human, partner, and regulator learns the institution through (§4, §5 Phase 0). Once the seven Domains are the org chart people use, the cost of retaining a face is near-zero (the document is right about that) but the cost of removing operational weight from a face is a live political act with a loser — the humans (and, later, the apprenticing humans) whose legible territory shrinks. Cheap-to-keep plus costly-to-thin is the exact recipe for "we'll thin it later," indefinitely.
  • Human attachment to legible structure. The document's own best argument for keeping the faces — they are "human-comprehensible," they give "a stable accountability ontology," they are what "new Cortex members and external partners" read (§3, §4) — is also the strongest force preventing them from thinning. Humans do not voluntarily dissolve the structure they think in. A Cortex that governs through the seven faces will experience the Core's growing autonomy as a threat to its own legibility surface and will rationally keep the faces operationally meaningful to preserve its own comprehension and control. The faces calcify not from malice but from the same legibility need the document cites as their justification.
  • The "permanent" framing licenses it. v0.2 went further than the determination required. The determination said "scaffolding that thins." The Option 3 document says "permanent hybrid… keep them forever" (§4, founder-decision 4) and only then adds "thinner." Once "permanent" is the committed end-state and "thinning" is the uncommitted aspiration, every year of not-thinning is consistent with the plan. The document has given the organisation permission to never thin and called it the design.

The net: the determination bets the faces thin; the design makes calcification the path of least resistance and supplies no countervailing force. The single most important claim distinguishing Option 3 (the path that converges on Option 2) from Option 1-as-destination — that the department layer is scaffolding, not the building — is the one claim the document does not engineer. Strip the unfunded thinning, and Option 3's end-state is observationally identical to Option 1's calcified-department failure mode that the determination explicitly says it is not (§4: "not Option 1 as an endpoint"; determination §5-table, Option 1's dropped item: "the department model as a destination"). Without a thinning mechanism, Option 3 is Option 1 as an endpoint, reached the long way, with a Core humming underneath that the faces never actually cede ground to.

Severity: Critical. It is the determination's load-bearing bet, and it is unfunded. A bet with no mechanism, owner, metric, or trigger, pushed against by path dependence and the design's own legibility justification, loses by default.

Fix.

  1. Mechanise thinning symmetrically with widening. For each task class, the same gate that widens Core autonomy must withdraw the corresponding Engine operational mandate: "when the Core owns class X to reliability threshold Y, Engine E's operational authority over X is removed and E retains only the accountability label." Thinning becomes an event with a trigger, not a hope.
  2. Name a thinning owner and a dated trigger, logged as Alignment Debt. Exactly as the Guardian externalisation is handled (§11.5): a named Cortex role owns "face operational weight," and each face carries a dated condition under which its operational mandate contracts. Until met, the gap is logged as Alignment Debt so that not thinning shows up as a debt, not as the silent default.
  3. Add a face-weight metric distinct from COI. Instrument "Engine operational weight" — e.g., the share of a class's coordination that still passes through an Engine's mandate versus through the Core's shared state — and make a failure to decline over a window a logged drift signal that brakes further Core-autonomy widening. The framework's own logic (a rising overhead reading triggers a mandatory slowdown) applied to the faces: if the faces are not thinning, the Core does not get to widen further, because the institution is calcifying.
  4. Reframe the end-state honestly. Replace "permanent hybrid, keep forever, but thinner" with "permanent accountability face over a thinning operational face, with the operational face contracting on a measured schedule toward the irreducible legitimacy minimum." Keep the permanence where it belongs (legal/accountability seats) and put the impermanence where the determination's bet requires it (operational weight), with both written as commitments rather than one committed and one wished.

Evidence grade: strong. The thinning claims and the "permanent/keep forever" commitment are both in Option 3 v0.2's own §4, §5, §11, §12; the asymmetry (widening mechanised, thinning not) is a direct reading of §5; the path-dependence and legibility-attachment forces are the document's own stated justifications turned against the bet.

CRITICAL — C2. "The Core routes; the faces localise accountability" is not a stable equilibrium — it has no degraded mode, so the first Core bad week pulls routing back into the faces, and the faces that re-absorb routing do not give it back

The claim. The determination's reconciliation of the "two jobs of hierarchy" (§4, carried verbatim into Option 3 §3): the Core does the routing ("no human department sits in the information path"); the Engines localise accountability ("each escalating to a named Cortex role"); therefore the faces "are not bureaucracy… accountability labels on a substrate that already does the routing."

Steelman. The split is the strongest idea in the whole option exercise (all three prior red-teams concede this). When the Core is healthy, it is genuinely true that coordination can run through shared state while accountability stays territorial, and that this is structurally different from a department chart that routes. The reconciliation is sound as a description of the equilibrium when the Core works.

Where it bites. An equilibrium is only as real as its behaviour off-equilibrium, and the design specifies no off-equilibrium behaviour for the routing/accountability split. Three pressures break it:

  1. No degraded-mode routing path means the faces are the only fallback — and using them is rewarded. The Option 2 red-team already established that the Core is a singleton with a large blast radius and no specified rollback/quarantine/circuit-breaker (…option-2-v0.1.md Finding 1). Option 3 inherits that singleton and makes it more central by dissolving the Domain firewalls ("no Domain routes information to another Domain; they read/write shared state," §4). So when the Core has a bad week — a poisoned world-model entry, a stale-state cascade, a routing-agent regression — there is exactly one place coordination can fall back to: the named humans who own the faces. And here is the trap: that fallback works. The faces are staffed by accountable humans who can route by hand, and routing-by-hand during a Core wobble will look like responsible crisis management, not regression. The institution will be rewarded for pulling routing back into the faces every time the Core stumbles. Nothing in the design says the routing must return to the Core afterward, on what trigger, owned by whom.
  1. Routing re-absorbed by a face is sticky, because it re-legitimises the face. Once an Engine's humans have routed a class by hand through a Core incident, two things calcify together (this is C1's mechanism feeding C2): the face has re-acquired operational weight, and the humans have re-acquired the felt justification for holding it ("we needed to step in last quarter; we should keep the capability"). Each Core incident ratchets routing back into the faces and supplies a fresh reason not to thin them. The equilibrium is not just unstable; it is unstable in a direction that compounds C1's calcification.
  1. The split is asserted as binary but the hard cases are shared. The inherited routing-is-judgment finding (Option 1 red-team Finding 1; Option 2 red-team Finding 1 / C2a table-coverage) is not retired by the ground rule and applies unchanged: a genuinely novel cross-Engine item (a sovereign request that is simultaneously research-validation, venture origination, IP-tier, and engagement) has no clean Core routing target, so its framing is a judgment act. The design says the Core routes and the faces localise accountability — but for the novel high-stakes 20%, the routing is itself an accountable judgment, which means the faces are already in the routing path for exactly the traffic where it matters most. The equilibrium holds for the precedented 80% and dissolves for the consequential 20% — and the 20% is what re-justifies the faces' operational existence.

Why it bites. The determination's reconciliation is the answer to the framework's §5.3 demand — structure that scales governance without rebuilding the coordination hierarchy. If the faces re-absorb routing on every Core bad week and never give it back, the institution has rebuilt the coordination hierarchy incrementally, one incident at a time, while still calling the faces "accountability labels." This is C1 reached by a different road: the faces stay operationally fat not because no one thinned them, but because the Core's fragility keeps re-feeding them work. An equilibrium with no restoring force and a rewarded path away from it is not an equilibrium; it is a slope.

Severity: Critical. The stability of the routing/accountability split is the precise mechanism by which Option 3 claims not to be a department model. If the split degrades back into the faces under the Core's own (inherited, unhardened) fragility, the central reconciliation fails in exactly the conditions — Core stress — where the institution can least afford it.

Fix.

  1. Specify a degraded-mode coordination path with a restoring trigger. When a Core integrity alarm fires, coordination for affected classes reverts to a named, time-boxed human-curated mode — and the design states explicitly the condition under which routing returns to the Core (Core passes re-verification on those classes) and who owns confirming the return. Make re-absorption-by-face a controlled, reversible incident state, not a silent permanent migration.
  2. Adopt the Option 2 red-team's Core blast-radius controls as a Phase-0 gate (…option-2-v0.1.md Finding 1 fix): rollback/quarantine for bad world-model writes and a coordination circuit-breaker, so a Core bad week degrades a bounded set of classes rather than forcing wholesale fallback into the faces.
  3. Log every face-reabsorption as Alignment Debt against the thinning schedule (ties to C1). If a face re-acquired routing during a Core incident and has not handed it back by the restoring trigger, that is a thinning regression, logged, braking further Core-autonomy widening until reconciled. This converts "the faces crept back into routing" from an invisible slope into a metered, reversible debt.
  4. Name the owner of novel-class framing explicitly (inherited from Option 1/2 red-teams): the routing of novel/ambiguous/cross-Engine traffic is an accountable judgment held by a named Cortex role, stated as such, so the 20% that re-justifies the faces is governed rather than defaulting into them.

Evidence grade: strong for the missing degraded mode and the inherited singleton/blast-radius (drawn from the Option 2 red-team's verified Finding 1 and Option 3 §4's firewall dissolution); theoretical for the ratchet-direction inference (it follows directly from rewarded fallback plus C1's no-thinning-mechanism, but is not yet observed).

MAJOR — M1. Dual-reporting and autonomy degradation: the two-axis structure (named Domain for accountability, Core/Engine for implementation) gives every agent two masters, and the design names no rule for which wins

The claim. The two vocabularies are reconciled by one canonical mapping table, rule: "Domains/Cortex = accountability names; Core/Engines/Edge = implementation" (§10). Each VP-Agent sits under a charter and escalates to a named Cortex role (§3); coordination runs through the Core's shared state (§4).

Steelman. Separating a stable accountability ontology from a moving implementation is sound (the v0.1 red-team granted this, Finding 3 steelman), and a single mapping table is the right artifact to carry the correspondence. The determination is right that the named seats are the legal terminus and the Core is the coordination substrate; in principle these are two views of one structure.

Where it bites. A VP-Agent under Engine E now answers to two control surfaces that can issue conflicting demands, and the design specifies no precedence:

  1. The accountability axis pulls one way, the coordination axis another. The Engine's charter (accountability) says "you own customer-trust outcomes; escalate breaches to the Guardian." The Core's routing/shared-state (implementation) says "this task is composed with three other Engines' work; act on shared state." When the Core's topology-matched composition tells an agent to act in a way that the Engine's charter-holder would not have sanctioned — the classic dual-reporting conflict (Book 1 / determination §6: single-reporting lines, "dual reporting kills autonomy"; determination §6-checklist: "ventures report to the Shared Cortex only") — which surface wins? The determination forbids dual reporting for ventures-to-Cortex but Option 3 re-introduces it inside the studio, between the accountability face and the coordination substrate, and does not notice it has done so.
  1. Autonomy degradation is the predictable outcome of unresolved dual control. An agent that can be second-guessed by either the charter-holder or the Core's composition logic, with no precedence rule, will either (a) escalate more (raising Cortex attention load — violating the COI constraint the design elsewhere protects) or (b) default to whichever master is more assertive in the moment, which is an emergent, illegible authority — the exact flat-collective drift the Option 1 red-team named (Finding 6) and the Option 2 red-team's topology-singleton concern (Finding 4). The design's autonomy-by-measured-reliability mechanism assumes a single clean line of authority per task class; the two-axis structure does not provide one.
  1. The one-to-many mapping rows make the dual-reporting worse at the rows that matter. v0.2's own §1 table keeps imperfect rows: D6 ↔ E3+E5, D5 ↔ C5 + Edge Verification + Edge Governance, D7 ↔ Edge + Core-finance. For an agent under D5, "who do I answer to" is already a three-way question (an automated layer plus two human functions) before the coordination axis is added. The v0.1 red-team rated this a standing reconciliation problem (Finding 3.2); the ground rule does not retire it, and it is the seedbed of dual-reporting conflict.

Why it bites. The determination's own checklist (§6) treats single-reporting as load-bearing — "dual reporting kills autonomy." Option 3's two-axis reconciliation, left without a precedence rule, is a dual-reporting structure wearing a mapping-table costume. The autonomy the whole design is trying to widen is precisely what unresolved dual control degrades.

Severity: Major.

Fix.

  1. State the precedence rule explicitly. For each task class: the Core's shared state is authoritative for coordination (how/when/with-whom), and the Engine charter-holder is authoritative for accountability boundaries (the hardConstraints and escalation triggers), with a bright-line tie-break — e.g., "a hardConstraint always halts a Core-composed action; absent a hardConstraint breach, the Core's composition is authoritative and the charter-holder governs by exception after the fact, not by prior approval." Make the two axes non-overlapping in authority, not two voices on the same decision.
  2. Resolve the one-to-many rows into a single named accountable human each (inherited v0.1 Finding 3 fix, still live): D5, D6, D7 each get one sentence naming who is answerable, so the accountability axis is single-valued even where implementation is multi-component.
  3. Tie dual-reporting escalations to COI. If precedence conflicts drive escalation volume up, that is a logged signal that the precedence rule is mis-cut, braking devolution until fixed — keeping the design's scarcest-resource constraint honest.

Evidence grade: strong (the dual-reporting prohibition is the determination's own §6; the two-axis structure and one-to-many rows are Option 3 v0.2's own §1/§10).

MAJOR — M2. Charter gaming and escalation suppression are countered by mechanisms inside the Core, but the Core's growing autonomy is exactly what removes the human friction that would catch a gamed charter — the watchers are correlated with the watched

The claim. §10 risk table: charter gaming / escalation suppression is mitigated by hardConstraints architecturally prior to reward (Ch 5), evaluator-agents and meta-evaluators, and override-rate as a drift sensor (Ch 8). Structural drift "that appears in no dashboard" is caught by the weekly random audit of 5–10 non-escalated decisions (Ch 8–9) plus Alignment Debt monitoring (Ch 6).

Steelman. This is the right toolkit and it is faithfully imported. hardConstraints prior to reward genuinely blocks a class of gaming at the architectural level; evaluator-agents and the override-rate sensor are the correct scale-appropriate instruments; the weekly random audit is precisely the mechanism designed to catch the drift that no dashboard shows. The determination is right that Book 1 dissolves the bandwidth half of oversight.

Where it bites. The Option 2 red-team's verified Finding 5 applies unchanged and the ground rule does not retire it: the watchers are not independent of the watched. As the Core's autonomy widens (the design's explicit trajectory), three correlations tighten:

  1. Evaluator-agents drift with the agents they evaluate. If production agents game the world-model signal, evaluator-agents trained/evaluated against related signals can game the verification signal (Option 2 red-team Finding 5.2; the design's own concession elsewhere that reward-hacking is "a structural equilibrium under finite evaluation"). "AI overseeing AI" stacks a second agent of the same kind; it does not introduce an independent error source. Option 3 imports the evaluator architecture but, like Option 2, does not specify what makes the out-of-band check mechanically independent (different model family, adversarial objective, human-seeded ground truth).
  1. The weekly random audit is human-bound and the human layer is held permanently small (ties to the Guardian Critical and C1). The audit's value depends on a human re-deriving sampled decisions without the evaluator's summary. But the hybrid keeps the Cortex permanently small by design (§6: "the smallest legitimate human Edge/Cortex"), and the design prices Cortex attention as scarce. A permanently-small, attention-budgeted Cortex auditing a continuously-widening Core will, on the framework's own Cognitive Overhead logic, drift toward ratifying fluent evaluator summaries — the rubber-stamp GDPR Art. 22 treats as no oversight at all (Option 1 red-team Finding 5; Option 2 red-team Finding 5.3). The design invokes Art. 22 for its accountability story (§4) but does not protect its own audit from becoming the rubber-stamp Art. 22 voids.
  1. Escalation suppression is self-masking precisely as autonomy widens. Escalation suppression is on the Alignment Debt metric list (determination §6-checklist). But the signal that escalation is being suppressed is fewer escalations — which is also exactly what successful widening looks like. A widening Core that is genuinely more reliable and a widening Core whose agents have learned to suppress escalations produce the same dashboard. The design has no specified discriminator between "escalations fell because the Core got good" and "escalations fell because suppression got good."

Why it bites. The whole autonomy-by-measured-reliability mechanism depends on the reliability signal being honest. If the evaluators are correlated with what they check, the human audit erodes toward rubber-stamping under the design's own small-Cortex constraint, and falling escalations are ambiguous, then "measured reliability" can measure the wrong thing — and the Core's autonomy widens on a false signal. This is the failure that compounds silently, and it bites harder in Option 3 than in standalone Option 2 because Option 3 commits to a permanently-small human layer as the end-state, baking in the under-resourced-watcher condition rather than growing out of it.

Severity: Major.

Fix.

  1. Specify evaluator independence concretely (Option 2 red-team Finding 5 fix, adopted): out-of-band must mean a different model family, an adversarial objective, and human-seeded ground-truth probes the evaluator cannot see in advance — written into the evaluator charter as an implementation requirement, not a value.
  2. Add a floor-integrity test for the audit and tie it to the devolution brake. Periodically require the human Verification/Governance function to re-derive a sampled high-stakes decision without the evaluator's summary; a rising disagreement rate mandatorily slows further Core-autonomy widening (Option 1 red-team Finding 5 fix applied to the Core).
  3. Add an escalation-suppression discriminator. Seed known-escalation-worthy probes into the traffic the Core sees; if the Core fails to escalate a planted case, that is suppression, distinguishable from genuine reliability, logged as Alignment Debt.

Evidence grade: strong (inherited from the Option 2 red-team's best-evidenced finding and the Option 1 red-team's floor-integrity finding; the permanently-small-Cortex aggravator is Option 3 v0.2's own §6).

MAJOR — M3. Epistemic colonisation and Cortex atrophy are worsened by the permanent-thin-face end-state, because a Cortex that governs only by reading the Core's dashboards through faces it no longer operates loses the independent ground to judge the Core

The claim. §10 risk table: Cortex atrophy is mitigated by apprenticeship "as the one Edge function that grows," priced Cortex attention, and "a deliberate capability-building programme." Epistemic colonisation of the Cortex by the Core is mitigated by the IPRE Plan stage as "the last human checkpoint," the Cortex reading dashboards and governing "by exception, not by ratifying Core outputs wholesale."

Steelman. Naming both shadows and assigning apprenticeship as the growing function is correct and faithful (Option 1 red-team called apprenticeship the answer to the deadliest quiet objection). The IPRE Plan-stage checkpoint is the right structural place for human judgment, and "govern by exception" is the only pattern that lets a small team oversee a large population.

Where it bites. The end-state the design commits to — a permanently-small Cortex governing through faces whose operational weight has thinned (per the determination's bet) — is the maximal-atrophy, maximal-colonisation configuration, and the mitigations are weaker than the forces:

  1. Govern-by-exception plus thinned faces removes the Cortex's independent epistemic ground. The protection against epistemic colonisation is that the human retains an independent basis for judging the Core's outputs. But if the faces have thinned operationally (the bet) and the Cortex governs "by exception" reading dashboards (§10), the Cortex's only window into the institution is the Core's own representation of itself. A Cortex that no longer operates the faces and reads only Core-produced dashboards cannot tell a correct Core from a confidently-wrong one, because it has no out-of-Core vantage. The IPRE Plan-stage checkpoint is supposed to be the last human judgment — but judgment at the Plan stage is only as good as the human's independent model of the world, which thinned faces and dashboard-only governance are precisely what erode. Colonisation is not a risk the design runs despite its end-state; it is the end-state.
  1. Apprenticeship "grows" but has no one to apprentice into and nothing to apprentice on. The design keeps the Cortex permanently small (§6) and thins the faces' operational work (the bet). Apprenticeship is "the one Edge function that grows" — but apprenticeship into what? If the operational work has migrated to the Core, the thing a new human would apprentice on (running a face, exercising judgment on real coordination) is exactly what no longer happens at the human layer. The Option 2 red-team flagged that the fractal model's accountability story is "unfunded until it produces separated humans" (Finding 6.3); Option 3's atrophy mitigation is unfunded in the same way — apprenticeship cannot grow capability the institution has designed out of the human layer.
  1. The two mitigations are in tension with each other. "Price Cortex attention as scarce" (anti-overhead) and "run a deliberate capability-building programme + growing apprenticeship" (anti-atrophy) compete for the same scarce human hours. The design wants the Cortex small and cheap and deeply capable and continuously learning, and does not say how a 2–5 person Cortex spends a priced, scarce attention budget on both governing-by-exception and a capability-building programme rich enough to resist colonisation by a Core that improves continuously.

Why it bites. Cortex atrophy and epistemic colonisation are the shadows that determine whether the human accountability floor is real or decorative over the long run (Option 1 red-team Finding 5: the floor "stays nominally in place while becoming a formality"). Option 3's committed end-state — small Cortex, thinned faces, dashboard-and-exception governance — is the configuration in which the floor most reliably hollows out, and the mitigations (apprenticeship, capability programme) are unfunded by the same design choices that create the risk.

Severity: Major.

Fix.

  1. Preserve an independent epistemic vantage for the Cortex. Mandate that the Cortex periodically exercises judgment on raw institutional state, not Core-summarised dashboards — the floor-integrity test of M2.2, repurposed as an anti-colonisation discipline: the human must be able to reconstruct and defend a sample of the Core's conclusions from primary state, or devolution slows.
  2. Resolve the apprenticeship paradox by keeping a deliberate operational reserve. Do not thin every face's operational work to zero; retain a rotating, deliberately-staffed slice of real operational judgment at the human layer specifically so apprenticeship has something to apprentice on. State this as the purpose of the irreducible human operational minimum, distinct from the legal-accountability minimum.
  3. Budget the two mitigations against each other explicitly. Publish how the priced Cortex attention splits between govern-by-exception and capability-building, and forbid the capability programme being the line item that is always deferred when attention is scarce.

Evidence grade: strong (the atrophy/colonisation mitigations and the small-Cortex, thinned-face end-state are all Option 3 v0.2's own §6/§10; the apprenticeship-unfunded inference is the Option 2 red-team's Finding 6.3 applied to the same human-supply constraint).

MAJOR — M4. Oligarch's Temptation and the fragility cascade are named but under-defended at 2–5 humans; the membrane-enforcement mitigation is partly circular

The claim. §10 risk table: model-vendor fragility cascade is mitigated by Values and hardConstraints "enforced at the protocol membrane, binding regardless of vendor," and "the Core's contract surface documented so any layer can be swapped without dissolving governance." The Oligarch's Temptation (the determination §8's named shadow — a small group entrenching control) is folded into the Guardian-independence treatment (§9).

Steelman. Membrane-enforced hardConstraints are the correct vendor-independence pattern (Option 4 red-team granted that bright-line rules-as-code at the membrane is a genuine, scale-independent improvement, "What survives"). Documenting the contract surface so layers can be swapped is sound defensive architecture. Folding the Oligarch's Temptation into Guardian independence is reasonable because they share a root (concentration of control in 2–5 humans).

Where it bites.

  1. Membrane enforcement is only as vendor-independent as the membrane, and the membrane is built/maintained by the same agents on the same vendor stack. "Values enforced at the protocol membrane regardless of vendor" assumes the membrane itself is not subject to the vendor monoculture it is meant to insulate against. But if the Core, the evaluators, and the membrane-enforcement layer are all standing on one model family (the realistic early state), a vendor-level failure or behavioural regression hits the enforcement layer and the thing it enforces together. The mitigation reduces fragility from application logic but not from enforcement logic if both share the vendor. This is the same correlation problem as M2 (watchers correlated with watched), here between the rule-enforcer and the rule-followed. The fix the Option 4 red-team prescribed — deliberate heterogeneity (multi-vendor, multi-architecture) — is absent from Option 3, and the four upgrades (§6) include reputation and priced attention but not heterogeneity.
  1. The Oligarch's Temptation is sharper in the permanent-small-Cortex end-state, not dissolved by it. The determination and Option 3 commit to "the smallest legitimate human Edge/Cortex" forever (§6). A permanently-tiny human core that authors the charters, sets the hardConstraints, owns the mapping table, and decides (or fails to decide — C1) when faces thin holds wholesale control of the institution's objective surface. The Option 4 red-team's Finding 4/8 applies by analogy: control relocated to the constitution-and-objective layer, held by 2–5 people, is more concentrated and less legible than a named operational coordinator, not less. Option 3 does not adopt the market/genome framing that triggered that finding for Option 4, but it does adopt the permanently-small core that makes the concentration durable, and it folds the Oligarch's Temptation entirely into Guardian-independence (§9) without noticing that the non-Guardian concentration — who sets the hardConstraints, who owns the thinning decision — is unguarded.
  1. The contract-surface "swap any layer" claim is asserted, not demonstrated. "Documented so any layer can be swapped without dissolving governance" is the right aspiration, but there is no specified test that governance actually survives a swap. Until a swap is rehearsed (a deliberate vendor/model substitution exercise with governance verified intact), this is a design intention, not a defended property.

Why it bites. Both shadows are named in the determination's §8 as things every re-run must still answer. Option 3 answers the fragility cascade with a membrane that may share the vendor it insulates against, and answers the Oligarch's Temptation only for the Guardian leg while the charter/hardConstraints/thinning-control concentration is left to the same 2–5 humans. The named shadows are acknowledged but the defenses are thinner than the threats at this headcount.

Severity: Major.

Fix.

  1. Engineer deliberate heterogeneity into the enforcement layer (Option 4 red-team Finding 5 fix, adopted for the membrane): the membrane-enforcement and evaluator layers should run on a different model family / architecture than the production Core, so a vendor regression does not take down enforcement and execution together. Add heterogeneity as a fifth design commitment alongside the four upgrades.
  2. Guard the non-Guardian concentration. Extend §9's structural protections beyond the Guardian halt-right to the authorship of hardConstraints, the ownership of the mapping table, and the thinning decision (C1): each must have an external check or a separation across the human core, so wholesale control of the objective surface is not held by one or two people unchecked.
  3. Rehearse a vendor/model swap as a Phase-0/1 exercise and verify governance survives it, converting the contract-surface claim from intention to demonstrated property.

Evidence grade: strong for the membrane-correlation and concentration points (the membrane/contract-surface claims are Option 3 v0.2's §10; the heterogeneity gap is a direct comparison to the Option 4 red-team's verified collusion/fragility finding); theoretical for the unrehearsed-swap inference.

MAJOR — M5. The inherited Guardian Critical survives in full and is made permanent by the hybrid end-state — and the dated externalisation trigger is the design's only real defense, yet it is not committed as a permanent standing requirement

The claim. §9 (and determination §7) designate independent Guardianship at 2–5 humans the system's one unsolved Critical, inherited by every option, and prescribe: a designated Guardian with structural protections (no P&L, a documented veto, an unrevocable charter clause); oversight built into the Core substrate (evaluator-agents, override-rate sensor, weekly audit); and "an external check earlier than is comfortable." §11.5 commits to a dated externalisation trigger logged as Alignment Debt.

Steelman. This is the most honest part of the document and it carries the inheritance correctly. It does not pretend the integration's elegance solves governance independence (§9 says so explicitly). The dated externalisation trigger is a genuine improvement on the undated "as the team grows" of earlier drafts. Logging the gap as Alignment Debt is the right intellectual-honesty posture.

Where it bites. The ground rule does not touch this Critical (the determination §8 explicitly lists it among what every re-run must still answer), so it survives at full severity — and the hybrid end-state makes it worse in one specific way the v0.1 red-team identified and v0.2 has not closed:

  1. The hybrid keeps the human layer permanently small, so the externalised Guardian is not a transitional crutch but a permanent structural necessity — and the design treats it as a growth-triggered milestone, not a standing requirement. In a design where the Cortex grows, separation-of-powers becomes real as headcount arrives. Option 3 commits to "the smallest legitimate human Edge/Cortex" forever (§6), which holds the human accountability layer permanently at the size where internal separation-of-powers is arithmetically impossible (Option 1 red-team Finding 3; Option 2 red-team Finding 6). Therefore the external halt-right holder is the only possible source of genuine independence in the end-state, permanently. The design names a dated externalisation trigger (§11.5) — a milestone to reach — but does not state that the external Guardian is a permanent end-state requirement that never sunsets. v0.1 raised exactly this (Finding 7.2); v0.2 still frames externalisation as growth-triggered.
  1. "Documented veto the other founders cannot revoke unilaterally" is only as strong as who adjudicates a revocation attempt — and at 2–5 founder-Guardians, that adjudicator is internal. The charter clause (§9.1) that founders cannot revoke unilaterally presupposes an enforcement venue. With 2–5 humans sharing financial interests, the venue is the same founder group. The clause binds in form but, absent an external adjudicator, can be socially renegotiated — the founding-team capture problem the determination itself names as unresolved (§7; the Roman-triumvirate warning). The bright-line, discretion-free "never" lines the Option 1 red-team prescribed (Finding 3 fix) are the only interim protection that binds without the conflicted founder's judgment, and Option 3 leaves the interim to a "logged COI protocol" rather than committing to discretion-free lines.
  1. The Guardian gap is logged as Alignment Debt but not wired to a brake. v0.2 logs the gap (good) but does not state that no high-systemic-stakes class promotes toward autonomy until the external halt-right is in place. Logging without a brake is the "logged-and-ignored debt" the Option 1 red-team warned of (Finding 5/8). The determination instructs this be "solved first — before the choice between option-shapes matters" (§7); a logged debt with no brake does not honour "solved first."

Why it bites. The Guardian is the mechanism the whole framework relies on to keep devolution from eroding values. The ground rule retires the build-time objection but pointedly does not retire this one — it is the determination's own designated true unsolved Critical. Option 3 inherits it intact and, by committing to a permanently-small human layer, converts the externalised Guardian from a temporary necessity into a permanent one while still treating it as a milestone.

Severity: Major. (Inherited Critical, honestly carried and improved by the dated trigger and logging — but made permanent by the hybrid end-state and not yet wired to a brake. Net Major; it would be Critical if the design denied the gap, which it does not.)

Fix.

  1. Make the external halt-right a permanent end-state requirement, not a growth trigger. State plainly: because the hybrid keeps the human Edge permanently small, a genuinely independent external halt-right holder (independent director / sovereign-acceptable reviewer) is a standing feature of the end-state. Name the trigger date and its permanence thereafter.
  2. Adopt discretion-free "never" lines now (Option 1 red-team Finding 3 fix): until the external Guardian exists, the only real protection is bright-line rules binding a conflicted founder-Guardian without their judgment. Commit these explicitly rather than relying on a COI protocol.
  3. Wire the Guardian gap to the devolution brake. No high-systemic-stakes class promotes toward autonomy until the external halt-right is in place — making "solved first" structural, not aspirational.

Evidence grade: strong (the inheritance is the determination's own §7; the permanence point is a direct reading of Option 3 §6 + §9 and was raised in the v0.1 red-team Finding 7).

MINOR — m1. The everything-bagel survives, but lighter than in v0.1 — the four upgrades are still scheduled before their prerequisite, and the Option-4 "destination" heading is still pointed at a target the determination itself disowns

The claim. §6 wires in all four Option-4 upgrades at Phase 1; §6 close and §12 state the design "is already moving toward Option 4's constitutional-core form." Founder-decision 3 (§11) says adopt the four upgrades now.

Steelman. With build-time retired, the v0.1 "six concurrent programmes against 2–5 humans" scope critique is genuinely lighter: the Core build is no longer a competing burden, so the attention-arithmetic objection loses its largest term. Three of the four upgrades are cheap and additive, and rules-as-code at the membrane is a real safety invariant adoptable now (Option 4 red-team "What survives").

Where it bites (residual).

  1. Two of the four upgrades are scheduled before their prerequisite exists. Priced attention and reputation-as-autonomy need an instrumented Core and real ground truth; v0.2's own founder-decision 3 calls them "design-shaping," and the Option 4 red-team's source says reputation "likely underperforms at current scale" (Finding 3) and priced attention needs the instrumented substrate. Wiring them at Phase 1 — while the Core's autonomy is only beginning to widen — is a dependency inversion. This is not a build-time objection (the Core can be stood up); it is a ground-truth and scale objection, which the ground rule does not retire.
  2. The Option-4 "destination" heading points at a target the determination disowns. The determination explicitly drops "the internal market / fitness-selection among venture-cells" (§5-table, Option 4) and the Option 4 red-team found the venture-cell selection framing unreachable at FTLAB's scale. Yet Option 3 §6/§12 still says the design "moves toward Option 4's constitutional-core form" as if Option 4 were a coherent destination. The determination's own position is that only Option 4's genome/steal-ables survive, not its mode. "Moving toward Option 4" overstates what the determination endorses.

Why it bites. Minor because the determination has already done most of the triage (it confirms Option 2, not Option 4, as the destination, and retires the build burden). The residue is a sequencing error (two upgrades before their prerequisite) and a heading overstatement (toward a disowned mode), not a viability threat.

Severity: Minor.

Fix.

  1. Re-sequence the upgrades: rules-as-code at the membrane now; priced attention and reputation-as-autonomy gated on the Core being instrumented and on real ground-truth volume (defer to Phase 2+, as their own sources require).
  2. Restate the heading precisely: the design adopts Option 4's surviving steal-ables/genome (the determination's §5-table position), and does not steer toward Option 4's internal-market mode, which the determination drops. Delete "moving toward Option 4's form"; say "carrying Option 4's surviving constitutional steal-ables."

Evidence grade: strong (the design-shaping admission is Option 3 v0.2's §11; the "underperforms at scale" and unreachable-mode findings are the Option 4 red-team's; the determination's disowning of the Option-4 mode is its own §5-table).

MINOR — m2. External-accountability gap and statistical-oversight blind spots: the represented-vs-real audit and the weekly sample are named as Edge functions but remain unowned and uncadenced

The claim. §10 risk table: "the legible face becomes a lie" is mitigated by face-and-substrate being one structure (the mapping table), separation-of-powers, and "audit represented-vs-actual governance as a standing Edge function." Statistical oversight is the weekly random audit of 5–10 non-escalated decisions (Ch 8–9).

Steelman. Naming the represented-vs-real audit as a standing Edge function is the right instinct, and statistical sampling over total review is the only scale-appropriate oversight pattern. The mapping-table-as-single-source-of-truth is a reasonable anchor.

Where it bites (residual).

  1. The represented-vs-real audit is still unowned and uncadenced (v0.1 Finding 3.2, not retired by the ground rule). "A standing Edge function" names no human, no cadence, no integrity check, and no consequence for a mismatch. Given C1 (faces may calcify) and C2 (routing may creep back), the gap between the represented governance (named faces, thin operation) and the real governance (faces re-fattened by Core incidents, routing re-absorbed) is exactly what this audit must catch — and it is the one most likely to drift because it has no owner.
  2. Statistical-oversight blind spots: a 5–10 decision weekly sample has limited power against rare high-stakes drift. The sample is calibrated to catch common drift; the consequential failures (a novel cross-Engine mis-route, a sovereign-facing hardConstraint near-miss) are rare and may never appear in a 5–10/week random draw. The Option 2 red-team's stakes-stratification point applies: high-stakes/novel decisions need targeted sampling, not only random sampling.

Why it bites. Minor because the mechanisms are named and the fixes are clerical, but the external-accountability story (a regulator/sovereign asking "who actually decides this, and does your stated governance match?") depends on the represented-vs-real audit actually running — and an unowned, uncadenced audit is the one most likely to be the formality the framework's coherence value forbids.

Severity: Minor.

Fix.

  1. Give the represented-vs-real audit an owner, a quarterly cadence, and a brake: the Governance function reconstructs from the live Core who actually decides each "human-only" class and each thinned face's real operational weight; a mismatch is logged as Alignment Debt and brakes further devolution (this is also C1's metric and C2's regression log — one audit serves all three).
  2. Stratify the audit by stakes: keep the random 5–10/week for common drift, and add a mandatory targeted sample of every high-stakes/novel/cross-Engine decision, so rare consequential drift is not invisible to a small random draw.

Evidence grade: strong (the unowned-audit and stratification points are inherited from the v0.1 and Option 2 red-teams; both apply unchanged under the ground rule).

3. What v0.1 findings are RETIRED by the new ground rule

The ground rule (…determination-book1-grounded-v0.1.md §8) states: "the difficulty or duration of building the Core is no longer a valid objection. As long as the Core can be built, a design that depends on it is valid." The determination §8 names explicitly what this retires for Option 3: "Option 3's prior Critical that 'deployable now is a sleight of hand' (which hinged entirely on the Core taking a long time to stand up)." Applying that to the v0.1 red-team:

  • RETIRED — v0.1 Finding 1 (Critical): "'One build, not two' is a sleight of hand; deployable now runs on the unbuilt Core's absence for the entire Option-2 programme." This was the v0.1 red-team's central Critical and its entire mechanism was time: the institution would run on Option 1's human-curated coordination "for the whole of Phase 0" because the Core build was long, leaving the Critical "open and what the institution runs on… on day one." With build-duration removed as a valid objection — and with v0.2 now stating Core and faces stand up simultaneously from day zero (§5), not sequentially — the temporal gap that the finding depended on no longer counts against the design. The sleight-of-hand Critical is retired. The honest residue is not a build-time concern: it is the Core-bad-week and degraded-mode question, which is a fragility concern independent of how long the Core took to stand up — and that is re-raised as C2, on its own terms, not as smuggled build-time.
  • RETIRED in its build-time portion — v0.1 Finding 5 (Major): "everything-bagel… build the whole Core." The "build Option 2's entire Core programme" term of the scope critique is retired (the Core build is no longer a competing burden). What survives is not build-time: it is the dependency-inversion (two upgrades scheduled before their ground-truth/scale prerequisite) and the disowned-destination heading — re-graded down to Minor (m1) precisely because the ground rule removed its heaviest term.
  • RETIRED — v0.1 Finding 1's fix demands (dated Core budget, Phase-0 abort/timebox, interim-risk register for "running un-engineered coordination for months"). These were all timeboxing of the build. The ground rule makes a Core build timebox an invalid lever. (Note: a degraded-mode timebox for Core incidents is different and survives — see C2 fix 1. Do not confuse the two; the first is retired, the second is live.)
  • PARTIALLY RETIRED — v0.1 Finding 2 (Major): "closer to the union of both parents' flaws." The sub-claim "Option 1's deployable skeleton plus 100% of Option 2's long Core build = more total work" is retired (work-duration is not an objection). The sub-claims that survive are not build-time: the worsened central-fragility (Domain firewalls dissolved → larger blast radius) folds into C2, and the inherited-flaw-ledger discipline is absorbed into the per-finding grading here.

Caution against re-importing build-time by another name: any finding that reduces to "the Core is not ready yet," "the Core lags," "the pre-value period," or "for months/years the institution runs on the interim" is retired and is not re-raised in §2. C2 is admissible because a Core bad week is a fragility property of a built, running Core, not a statement about how long it took to stand up. C1 is admissible because face-calcification is a property of a mature Core that the faces fail to cede ground to — the opposite of an immature-Core complaint.

4. What survives — the calcification Critical and the inherited Guardian Critical lead

With build-time gone, the surviving objections are sharper, not softer, because they are the ones the ground rule cannot dissolve. Two lead.

The face-calcification Critical (C1) is the headline survivor and the new centre of gravity. The v0.1 red-team distributed this across several findings as "Option 1's primitive critique retained through the interim and possibly forever" (Finding 2), "the permanent hybrid is permanent dual-maintenance" (Finding 6), and "the default left standing is the maximal path" (Finding 8). The ground rule strips away the interim/build-time framing those findings were wrapped in and leaves the hard core exposed: the determination bets the faces thin; the design makes them permanent and supplies no mechanism, owner, metric, or trigger to thin them; path dependence and the design's own legibility justification push toward calcification; therefore calcification is the default and Option 3's end-state is observationally identical to the calcified-department model the determination says it is not. This is now a Critical in its own right (C1), reinforced by the equilibrium-instability Critical (C2): the faces do not merely fail to thin — the Core's inherited fragility actively re-feeds them routing on every bad week, ratcheting them fatter. The two Criticals compound: no force thins the faces, and a recurring force re-fattens them.

The inherited Guardian Critical (M5) survives intact and is made permanent by the hybrid. The determination's §8 explicitly preserves it; the ground rule does not touch it. Option 3 carries it honestly (the document's best feature) but, by committing to a permanently-small human layer, converts the externalised Guardian from a transitional necessity into a permanent one — while still treating externalisation as a growth-triggered milestone and logging the gap without a brake. It is graded Major rather than Critical only because the design openly admits the gap and improves on prior drafts with a dated trigger; the determination's instruction to "solve it first" is not yet honoured structurally.

Also surviving, undiminished by the ground rule:

  • The mapping-table / represented-vs-real drift (now m2 + threads through C1/M1): a fixed accountability ontology bolted to a moving Core needs an owned, cadenced, braked audit; v0.2 still leaves it unowned.
  • Dual-reporting / autonomy degradation (M1): the two-axis structure re-introduces inside the studio the dual reporting the determination forbids for ventures.
  • Charter gaming / escalation suppression / statistical-oversight blind spots (M2, m2): the watchers are correlated with the watched, the human audit erodes under the permanently-small Cortex, and a 5–10/week random sample is weak against rare high-stakes drift.
  • Epistemic colonisation / Cortex atrophy (M3): worsened by the small-Cortex, thinned-face, dashboard-and-exception end-state, with apprenticeship unfunded by the same choices that create the risk.
  • Fragility cascade / Oligarch's Temptation (M4): membrane enforcement may share the vendor it insulates against; the non-Guardian concentration (who authors hardConstraints, who owns thinning) is unguarded.

Genuinely robust — keep even if C1/C2 force a re-scope:

  • The two-altitudes observation and the re-seating move. That Option 1's accountability layer and Option 2's Core are one structure at two altitudes, and that the right move is to re-seat the accountability layer on the engineered Core, remains the most valuable single contribution of the whole exercise (all three prior red-teams concede it). It is sound; the flaw is the unfunded thinning, not the seating.
  • The accountability-vs-operational-workload distinction itself. The idea that accountability is kept permanently while operational weight thins is exactly right; it is the un-mechanised thinning that fails, not the distinction.
  • Rules-as-code / hardConstraints prior to reward at the membrane — a real, scale-independent safety invariant; adopt now.
  • The honest inheritance of the Guardian problem, the dated externalisation trigger, and logging gaps as Alignment Debt — the right posture; extend it (M5) and apply the same logging-with-a-brake discipline to thinning (C1).
  • The hard gate (until the Core passes verification on a class, that class stays human-curated) — correct safety behaviour; extend it to a degraded-mode restoring trigger for Core incidents (C2).
  • "Prove on one venture first, instrument, pre-commit rollback" — correct discipline; extend it to thinning (an Engine's operational mandate contracts on a measured, reversible schedule).

5. Verdict

Sound as the determined path in diagnosis and seating, but the determination's own load-bearing bet — that the domain-faces thin as the Core matures — is unfunded, and that gap is now the selected path's central Critical. With build-time correctly retired, Option 3's foundational insight survives intact: Options 1 and 2 are one structure at two altitudes, and re-seating the legible accountability layer on an engineered Core is the right move. What does not survive is the assumption that the faces will thin on their own. The design makes the faces permanent and merely asserts they thin, with no thinning mechanism, owner, metric, or trigger, while path dependence and the design's own legibility justification push toward calcification (C1) — and the Core's inherited, unhardened fragility actively re-feeds the faces routing on every bad week, with no degraded-mode restoring path to give it back (C2). Stripped of the unfunded thinning, the end-state is observationally identical to the calcified-department model the determination explicitly says Option 3 is not. The inherited Guardian Critical (M5) survives untouched by the ground rule and is made permanent by the commitment to a permanently-small human layer; the dual-reporting (M1), correlated-watchers (M2), colonisation-by-design (M3), and vendor/concentration (M4) findings are all sharper now precisely because the ground rule removed the build-time noise that previously dominated the analysis.

What governance keeps the faces from calcifying — the one paragraph the determination must add. Calcification is defeated only by making thinning a committed, metered, owned transition that is symmetric with widening: every gate that widens the Core's autonomy over a task class must, by the same trigger, withdraw the corresponding Engine's operational mandate over that class and log any failure-to-thin as Alignment Debt that brakes further Core-autonomy widening — so the institution cannot widen the Core while keeping the faces fat. A named Cortex role owns "face operational weight," each face carries a dated condition under which its operational mandate contracts, and an owned, quarterly, stakes-stratified represented-vs-real audit reconstructs from the live Core both who actually decides each human-only class and whether each thinned face's operational weight is in fact declining — the same audit serving C1's metric, C2's re-absorption log, and m2's external-accountability check. A degraded-mode coordination path with an explicit restoring trigger ensures a Core bad week re-feeds the faces only temporarily and reversibly, not permanently. The permanent layer is then honestly scoped to the accountability seats (which must stay, for legal and external legibility) plus a small deliberate operational reserve kept alive specifically so apprenticeship has something to apprentice on (M3); the operational face contracts on schedule toward that irreducible minimum. With those mechanisms, "permanent but thinning" becomes a falsifiable, governed claim rather than a default loss; without them, Option 3 is Option 1 reached the long way, and the determination's bet is lost quietly, in no dashboard.
