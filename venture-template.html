The Venture-Org Template

The reusable, fractal pattern every venture FTLAB spins out inherits — the second tier.

34 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-venture-org-template-v0.1.md
In brief

The venture-tier companion: each venture as its own Domain-lattice with its own small human cortex and agent population, sized by sector; how it grows across the pipeline (Thesis → Prototype → Named → Growth); and how the studio governs it without a management chain.

Key takeaways
  • Venture teams are not FTLAB headcount — each venture has its own small human core.
  • Regulated ventures (e.g. Adaptic) default to tighter human oversight; high-autonomy ones widen from there.
  • The venture’s Guardian sits with its own cortex, with studio oversight.
  • Governance scales by repeating the pattern, not lengthening a chain.

This template specifies the standard organisational pattern that every venture FTLAB spins out inherits and then adapts to its sector. It is the venture-tier companion to the studio architecture. It uses the same method as that document — start from the work a venture must actually do, derive the functions that work requires, render each function as a Domain (a lead "Head" agent + its agent team + an assigned human Triumvirate + a Charter bounding its authority), then decide how the whole coheres and how it evolves. It uses the same unit as the studio (the Domain) and the same coordinating model (a small human Cortex governing agent-run Domains through the shared institutional memory and its integrator and one repeated governance question, not a management chain). It treats the AI-Born book and external models as direction of travel — where ventures are heading, held with conviction but not validated — not as a settled specification, and marks such claims accordingly. Where the venture tier differs from the studio, it is because an operating company in a real market does different work than a research-led studio: it builds and ships a product, acquires and serves customers, complies with sector regulation, and earns its own revenue. The template is written to be adapted, not copied. Adaptic — FTLAB's live first venture, in regulated financial services — is used as a worked example where it sharpens the point, and kept illustrative throughout.

0. Read this first: who is an agent, and who is a human

This carries over from the studio document (§0) and is the single most important thing to hold while reading, because it is just as true one tier down:

Every venture Domain below — every "Head" and every member of its team — is an AI agent. They are not people. The only humans in a venture are its own small Cortex: the venture's lead and a few key individuals who govern the agent Domains through three functions (Architect, Intent-Setter, Guardian), plus the studio Cortex during founding stewardship. Humans do not sit in the Domains; they set the Domains' intent, bound their authority, and judge what they escalate.

Two consequences follow, exactly as at the studio:

  • The "company-role equivalent" labels (CPO, CTO, COO, CRO, and so on) are mapping analogies only. They tell you what kind of work a Domain owns by pointing at the nearest familiar company role. They do not mean a person holds that title — the seat is an agent. When this template says "the venture's human Guardian," it means a human in the venture Cortex who governs the Risk/Compliance & Guardianship agent Domain and can halt it, not a human who runs it.
  • Because Heads are agents, every venture role must be a clean, single-owner Charter that compiles. A role with no human governor is a backlog item, not a chartered function. This is why the venture default set is kept small and why ownership is single-owner rather than matrixed.

0.1 How to read this template, and what it is for

The studio document (ftlab-organisational-architecture-v0.3.md) designed FTLAB itself: seven Domains under a founder Cortex (with ten as a documented later end-state to federate into), coordinated by the shared institutional memory and its integrator rather than a chain, devolving toward autonomy on evidence, with a permanent human floor. Its Part 7 establishes that FTLAB is a studio and a portfolio built from the same Domain unit, and that a full reusable venture-org template is its companion. This is that companion.

The template answers one question: when FTLAB spins out a venture, what organisation does that venture get? Not a bespoke design each time, and not a copy of the studio (a venture does different work). A reusable pattern — the same grammar of Domains, Triumvirates, and Charters — instantiated against the work an operating AI-born company must do, then sized to its sector and grown through the pipeline. The method is the studio's, in the same order: start from the venture's work (§1), derive its functions and render each as a Domain (§2), establish the venture's own Cortex (§3), specify the Domains in full with a summary table (§4), size by sector (§5), grow through the pipeline (§6), fix the relationship to the studio (§7), and state the direction of travel and the permanent floor (§8).

Claims are marked as in the studio document: [Framework] = FTLAB's conceptual framework or business plan; [Founder] = founder statements (the open-questions responses); [Direction] = drawn from AI-Born / external models, treated as aspiration not fact; [Synthesis] = this document's proposal; [Evidence] = external evidence used to caution or support.

Two disciplines are carried over from the studio document and from the founder's corrections, and are load-bearing here:

  • The unit is fixed; the lattice is not. Every venture uses the Domain/Triumvirate/Charter unit. How many Domains a venture runs, and how tightly each is bounded, varies by sector and by pipeline stage. The template specifies a small default set that a single-digit human core can actually govern, and lets Domains split as the venture's human core grows — the same federation logic the studio applies to itself, applied inside a venture. [Synthesis]
  • Size to acting agents, not to humans. The binding constraint is human attention, but the coordination problem is a function of how many agents act and how they interact, not how few humans sign off. A venture's sizing decisions are made against its agent population and its escalation load, not against a slogan ratio. [Synthesis/Evidence]

1. Start from the work: what an operating AI-born venture must do

The studio's work is research-heavy: monitor the frontier, investigate, synthesise, validate the thesis, build and operate ventures, steward sovereign relationships, run itself. A venture's work is different. A venture is a company in a market. Its work — independent of sector — is to:

Activity (what the venture must do)The concrete tasks it requires
1. Build and evolve a productTurn the venture thesis into a product; decide what to build and for whom; ship, measure, iterate; own the roadmap and the user experience.
2. Run the technical stackBuild and operate the Five-Planes system (Data, Model, Agent, Orchestration, Actuation) on which the product runs; the data foundation, the models, the agent fleet, the orchestration layer, security and reliability. [Framework]
3. Acquire and serve customersFind demand, win customers, price, sell, onboard, retain, support; carry the venture's voice into its market; turn usage into revenue.
4. Operate the businessKeep the lights on: delivery, supply/vendor relationships, support operations, the day-to-day throughput that a running company generates.
5. Stay legitimateComply with the sector's regulation, manage risk, protect data, hold licences, and keep the venture's behaviour inside its values — the Guardian work, sharpened by sector.
6. Manage capital and itselfMoney in and out, runway, hiring its own (non-FTLAB) human core, board and investor relations, the venture's own governance and culture.

Two facts about a venture's work shape everything downstream, and both differ from the studio's:

  • A venture lives or dies on actuation in a real market. The studio's output is largely knowledge and ventures; a venture's output is effects in the world — transactions executed, services delivered, decisions made for customers. Actuation (Plane 5) is where the venture's value is created and where the most consequential governance sits: the point at which autonomous action must be aligned with intent, ethical boundaries, regulation, and customer expectation. [Framework] This pushes the venture's human attention toward the actuation edge in a way the studio's does not.
  • A venture is accountable to outsiders the studio mostly is not. A venture has customers, a regulator, and (often) co-investors who are not FTLAB. Its Guardian work is therefore not only internal-values work; it is external-legitimacy work, and in a regulated sector it is the gating constraint. This is why the venture's Domains include functions (Customer/Growth, Risk/Compliance, Capital) that the studio either folds into other functions or carries centrally. [Framework/Synthesis]

The binding constraint is the same as the studio's — human attention, not capital — so the same design principle holds: spend human attention only where it is irreplaceable (judgment, intent, values, trust, novel/ethical calls), and move everything else to the agent fleet as fast as that can be done safely, where "safely" is set by the sector. [Framework]

2. From tasks to functions: the venture's Domains

Grouping the venture's work into functions, each ownable end-to-end and each mapping to a recognisable company role, yields a default set of six Domains. These are not borrowed from a book; each is required by one or more of the activities in §1 — the irreducible functions of an operating AI-born company. The set is deliberately small (the studio now runs seven Domains, holding the ten as a documented later end-state; a venture's human core is even smaller than the studio's, so the venture default must be smaller still). A venture splits Domains as it grows and adds humans, and adapts the set where its sector demands — a fintech needs a heavier Risk/Compliance Domain; a developer-tools venture might fold Customer/Growth and Product together early. [Synthesis]

#Domain (the "Head" role)Company-role equivalentWhich venture activity it serves
V1ProductChief Product OfficerActivity 1 — what to build and for whom
V2Platform & Engineering (the Five Planes)CTOActivity 2 — the technical stack the product runs on
V3Customer, Growth & Revenue (GTM)Chief Revenue / Growth OfficerActivity 3 — demand, sales, service, retention
V4OperationsCOOActivity 4 — delivery, support ops, vendors, throughput
V5Risk, Compliance & GuardianshipChief Risk Officer / General CounselActivity 5 — regulation, risk, data protection, the venture Guardian

The contrast with the studio's seven is instructive and intended. The studio carries Research & Intelligence, Knowledge/Memory & Coordination, Communication & Ecosystem (editorial, education, dissemination, sovereign relationships, the intellectual commons), and Capital/People & Operations (including the apprenticeship that grows the next humans) as first-class Domains because the studio's work is research, knowledge, dissemination, sovereign relationships, and growing the next humans. A venture does not re-run those as separate functions — it inherits the studio's platform core, institutional-memory substrate, and governance grammar (§7), and pours its scarce human attention into the six functions that make and sell its product and keep it legitimate. A venture that rebuilt the studio's full Domain set internally would be spending attention it does not have on functions the studio already provides. [Synthesis]

The rest of this document specifies what kind of thing each venture Domain is, how a venture's tiny Cortex governs six of them without a chain, how the set is sized by sector, and how it grows through the pipeline.

3. The venture's own small human Cortex

The founders are explicit that venture teams are not FTLAB headcount — each venture has "its own small human core." [Founder] So a venture is not a studio Domain staffed by studio people; it is a second instance of the whole model, with its own Cortex governing its own Domains.

The venture Cortex is the same kind of thing as the studio Cortex, one tier down. It is the smallest human team that can hold the venture's judgment, intent, values, and trust relationships, governing the venture's agent-run Domains through shared memory and the single repeated governance question, not through a management pyramid. Machine Core + Human Cortex applies inside the venture exactly as it applies to the studio: the agent fleet executes; the human core sets intent, bounds authority, judges what is escalated, and keeps the venture coherent with what it stands for. [Framework]

Who holds the venture's Triumvirate. Every venture Domain is governed by an assigned Architect (designs how the function works), Intent-Setter (defines what it is for and tunes its priorities), and Guardian (keeps it within its values and can halt it) — the New Triumvirate, instantiated for the venture. [Framework] The roles are designed to be in productive tension: the Architect wants the function capable, the Intent-Setter wants it purposeful, the Guardian wants it safe. The health of a venture's governance is read off the health of those tensions. At a venture's small scale, one human holds the relevant role across several Domains — which is workable only because the Domain set is kept small (§2) and because an explicit attention budget names, in one line per Domain, which human holds each role today. A role with no human owner means the Domain does not exist yet; it is a backlog item, not a chartered function. [Synthesis] (This is the same discipline the studio applies to itself; it matters more here because the venture core is even smaller.)

The venture Cortex holds bounded authority — say so plainly. The studio document (v0.3 §2) drops the earlier "radiates, does not descend" language as rhetoric and states the relationship honestly; the venture tier follows suit. The authority relationship is legible: the venture Cortex sets the venture's intent, bounds each Domain's Charter, judges escalations, and can halt. That authority is real — those are the powers of an accountable governing body. Calling it something softer would obscure exactly the accountability a regulated venture must be able to point to. [Synthesis/Evidence]

3.1 How the venture Cortex relates to the studio Cortex

The relationship is the framework's "founding and stewardship" relationship, made operational. The studio is to the venture "not a holding company to a subsidiary; it is closer to a parent and an increasingly independent entity." [Framework] It runs along a clear arc:

  • At incubation, before a venture team is constituted, the venture is typically 100% FTLAB-owned and the studio Cortex holds the venture's Triumvirate directly — FTLAB's people are the venture's Architect, Intent-Setter, and Guardian because there is no one else yet. This is the only stage where studio and venture cortex are the same people by design. [Founder]
  • As the venture matures, the studio cedes operational authority and the venture's own human core takes over the Triumvirate roles, Domain by Domain. The studio retains the strategic and architectural oversight appropriate to a founding steward — typically board representation and architectural review — and lets the venture run. [Framework]
  • The trigger for each handover is principled, not arbitrary: the extent of intellectual property, capital, and operational resource contributed by FTLAB relative to the venture team and other partners. A Domain the venture team now substantially resources and runs is a Domain whose Triumvirate should be the venture's, not the studio's. Decentralisation should not hold ventures and teams back from arising. [Founder]

Founder involvement varies by venture significance. Shayan, as Managing Partner, has more direct involvement than Mehran, as General Partner; they may divide oversight of a venture's Triumvirate or place others in their stead, weighted by the venture's significance (its social impact, or its role as a means to a larger end in the overall thesis). As the portfolio grows, each founder develops their own governance apparatus rather than the studio Cortex personally holding every venture's roles. [Founder]

3.2 The Guardian-independence requirement — and how a tiny venture meets it honestly

The framework requires the Guardian to be able to halt the venture on a values or integrity breach and never be overridden by a revenue consideration. [Framework] At a venture's scale this collides with arithmetic, exactly as it does at the studio's: when the same two or three people set intent, chase revenue, and guard values, "independent of profit-and-loss" is not a structure one person can embody — a Guardian who is also the venture's revenue-accountable lead cannot halt a revenue-driving behaviour against their own interest without halting themselves. [Evidence]

The studio settled this for itself (v0.3 §4): the two founders jointly hold the Guardian function for now — for any given decision, the founder not driving it acts as Guardian over it — under a written conflict-of-interest protocol, with the gap logged as Alignment Debt and the planned move being to externalise the halt-right to a board or independent reviewer as the team grows. The venture tier reflects the same answer one tier down: the venture's own small human Cortex holds its Guardian, under studio oversight, facing the identical independence-at-small-scale challenge and adopting the identical interim answer. The template does not assert an independence the size cannot support. It requires one of two honest arrangements, declared per venture and logged as a known risk where it is interim:

  1. Externalise the Guardian. Place the venture's halt-authority with a party who has no stake in its P&L and a real, exercisable right to stop a behaviour: the studio's institutional Guardian (the studio's Quality & Guardianship Domain, which is structurally outside venture revenue), an independent board member, an external ethics/audit reviewer, or — in a regulated venture — a compliance officer whose reporting line and licence obligations make them answerable to the regulator, not to the venture's growth target. In regulated financial services this is not optional: the regulator effectively requires an independent control function, which the template uses as the venture's structural Guardian rather than inventing one. [Synthesis/Evidence]
  2. Hold it with the venture's founders/lead under a logged conflict-of-interest protocol — the studio's interim answer, applied to the venture. Where, very early, a venture lead unavoidably holds Guardian and operating roles together, mirror the studio: have the venture cortex member not driving a decision act as Guardian over it where more than one human exists; bind the conflict with pre-committed "never" rules that bind without discretionary judgment (the Charter's never-lines, §4); require mandatory external review whenever a values-versus-revenue conflict actually arises; log the gap as Alignment Debt; and state plainly that true independence arrives only when the venture's headcount or an external party makes it possible. This is the honest, workable answer at single-digit scale, just as it is for the studio — and it externalises as the venture grows. [Founder/Synthesis]

The point is the framework's own: the Guardian is the mechanism that keeps devolution from eroding values, so it must be real. A regulated venture gets its independent Guardian "for free" from the regulator's control-function requirement; a high-autonomy venture must construct one deliberately, or hold it with its lead under a logged protocol and accept and track the gap. [Synthesis/Evidence]

4. The venture's Domains, in full

Each Domain is specified as: the venture tasks it owns; its company-role equivalent; how its agent team is shaped; where its Triumvirate sits; a sketch of its Charter (what it may decide alone / what it must escalate / what it may never do); and its devolution posture (how early its Charter widens). As in the studio model, the Charter is the practical meaning of "fully empowered with devolved agency": the autonomous envelope widens over time on evidence of verified performance; the never line never moves. The Charter sketches below are the regulated default (tighter); §5 shows how a high-autonomy venture widens them.

The table above is the canonical specification of each Domain's team shape, Triumvirate locus, and Charter. The notes below carry only what a table cannot — the rationale for each Domain and the one fact about it that most shapes how it is governed.

V1 — Product. Translates the venture thesis into a product: what to build, for whom, on what roadmap. Its experimentation is high-volume and mostly reversible, so its envelope widens relatively early — except where a feature touches a regulated outcome, which is where its Guardian dependency on V5 bites. [Framework/Synthesis]

V2 — Platform & Engineering (the Five Planes). Builds and runs the venture's technical stack as the framework's Five Planes (Data, Model, Agent, Orchestration, Actuation), with MLOps, reliability, and security. This is where the agent fleet that is the venture's Machine Core is built. Its devolution is deliberately uneven: the Data and infrastructure planes widen early; the Actuation plane widens last in a regulated venture, because actuation is the point where an autonomous action meets a customer and a regulator. [Framework/Synthesis]

V3 — Customer, Growth & Revenue (GTM). Carries the venture's voice into its market and converts usage into revenue — demand, sales, pricing, onboarding, support, retention. The governing distinction is between service and marketing operations (which devolve early) and regulated advice or a regulated sale (which stay human, because conduct rules require a person behind the promise). [Framework/Synthesis]

V4 — Operations. Runs delivery, support operations, vendors, and the day-to-day throughput a company generates. High-volume and largely procedural, so among the first to widen — but always inside the compliance perimeter set by V5. [Framework/Synthesis]

V5 — Risk, Compliance & Guardianship (the venture's floor). Owns the sector's regulation, risk, data protection, licence-holding, and — critically — the venture's Guardian: the structurally independent halt-authority (§3.2). In a regulated financial venture it owns AML/KYC, conduct, and the regulator relationship. It devolves latest of all: monitoring can be heavily agent-assisted, but the halt-authority and the regulator-facing accountability stay human. This Domain is the venture's accountability floor. [Framework/Evidence]

V6 — Finance & the Cortex's own house. Owns capital, treasury, runway, board and investor relations, and the venture Cortex's own composition, governance, and culture — including hiring the venture's non-FTLAB human core. Reporting and treasury devolve mid; the governance-of-itself part — composing the Cortex, succession, anything affecting a human's role — stays human, because it produces and protects the humans the model depends on. [Framework/Synthesis]

5. Sector-dependent sizing

The Domain set is broadly stable across ventures; what changes by sector is how tightly each Charter is bounded, where human attention concentrates, and how large the agent population can safely grow. Sizing is a function of the sector's tolerance for autonomous action — chiefly the cost of an error and the presence of a regulator — and of the venture's agent-population coordination load, not of any fixed human:agent ratio. [Synthesis]

The regulated venture (Adaptic as worked example). Adaptic operates in financial services — a regulated sector, DFSA-pathway, DIFC-based. [Framework/Founder] In a regulated venture:

  • Charters are tighter and the escalate band is wider. Decisions that a high-autonomy venture would let an agent take alone — extending credit, executing a transaction, giving anything resembling advice, onboarding a customer — sit in escalate, because each carries regulatory and customer-harm consequences. The autonomous envelope widens slowly, on documented evidence, and only after the control function (V5) is satisfied.
  • Human attention concentrates at the Actuation plane and at V5. The framework's general point — that actuation is where human governance is most consequential — is sharpest here: the moment an agent acts on a customer's money or risk is the moment a human (or a binding, pre-approved control) must stand behind it. The venture's scarce attention is spent at that edge.
  • The Guardian is genuinely independent because the regulator requires it. A regulated venture's compliance/control function reports in a way that makes it answerable to the regulator, not to the growth target — which is exactly the structural independence the framework demands and which a tiny unregulated venture struggles to construct (§3.2). The regulated context turns a hard governance requirement into a structural feature.
  • The agent population can still be large, but its acting surface is gated. Agents may do extensive sensing, analysis, drafting, and monitoring autonomously; the gate is on consequential action. So a regulated venture can run a large agent fleet whose high-stakes actuation is narrow and human-gated, rather than a small fleet that acts freely. The coordination problem is managed by keeping the acting surface legible, not by keeping the fleet small. [Synthesis/Evidence]

The high-autonomy venture (illustrative contrast). A venture in a domain with low per-action stakes and no licensing regime — for example, an internal-productivity or content-tooling venture — can run wider Charters: agents take and execute most decisions autonomously, the escalate band is reserved for genuinely strategic or irreversible moves, and the human core is smaller and more purely directional. Here the constraint is not a regulator but the venture's own coordination integrity — the risk is agents drifting into contradictory or duplicated action — so the sizing question becomes "how many acting agents across how many interaction surfaces, and what keeps them coherent?" rather than "what will the regulator allow?" [Synthesis/Evidence]

A sizing heuristic, not a formula. For each Domain, place each class of decision in autonomous / escalate / never by asking three questions: How reversible is the action? How large is the harm if it is wrong? Is there a regulator or external party who must be able to point to an accountable human? The more irreversible, the more harmful, and the more externally-accountable, the more the decision sits in escalate or never, and the later that Domain devolves. Adaptic sits far toward the tight end on all three; a low-stakes internal venture sits far toward the wide end. Most ventures sit between, and the template expects each to publish its own placement rather than inherit a number. [Synthesis]

6. Growth across the pipeline

A venture is not born with six fully-staffed Domains. The organisation grows through the framework's Venture Creation Pipeline (Research Thesis → Prototype → Named → Growth), and the studio's role shifts from operator to steward as it grows. [Framework] The mechanism of growth is the studio's mechanism, applied internally: Domains come into existence as the venture acquires the human to hold their Triumvirate and the work to justify them, and each Domain's Charter widens from escalate-heavy toward autonomous on evidence of verified performance.

Stage 1 — Research Thesis. There is no venture organisation yet. The venture exists as a hypothesis inside the studio's Venture Domain. The "Cortex" is the studio's. No venture Domains exist. [Framework]

Stage 2 — Prototype. A thin organisation appears, run almost entirely by the studio. In practice only two functions are live: a Product Domain (V1) testing whether the thing can be built and wanted, and a Platform Domain (V2) standing up the minimum Five-Planes stack. Risk/Compliance (V5) exists as a checklist held by the studio's Guardian, not yet a venture Domain. The venture is typically 100% FTLAB-owned and the studio Cortex holds every Triumvirate role. Charters are almost entirely escalate — the prototype proves assumptions under close human watch. [Framework/Founder]

Stage 3 — Named Venture. The organisation becomes real. The venture is formally constituted with its own identity, its own (non-FTLAB) human core beginning to form, and governance formalised on the New Triumvirate. This is where the full six-Domain set comes into being — Customer/Growth (V3), Operations (V4), a standing Risk/Compliance & Guardianship Domain (V5), and Finance & the Cortex's house (V6) join Product and Platform. The studio transitions from sole operator to founding steward: it begins handing Triumvirate roles to the venture's own core, Domain by Domain, on the IP/resource-contribution trigger (§3.1). Charters begin to widen in the lowest-stakes Domains (Operations, service-side GTM). The governance model at this stage varies by venture — wholly FTLAB-owned, co-founder equity, or co-owned with a sovereign/institutional partner who contributed to the venture's development. [Framework/Founder]

Stage 4 — Growth Venture. The venture runs itself. It has product-market fit, may raise external capital, and expands its own team. The venture's human core holds its full Triumvirate; the studio's role narrows to strategic stewardship — board representation and architectural oversight — having ceded operational authority. Domains may now split as the human core grows: a single Customer/Growth Domain may divide into Sales, Marketing, and Customer Success; Platform may spin out a distinct Data or Security Domain. This is the same federation logic the studio uses on itself, now operating inside the venture. Charters are at their widest consistent with the sector — and in a regulated venture, V5's halt-authority and Actuation gating remain human throughout. [Framework/Synthesis]

Across all four stages, two things are held constant and one thing changes. Held constant: the unit (Domain/Triumvirate/Charter) and the floor (a nameable, answerable human behind the venture's consequential action). Changing: who holds the Triumvirate (studio → venture core) and how wide the Charters are (escalate-heavy → as-autonomous-as-the-sector-allows). The studio's stewardship is the framework's "stewardship over extraction" made concrete: governance decentralises as the venture matures, paced by what serves the venture's success, not by what maximises the studio's short-term return. [Framework]

7. Relationship to the studio: what is inherited, what is the venture's own, and how the studio governs without a chain

A venture is a second instance of the model, but it is not built from nothing each time. It inherits a great deal from the studio and owns the rest.

What the venture inherits from FTLAB:

  • The shared platform core. The agent-orchestration infrastructure, the Five-Planes tooling, and the VP-Agent platform the studio builds and licenses are the substrate a venture's Platform Domain (V2) builds on, not something each venture reinvents. A venture's engineers configure and extend the shared core for their sector; they do not start at bare metal. [Framework/Synthesis]
  • Shared institutional memory, the integrator, and governance grammar. The venture writes into, and reads from, the studio's institutional-memory substrate (the studio's keystone Domain, D2), whose integrator — the routine-coordination agent that lives with that memory — routes work, sequences handoffs, and surfaces conflicts rather than commanding them. The venture governs with the same vocabulary — Domains, Triumvirate, Charters, the autonomous/escalate/never structure, the diagnostic metrics. Coordination runs through the shared memory and its integrator, not a management chain. This shared grammar is what lets the studio govern a growing portfolio without a lengthening chain: every venture is legible to the studio because every venture is described in the same terms. [Framework/Synthesis]
  • Founding stewardship. Early on, the studio is the venture's Cortex (§3.1); it contributes thesis, architecture, capital, and operating methodology, and it provides the independent Guardian a tiny venture cannot yet construct for itself (§3.2). [Framework/Founder]
  • The diagnostic metrics. The framework's three gauges apply at the venture level as early-warning indicators: Cognitive Overhead (is the venture's human core spending its attention on judgment, or being consumed by operations? — a rising reading means the venture is drifting toward a conventional structure), Alignment Debt (the gap between the venture's stated values and its agents' actual behaviour, tracked and managed, not pretended to zero), and Iteration Half-Life (how fast the venture completes a full learning cycle; lengthening signals calcification). [Framework]

What is the venture's own:

  • Its own Cortex — its own Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian, increasingly its own (non-FTLAB) people.
  • Its own agent population, sized to its sector (§5), built on but distinct from the shared core.
  • Its own Charters — the same structure, but its own autonomous/escalate/never placements, set by its sector.
  • Its own P&L, customers, regulator, and trust relationships — the things that make it accountable to outsiders and that the studio cannot hold on its behalf.

How the studio governs without a management chain. The studio does not manage its ventures through a reporting line. It governs each venture by applying, to that venture exactly as to each studio Domain, one repeated question: who holds Architect, Intent-Setter, and Guardian here, and are they in tension? [Framework] A venture is well-governed when those three roles are clearly assigned to named humans, adequately resourced, and in healthy productive tension — and the studio's oversight is the disciplined recurrence of that question, not a chain of command. This is how governance federates: the pattern repeats at the venture tier, and the chain does not lengthen as the portfolio grows.

The honest caveat (a known frontier, marked as such). The framework lists "how does governance scale across a portfolio without hierarchical bureaucracy" as an open question, and the federation answer should be held as a hypothesis to be tested on ventures #2 and #3, not as a settled result. [Framework] Two specific gaps the template flags rather than papers over:

  1. The single question is a diagnostic, not a coordinator. Asking "are the three roles in tension?" tells the studio whether a venture is well-governed; it does not, on its own, resolve a live conflict — between two ventures, or between a venture and the studio. Federation copies the unit; it does not automatically coordinate the copies. The template therefore requires a thin, explicitly human-held integration point in the studio Cortex whose job is exactly the cross-venture and venture-versus-studio conflicts the single question cannot resolve: two ventures competing for the same sovereign relationship, two ventures contradicting each other in public, a venture whose interest diverges from the studio's. This is not rebuilding a pyramid; it is naming the irreducible minimum of coordination that cannot yet be automated. [Synthesis/Evidence]
  2. The escalate→autonomous boundary needs protecting from ratchet. Charters widen "on evidence of verified performance," but small autonomous wins can widen the envelope by ratchet until the cumulative effect has crossed a line no single step appeared to cross. The never line is immovable by design; the escalate→autonomous boundary must be given comparable protection — each widening logged, attributed to a named human, and reversible, with a rising Cognitive Overhead or Alignment Debt reading triggering a mandatory slowdown of further widening. [Synthesis/Evidence]

8. Direction of travel and the permanent human floor

This is where a venture is heading — stated, as in the studio document, as a trajectory the venture tests in action, paced by evidence, not as a schedule. [Direction/Founder]

The direction. Over time, more of each venture Domain's work — and eventually much of its coordination — moves from the venture's human core to its agent fleet, so that the human core shrinks toward the smallest team that can hold the venture's judgment, values, and accountability. A mature FTLAB venture looks like a very small human Cortex coordinating a large, capable agent population that does the building, selling, operating, and monitoring, with humans concentrated at the venture's irreducible edges. This is the studio's own direction of travel, instantiated in a company in a market — and FTLAB intends its ventures to be among the demonstrations of it. It is an aspiration tested in action, not a validated operating manual, and the template treats it accordingly. [Direction]

The mechanism is the studio's: widen each Domain's Charter from escalate toward autonomous on verified performance, never touching the never line, while the venture's human role in each Domain shifts from doing the work, to directing it, to only setting its intent and judging what it escalates. The sequence weights systemic stakes, not only per-action stakes: a Domain other Domains depend on (a venture's own coordination substrate, its Platform Actuation plane) devolves late even if its individual actions look low-stakes, because its drift would silently mis-coordinate everything downstream. [Direction/Synthesis]

The sequence, for a venture (per-Domain ordering is in the §4.0 table's devolution column). The rule: devolve highest-volume, lowest-stakes-per-action work first — Operations, service-and-marketing GTM, the Data plane of Platform; keep human-anchored where trust, novelty, ethics, and external accountability live — Risk/Compliance & Guardianship (V5), the Actuation plane of Platform in a regulated venture, regulated advice and sales in GTM (V3), and the governance-of-itself part of V6.

The permanent floor. "Fully autonomous" can become true of a venture's doing and much of its coordinating; it cannot become true of its accountability. The reason is legitimacy, not capability: a regulator, a customer, a court, or a sovereign co-investor needs a nameable, answerable human — and a venture's values must be authored by humans even when they are implemented by agents. So a venture's end-state is best described not as "no humans" but as the smallest human Cortex that can hold legitimacy, novel and ethical judgment, values authorship, and trust relationships — governing a near-fully-autonomous agent company. The four irreducible human holdings are:

  1. Legitimacy — the human a regulator, customer, or court can hold answerable. In a regulated venture this is non-negotiable and arrives early (the control function); in any venture it is permanent.
  2. Novel and ethical calls — the genuinely new, contested, or off-distribution decisions a venture's agents have no precedent for, which are exactly the decisions a memory-coordinated agent org is least equipped to make and where a human judgment is most valuable.
  3. Values authorship — the venture's Values-level constraints and Charter never-lines must be human-originated and human-changeable only; agent proposals are logged as proposals, never adopted as values. This protects the floor from being silently laundered through the agent layer.
  4. Trust relationships — the customer, regulator, partner, and co-investor relationships on which the venture's defensibility ultimately rests (the framework's A.G.E.N.T. stack puts Trust at the top for this reason); these are held by humans because trust is extended to humans.

Protecting the floor from silent erosion. A nameable human guarantees a human signs, not that a human judges; as agents handle more, the human's ability to meaningfully review what they sign can decay into a formality — which the framework's own Cognitive Overhead and Alignment Debt metrics predict. The template therefore requires a periodic floor-integrity test: can the venture's accountable human reconstruct and defend the reasoning behind a sampled decision without the agent's summary? A failing test, or a rising Cognitive Overhead / Alignment Debt reading, triggers a mandatory slowdown of devolution. The floor is kept real by being tested, not by being asserted. [Synthesis/Evidence]

This is the framework's founder-sanctioned end-state — an "autonomous methodology with human stewardship at its core" — made precise for a venture: not the disappearance of the human core, but its concentration into the smallest set of holdings that legitimacy, judgment, authorship, and trust genuinely require. [Direction/Framework]

9. Status, assumptions, and decisions for the founders

This is v0.1 of the venture-org template, aligned to studio architecture v0.3 — the second tier promised in Part 7 of the studio architecture, written to the same method and voice, with the book held as direction rather than specification.

Assumptions made (flag for correction):

  • A six-Domain default (Product, Platform, Customer/Growth, Operations, Risk/Compliance & Guardianship, Finance) is proposed as the smallest set that covers an operating AI-born company's real work and that a single-digit human core can govern. The number is a proposal, derived from the work — not doctrine, and expected to be adapted per sector and split as ventures grow. [Synthesis]
  • The regulated Charter sketches in §4 are the tighter default, with high-autonomy ventures widening from there. This reflects that FTLAB's live first venture (Adaptic) is regulated, so the safe default is the regulated one. [Synthesis]
  • Adaptic specifics are kept illustrative — regulated financial services, DIFC-based, DFSA pathway, live first venture. No internal operating detail of Adaptic has been invented; where the example is used it draws only on these grounded facts. [Founder/Framework]

Where this template extends or diverges from the studio document:

  • It uses the same unit and coordinating model, deliberately (Domain, Triumvirate, Charter, coordination through the shared memory and its integrator, the single governance question, the permanent floor) — fidelity to the studio doc's Part 7 is the point.
  • It derives a different and smaller Domain set, because a venture's work (build/sell/operate/comply) differs from the studio's (research/knowledge/ventures/relationships). The studio now runs seven Domains (with ten documented as a later end-state); the venture default is six.
  • It builds in the venture-tier robustness the studio doc's own pressure-test asked for: an explicit cross-venture/venture-studio integration point (federation copies the unit but does not coordinate the copies); honest handling of Guardian-independence at tiny scale (externalise, or hold it with the venture's lead under a logged conflict-of-interest protocol — the studio's own interim answer — and log the gap); devolve the coordination-critical pieces late; protect the escalate→autonomous boundary from ratchet; a floor-integrity test against silent erosion; and plain language that the venture Cortex holds bounded authority (dropping the earlier "radiates, does not descend" rhetoric, as v0.3 does).

Decisions only the founders can make:

  1. Confirm the default Domain count and set — six, or a different small number; whether to merge or split any of the six for the studio's expected near-term sector mix.
  2. Confirm the Guardian arrangement for the first non-regulated venture — where the regulator does not supply independence for free, which externalisation (studio Guardian / board member / external reviewer) is the standard.
  3. Confirm the studio's integration point — who, on the studio Cortex, holds cross-venture and venture-versus-studio conflict resolution today (this is the same role the studio architecture debates for itself).
  4. Confirm the devolution risk appetite per sector — how fast Charters may widen in a regulated venture versus a high-autonomy one.
  5. Naming — "Domain", "Cortex", and the V1–V6 labels are inherited from / consistent with the studio document; cheap to change.

Held, as the framework asks, with conviction but not rigidity — written to be tested against FTLAB's actual ventures (Adaptic first, then #2 and #3) and revised by what they teach. The federation claim in particular is a hypothesis the portfolio will test, not a result it has proven.
