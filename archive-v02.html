Org Architecture v0.2 (superseded)

The regrounded ten-Domain draft; superseded by the decided seven (v0.3).

19 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-organisational-architecture-v0.2.md
In brief

Superseded by v0.3. The regrounding pass (framework-and-tasks first, book as direction) with ten Domains, before consolidating to the decided seven.

This version regrounds the organisational design where it belongs: in FTLAB's conceptual framework and in the concrete work FTLAB has to perform. The method is deliberately ordered — first establish what FTLAB does, then derive the functions that work requires, then assign roles to those functions, then decide how the whole is governed and how it evolves. The AI-Born book and external models (notably Block's "From Hierarchy to Intelligence") are treated as direction of travel — a thesis about where AI-born institutions are heading, held with conviction but not yet validated — not as a blueprint for where FTLAB is today. Where this document draws on them, it says so and marks the claim as directional.

0. How this version differs, and how to read it

The prior draft started from a debate about hierarchy and from the book's detailed agent machinery, and worked back toward FTLAB. That was the wrong order. This version starts from FTLAB's own purpose and activities — as set out in the conceptual framework and the business plan — and asks the only question that should generate an org chart: what work must this institution do, and what roles must exist to do it well? The book supplies the long-range direction (a small human team coordinating an increasingly autonomous agentic workforce); the framework supplies the operating principles (Machine Core + Human Cortex, the New Triumvirate, the Knowledge Flywheel); FTLAB's actual activities supply the functions. The org is the answer, not the premise.

Claims are marked: [Framework] = FTLAB's conceptual framework or business plan; [Founder] = founder statements; [Direction] = drawn from AI-Born / external models, treated as aspiration not fact; [Synthesis] = my proposal; [Evidence] = external evidence used to caution or support.

1. Start from the work: what FTLAB has to do

FTLAB is a thesis-driven venture architect: a research-led institution that designs, builds, and scales AI-born ventures, and that intends to demonstrate its own thesis by being run as a small human team coordinating a large agentic workforce. [Framework] Its activities, as stated in the framework and business plan, are four streams plus education plus the work of running itself:

Activity (stream)The concrete tasks it requires
1. Research & DevelopmentMonitor the frontier (models, regulation, competition); run deep investigations; synthesise findings into frameworks; validate the thesis against evidence; maintain methodology and rigour; publish.
2. Venture Architecture & BuildingTurn research into venture hypotheses; design ventures plane-by-plane; prototype and incubate; build and operate them through the pipeline (Research Thesis → Prototype → Named → Growth); set up each venture's own governance and team.
3. Consulting & AdvisoryEngage sovereign and institutional partners; design AI-transformation and organisational architecture for them; generate evidence and venture-origination from each engagement. (Always tied to a venture or investment — never standalone advisory.) [Framework]
4. IP LicensingProductise proven frameworks and methodologies; license them; build the software/platform that carries them; capture implementation feedback from licensees.
5. Education & Thought LeadershipExecutive education; accessible translations of the research; convenings (Edition One, Sessions); dissemination.
6. Running the institutionCapital, runway, and resource allocation; governance, legal, and DIFC/DFSA compliance; institutional memory; people and culture; sovereign and partner relationships; and the platform/agent infrastructure everything else runs on.

Two facts about this work shape everything downstream:

  • Consulting is not a separate business; it is a mode of venture-building and research. The framework is explicit that advisory is always tied to a venture or investment and functions as a sensing/evidence mechanism for the Knowledge Flywheel. So it does not need its own org function — it lives inside venture-building and partner relationships. [Framework]
  • The binding constraint is human attention, not capital. For a small team operating across all of this, "the most binding constraint is not capital but attention." The org must therefore be designed to spend human attention only where it is irreplaceable — judgment, intent, values, and relationships — and to move everything else to the agentic workforce as fast as that can be done safely. This single principle, taken from FTLAB's own framework, generates the entire structure that follows. [Framework]

2. From tasks to roles: the functions FTLAB needs

Grouping the tasks above into coherent functions — each large enough to be owned end-to-end, each mapping to a recognisable human-organisation role — yields ten functions. These are not invented; each is required by one or more of FTLAB's activities, and most already exist in scattered form across the 28 agent definitions already drafted in the repository. The design move is to elevate those scattered agents into ten accountable functions, and to add the four functions the current roster is missing for an institution that builds and operates ventures (rather than only researching them).

The four genuine additions — a venture builder/operator (today FTLAB designs ventures on paper but has no role that runs one), capital/finance, platform/product engineering, and an apprenticeship pipeline — are not borrowed from the book; they are simply what an institution that builds and operates ventures, raises and allocates capital, ships software, and intends to last beyond its founders must have. The current roster's missing "Platform Architect" (referenced by other agents but never created) confirms the platform gap was already half-recognised. [Synthesis]

This is the "heads of major functions with teams underneath, mapped to human roles" that was asked for: ten functions, each ownable, each mapping to a C-suite equivalent. The next section is about what kind of thing each head is, and how ten of them cohere without a management pyramid.

3. How the functions cohere: FTLAB's own operating principles

The framework already contains the principles that determine how these ten functions should be organised. We do not need the book for this part; we need FTLAB's own architecture.

Machine Core + Human Cortex. Autonomous systems handle execution; a small human team provides "judgement, intention, interpretation, creativity." The relationship is "not hierarchical but symbiotic." So each function is run by the agentic workforce (the Core) and governed by the human team (the Cortex). A "Head of Function" is therefore best rendered as a lead agent that owns and orchestrates that function's work, with humans setting its intent, bounding its authority, and judging what it escalates — not as a human manager routing tasks to subordinates. [Framework]

The New Triumvirate. Governance is distributed across three functions — Architect (designs how a function works), Intent-Setter (defines what it is for and tunes its priorities), Guardian (keeps it within its values and can halt it) — held by individuals, teams, or, over time, by machine capability under verification. Every one of the ten functions is governed by an assigned Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian. This is the accountability map: for any function, it must be answerable in one sentence who performs each of the three. [Framework]

The Knowledge Flywheel as the coordinating substance. The framework already says FTLAB's coherence comes not from a management chain but from knowledge circulating between research, ventures, consulting, and licensing. That circulation — FTLAB's institutional memory, kept current and shared — is what lets ten functions coordinate without reporting up to a common boss. Function 2 (Knowledge & Institutional Memory) exists precisely to make that substrate real. [Framework]

"Radiates, does not descend." The framework's master diagram is explicit that the architecture is "a learning architecture, not a command-and-control hierarchy," with worldview at the centre and operations at the edge, and feedback flowing inward. The org chart should be read the same way: the human Cortex is not above the functions issuing orders; it is at the centre holding intent and values, with the functions radiating outward and learning flowing back. [Framework]

Put together, these give FTLAB's organisational model in one sentence: a small human Cortex governs ten agent-run functions, coordinated by shared institutional memory rather than a management chain — neither a pyramid nor a flat collective. For brevity this document calls the model the Federated Cortex and calls each function-unit a Domain (a Head agent + its team + an assigned Triumvirate + a Charter that bounds its authority). The name is a proposal; the substance is FTLAB's own framework.

3.1 The direction of travel, and the challenge — clearly marked as such

Here is where the book and Block belong: as where FTLAB is heading, and as a challenge to keep the design honest — not as the design's foundation.

  • The direction (from AI-Born, treated as thesis not fact). Over time, more of each function's work — and eventually much of its coordination and even its orchestration — moves from humans to agents, so that the human team shrinks toward the smallest core that can hold judgment, values, and accountability. FTLAB intends to be the first demonstration of this. This is an aspiration FTLAB is testing in action, not a validated operating manual, and this document treats it accordingly. [Direction]
  • The challenge (from Block, used to sharpen the design). Block's useful contribution is one distinction: hierarchy historically did two jobs — routing information and locating accountability/judgment — and AI can now do the first (a shared world model routes what managers used to route) while the second stays human. This is worth adopting because it is also the answer to FTLAB's own open question — "how does governance scale across a portfolio without creating the hierarchical bureaucracy the thesis seeks to transcend?" Automate the routing (Function 2's institutional memory); keep accountability and judgment in the human Cortex. [Direction/Synthesis]
  • The caution (evidence, so we do not over-believe the direction). The flat-organisation experiments that ran long enough to judge (Zappos, GitHub, others) mostly reverted, because removing legible authority just creates illegible authority. And recent attempts to over-automate judgment (Klarna's customer-service reversal) had to bring humans back for empathy and complex cases. The lesson is not "don't devolve" but "devolve the routing, keep accountability legible, and keep humans where judgment and trust live." FTLAB's structural advantage is that its human team is small enough (single digits to low tens) to sit in the size range where legible coordination reliably works — so it should claim that, not bet on holding flatness at thousands of people. [Evidence]

The design that follows is therefore framework-grounded in the present and book-directional about the future, with the future treated as a trajectory to be earned, not assumed.

4. The ten Domains, in full

Each Domain is specified as: the FTLAB tasks it owns; its human-role equivalent; how its agent team is shaped; its Triumvirate governance; a sketch of its Charter (what it may decide alone / what it must escalate / what it may never do); and where it sits today versus its direction of travel. Charter authority is the practical meaning of "fully empowered with devolved agency": the envelope widens over time, never the forbidden line.

Domain 1 — Research & Intelligence

Owns: frontier monitoring, deep investigation, synthesis into frameworks, thesis validation, the research agenda. Human-equivalent: Chief Research Officer. Team: a lead research agent orchestrating scanning, investigation, and synthesis sub-agents (today: research-director + research-scout + synthesis-agent + strategy-analyst). Triumvirate: Intent-Setter human (sets the agenda and what evidence counts); Architect designs the research system; Guardian = Function 4. Charter: autonomous — scan, gather, draft, propose lines of inquiry; escalate — admitting a finding as validated, changing the agenda, any claim that touches the thesis; never — publish externally without the rigour gate. Today→direction: already substantially agent-run (FTLAB runs agents across research now); the first and furthest-devolved Domain. [Framework/Founder]

Domain 2 — Knowledge & Institutional Memory (the keystone)

Owns: the shared, machine-readable institutional memory; framework architecture and currency; the glossary and decision logs; the substrate through which all other Domains coordinate. Human-equivalent: Chief Knowledge Officer. Team: knowledge-manager, framework-architect, research-architect, technical-writer, consolidated. Triumvirate: Architect-led (this Domain is the coordination architecture). Charter: autonomous — capture, organise, surface, version knowledge; escalate — resolving genuine conflicts between authoritative documents, changing a framework's core definitions; never — alter the recorded provenance/history. Today→direction: partially exists as documentation; must become a continuous memory that coordinates the institution. This is the Domain to build first — coordinating ten agent-run functions through shared memory is impossible if the memory is thin. [Synthesis/Framework]

Domain 3 — Venture Architecture & Building

Owns: venture hypotheses; plane-by-plane venture design; prototyping/incubation; building and operating ventures through the pipeline; standing up each venture's own cortex. Human-equivalent: Chief Venture Officer. Team: the venture-architect (design) plus a new builder/operator capability plus per-venture incubation cells. Triumvirate: Intent-Setter human (which ventures, on what thesis); Architect designs venture operating models; Guardian watches stewardship-coherence. Charter: autonomous — design work, prototype experiments within an allocated window and budget; escalate — capital commitment, stage-gate promotion (Prototype→Named→Growth), external partner terms; never — launch a venture that could succeed as a traditional enterprise (out of thesis) or that externalises costs. Today→direction: the biggest gap — design exists, build/operate does not; this is where the studio meets its ventures (Part 6). [Framework/Synthesis]

Domain 4 — Quality, Rigour & Guardianship

Owns: one accountable rigour gate (replacing today's seven independent publication vetoes); methodology, critique, citation, empirical validation, agent-output validation; alignment-debt tracking; the institutional Guardian function. Human-equivalent: Chief Quality Officer. Team: critical-reviewer, qa-engineer, data-engineer. Triumvirate: holds the Guardian role institution-wide; must be structurally independent of profit-and-loss. Charter: autonomous — review, sample, audit, flag, and halt outputs/agents that breach values or quality thresholds; escalate — values conflicts it cannot resolve; never — be overridden on a values breach by a revenue consideration. Today→direction: consolidate the diffuse veto into one owner now; among the last functions to devolve, because oversight is the human accountability floor. [Framework/Evidence]

Domain 5 — Editorial, Education & Communication

Owns: voice and publication quality; educational translation; distribution; investor/stakeholder translation. Human-equivalent: Chief Communications Officer. Team: editorial-director, creative-director, education-designer, content-distribution-manager, investor-relations (as a translation sub-function). Triumvirate: Intent-Setter human (what FTLAB says publicly and why); Guardian = Function 4 on claim-accuracy. Charter: autonomous — produce, adapt, schedule, distribute within voice and thesis guidelines; escalate — external positions on contested matters, anything implicating a partner or the thesis; never — publish unreviewed claims or violate the open/proprietary IP boundary. Today→direction: well-developed already; early-to-mid devolution. [Framework]

Domain 6 — Governance, Legal & Compliance

Owns: AI governance for FTLAB's own agents; IP architecture (open/licensed/proprietary tiers); DIFC/DFSA and data-protection compliance; the institutional integrity layer. Human-equivalent: General Counsel / Chief Risk. Team: compliance-officer, legal-counsel, plus the agent-governance function. Triumvirate: holds an Architect-of-governance and a Guardian role. Charter: autonomous — monitor compliance, maintain registers, draft policy; escalate — any regulatory ambiguity (regardless of confidence), IP-ownership decisions, liability exposure; never — conceal information from a partner against their wishes (the framework's stated non-negotiable). Today→direction: exists; devolves late (regulatory accountability is human-bound). [Framework]

Domain 7 — Capital & Operations

Owns: finance, runway, treasury; resource allocation across the temporal horizons; studio operations; the principle that research is always funded. Human-equivalent: COO / CFO. Team: new finance/ops agents + the operational parts of today's chief-of-staff. Triumvirate: Intent-Setter human (allocation reflects strategy, not opportunism). Charter: autonomous — track, forecast, report, run routine operations within budget; escalate — capital allocation decisions, reallocation across horizons, anything affecting runway materially; never — starve the always-funded research allocation; never commit capital outside thesis. Today→direction: a genuine gap; mid devolution. [Framework/Synthesis]

Domain 8 — Network & Ecosystem

Owns: sovereign engagement and consulting relationships; institutional and academic partnerships; the intellectual-commons community; convenings. Human-equivalent: Chief Relationships Officer. Team: founder-partner, partnership-development, community-manager, event-coordinator. Triumvirate: Intent-Setter human; this Domain is the most human-anchored of all. Charter: autonomous — research relationships, prepare engagements, run community operations, organise convenings; escalate — entering or shaping a sovereign relationship, partnership terms, anything implicating national-strategy alignment; never — subordinate FTLAB's intellectual independence to a partner, or make a binding relational commitment without a human. Today→direction: exists but fragmented; devolves last — trust and sovereign relationships are where the human edge is most irreplaceable. [Framework/Evidence]

Domain 9 — Platform & Agentic Systems (the brief's "Head of Agentic Systems")

Owns: the technical stack (the framework's Five Planes: Data, Model, Agent, Orchestration, Actuation); the agent-orchestration infrastructure; the VP-Agent platform FTLAB licenses; MLOps; security. Human-equivalent: CTO. Team: a platform/architecture lead orchestrating engineering and infrastructure sub-agents, with the agent-orchestrator demoted from a peer "coordinator" to a platform capability here. Triumvirate: Architect-led (this Domain builds the system the others run on). Charter: autonomous — build, deploy, monitor, and maintain platform and agents within spec; escalate — architectural changes that alter other Domains' authority, security incidents, new agent-role creation; never — deploy an agent without a Charter, or change Values-level constraints. Today→direction: the missing "Platform Architect," reinstated; early-to-mid devolution. This is the brief's own example — "Head of the agentic systems, with engineers underneath" — rendered as a lead platform agent orchestrating an engineering swarm under a human Architect. [Synthesis/Framework]

Domain 10 — People, Culture & Apprenticeship

Owns: the human Cortex's composition and health; human-agent collaboration protocols; culture and rituals (there are "no hallways" at this ratio, so culture must be designed); and — critically — the apprenticeship pipeline that grows the institution's next humans. Human-equivalent: Chief People Officer. Team: people-operations, vibe-director, plus a new apprenticeship function. Triumvirate: Intent-Setter human. Charter: autonomous — run culture operations, onboarding, collaboration tooling, well-being monitoring; escalate — cortex composition changes, succession, anything affecting a human's role; never — be automated away (this Domain produces the humans the model depends on). Today→direction: exists for culture; the apprenticeship mandate is new and important — an institution of "seniors plus agents" with no way to grow new seniors mortgages its own future, which for a studio seeding ventures with human cortexes is existential. [Synthesis/Evidence]

5. Direction of travel: how authority devolves over time

This is where FTLAB is heading — stated as a trajectory FTLAB is testing, consistent with the framework's allowance that FTLAB itself "could evolve beyond being an institution into something more like an autonomous methodology or framework." It is deliberately humble: a direction, paced by evidence, not a schedule. [Direction/Founder]

The mechanism. Devolution happens by widening each Domain's Charter — moving decisions from "escalate to a human" toward "autonomous," on evidence of verified performance, never touching the "never" line. In parallel, the human role in each Domain shifts from doing the work, to directing it, to only setting its intent and judging what it escalates.

The sequence. Devolve where the work is highest-volume and lowest-stakes-per-action first, and keep human-anchored where trust, novelty, ethics, and accountability live:

  • First / furthest: Research & Intelligence (1), Knowledge (2) — already agent-run, high artifact density, low per-action stakes.
  • Early–mid: Platform (9), Editorial (5), Capital/Ops routine work (7).
  • Mid: Venture building (3).
  • Last / never fully: Quality & Guardianship (4), Governance & Compliance (6), Network & sovereign relationships (8) — and the human accountability floor below.

The permanent floor. "Fully autonomous" can become true of doing and coordinating; it cannot become true of accountability. The reason is legitimacy, not capability: a regulator, a sovereign partner, or a court needs a nameable, answerable human — and values must be authored by humans even when implemented by agents. So FTLAB's end-state is best described not as "no humans" but as the smallest human Cortex that can hold legitimacy, novel/ethical judgment, values authorship, and trust relationships — governing a near-fully-autonomous agent institution. That is the founder-sanctioned "autonomous methodology with human stewardship at its core," made precise. [Direction/Framework]

How we will know it is working. The framework already supplies the right gauges (its §5.2 diagnostic metrics): Cognitive Overhead (the share of human attention spent on operations versus judgment — the core devolution KPI; if it rises, FTLAB is drifting back toward a conventional structure), Alignment Debt (the gap between stated values and actual agent behaviour — to be tracked and managed, not pretended to zero), and Iteration Half-Life (the speed of a full learning cycle — lengthening signals calcification). [Framework]

6. Two tiers: the studio and its ventures

FTLAB is a studio and a portfolio. The founders are explicit that venture teams are not FTLAB headcount — each venture has "its own small human core." So the architecture has two tiers built from the same Domain unit:

  1. The studio (FTLAB): the ten Domains above, under the founder Cortex.
  2. Each venture: spun out by Domain 3 with its own small human cortex (its own Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian) and its own agent population, sized by that sector's needs — tighter human oversight in regulated domains (e.g., Adaptic in finance), larger agent populations in high-autonomy domains.

The studio does not manage its ventures through a chain; it governs them through the shared institutional memory and one repeated question — who holds Architect, Intent-Setter, and Guardian here, and are they in tension? — applied to each venture exactly as to each studio Domain. This is how governance scales across a growing portfolio without rebuilding bureaucracy: the pattern repeats (federates), the chain does not lengthen. A full, reusable venture-org template specifying this second tier is a companion to this document.

7. Contradictions to resolve, and decisions for the founders

Contradictions in the current corpus that should be settled (so the org and framework stay coherent):

  • The ratio. "5:100," "1:500," and the book's "1:100–1:10,000" all appear. Recommendation: stop treating any fixed number as load-bearing; size each Domain's agent population to its function and sector, holding the human team small (single digits to low tens). [Synthesis]
  • "Five Planes" used two ways (a technical stack: Data/Model/Agent/Orchestration/Actuation; versus org-layers). Recommendation: keep "Five Planes" for the technical stack only (its canonical meaning); call the organisational units Domains.
  • Registry hygiene. Reconcile the phantom "Deal Scout" and "Platform Architect" references before elevating anything.
  • Titles. Business plan says Co-CEO/Co-CEO; founder responses say Managing Partner / General Partner. Recommendation: Managing/General Partner internally (the model governs, it does not command); CEO-equivalents only for external legibility.

Decisions only the founders can make:

  1. Where the Guardian sits. The model requires the institutional Guardian (Domain 4) to be independent of profit-and-loss; today Shayan holds Guardian-type and operating roles together. This is the most consequential near-term human decision.
  2. Pace of devolution — the risk appetite for widening Charters, especially in Venture-building and Capital.
  3. Confirm the build order: Knowledge & Institutional Memory (Domain 2) first.
  4. Confirm scope — this designs the studio and points to the venture template; confirm you want the venture template specified to equal depth.
  5. Naming — "Federated Cortex" and "Domain" are proposals; cheap to change.

8. Status and next steps

This is v0.2, regrounded in FTLAB's framework and activities, with the book and Block held as direction rather than specification. The companion deliverables in progress are (a) the reusable venture-org template (Part 6's second tier) and (b) an independent adversarial pressure-test of this model. The immediate build recommendations remain: stand up the institutional memory (Domain 2); consolidate the rigour gate and settle Guardian independence (Domain 4); reconcile the corpus contradictions into the next version of the conceptual framework so architecture and framework stay coherent.

Held, as the framework asks, with conviction but not rigidity — written to be tested in action and revised by what reality teaches.
