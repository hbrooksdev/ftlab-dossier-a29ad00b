Option 1 · the model

Seven Domains

A legible department model: seven AI Domain-heads under a thin human Cortex. The determination keeps it only as the human-legible face — not as the destination.

Option 117 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-organisational-architecture-v0.3.md
In brief

Seven AI “Domain” head-agents, each owning a function under a single-owner charter, governed by a small human Cortex holding the New Triumvirate (Architect / Intent-Setter / Guardian). Regulator-legible, deployable, and the origin of the familiar “faces.”

Re-graded by the determination: with build-time no longer an objection, Option 1’s one advantage (time-to-value) disappears and it is strictly dominated by Option 3. It survives as the accountability face — and the work says that face is ~3 Engines, not seven (see “How Many Roles?”).

Key takeaways
  • Every function has exactly one accountable owner — a charter a regulator reads in one sentence.
  • Guardian = the founders jointly for now; externalised to a board as the team grows (the inherited Critical).
  • Not a destination: the department model rebuilds the routing job AI dissolves — “governance theater” on an AI-enabled skeleton.
  • The seven over-built the count; derived from the work it is ~3 Engines over a Core and an Edge.

This version sets FTLAB's organisational design as decided with the founders: seven functional Domains for now, each one an AI agent, governed by a small human Cortex — with a tenth-role end-state documented for later. It folds in the corrections from the independent pressure-test: the institutional memory becomes a standalone Domain rather than a shared responsibility; coordination runs through a routine-coordination agent with a human Guardian holding halt-rights; devolution is sequenced by systemic stakes; and the language about where authority sits is made honest. The book and external models remain direction of travel*, not specification.*

0. Read this first: who is an agent, and who is a human

This is the single most important thing to hold while reading, and the prior drafts blurred it:

Every Domain below — every "Head" and every member of its team — is an AI agent. They are not people. The only humans in FTLAB are the Cortex: the founders and a few key individuals who govern the agent Domains through three functions (Architect, Intent-Setter, Guardian). Humans do not sit in the Domains; they set the Domains' intent, bound their authority, and judge what they escalate.

Two consequences follow, and they shape every decision in this document:

  • The "human-org analogue" labels (CRO, CTO, COO, and so on) are analogies for mapping only. They tell you what kind of work a Domain owns by pointing at the nearest familiar human role. They do not mean a person holds that title. The Chief Research Officer "seat" is an agent. When this document says "the human Guardian," it means a human in the Cortex who governs the Quality & Guardianship agent Domain and can halt it — not a human who runs it.
  • Because Heads are agents, every role must be a clean, single-owner Charter. Human organisations tolerate matrixed, shared, negotiated ownership; agents cannot — a Charter has to be explicit and must compile. This is why, when the institutional memory could not be cleanly owned by two Domains at once, it became its own Domain. Across the whole design we favour one accountable owner per function over human-style matrixing.

Claims are marked [Framework] (FTLAB's framework/business plan), [Founder] (founder decisions, including this session's), [Direction] (from AI-Born/external, treated as aspiration), [Synthesis] (my proposal), [Evidence] (external evidence used as a check).

1. Start from the work: what FTLAB has to do

The organisation is derived from FTLAB's actual activities, not from a theory of org charts. FTLAB is a thesis-driven venture architect that researches, designs, builds, and scales AI-born ventures, and intends to demonstrate its own thesis by running as a small human team coordinating a large agentic workforce. [Framework] Its work is four activity streams plus education plus running itself:

ActivityThe concrete tasks it requires
Research & DevelopmentMonitor the frontier; investigate; synthesise findings into frameworks; validate the thesis; maintain rigour; publish.
Venture Architecture & BuildingTurn research into venture hypotheses; design ventures plane-by-plane; prototype, build, and operate them through the pipeline; stand up each venture's own cortex.
Consulting & AdvisoryEngage sovereign/institutional partners; design AI-transformation for them — always tied to a venture or investment, never standalone. (A mode of venture-building + relationships, not its own function.) [Framework]
IP LicensingProductise proven frameworks/methodologies; license them; build the platform that carries them; capture licensee feedback.
Education & Thought LeadershipExecutive education; accessible translations; convenings; dissemination.
Running the institutionCapital, runway, allocation; governance, legal, DIFC/DFSA compliance; institutional memory; people and culture; sovereign and partner relationships; the platform everything runs on.

The governing constraint, taken from FTLAB's own framework: the scarcest resource is human attention, not capital. The organisation must spend human attention only where it is irreplaceable — judgment, intent, values, relationships — and move everything else to the agentic workforce as fast as it can be done safely. That single principle generates the structure below. [Framework]

2. The operating principle: agents do the work, the Cortex governs

FTLAB's own framework supplies the design rules; the book supplies only the long-range direction.

Machine Core + Human Cortex. Autonomous systems execute; a small human team provides judgment, intent, interpretation, and values. So each function is run by agents (the Core) and governed by humans (the Cortex). A "Head of Function" is therefore a lead agent that owns and orchestrates that function, not a human manager. [Framework]

The New Triumvirate is how the Cortex governs each agent Domain — three functions, each a clear accountability:

  • Architect — designs how the Domain works (its agents, its data, its interfaces).
  • Intent-Setter — defines what the Domain is for; tunes its priorities and preferences.
  • Guardian — keeps the Domain within its values; holds the power to halt, redirect, or constrain it.

These are held by humans now, devolve partly to agents over time (under verification), and never fully leave human hands at the top (Part 6). For every Domain it must be answerable in one sentence who performs each of the three. [Framework]

Coordination runs through the memory Domain, not a management chain. This is the part the pressure-test rightly demanded be engineered rather than asserted, so it is now a Domain of its own (D2): the institutional memory, plus a routine-coordination agent ("the integrator") that reads and writes that memory to route work, sequence handoffs, and surface conflicts across the other Domains. The integrator is chartered to route and surface — never to make values-level or contested cross-Domain judgments, which escalate to the human Guardian. [Founder/Synthesis]

Authority is held honestly. Earlier language ("radiates, does not descend") is dropped as rhetoric. The plain statement: the Cortex holds bounded authority over the agent Domains — it sets intent, bounds the Charters, and can halt. That authority is real; calling it something softer would obscure the accountability the whole design exists to protect. [Synthesis, correcting v0.2]

2.1 Direction, challenge, caution — clearly marked

  • Direction (from AI-Born, a thesis FTLAB is testing, not a fact). Over time more of each Domain's work, coordination, and orchestration moves from humans to agents, shrinking the human Cortex toward the smallest core that can hold judgment, values, and accountability. [Direction]
  • Challenge (from Block, used to sharpen). Hierarchy did two jobs — routing information and locating accountability/judgment. AI now does the first (the memory Domain), so the human Cortex keeps only the second. This is also the answer to FTLAB's own open question — governing a portfolio without rebuilding bureaucracy. [Direction/Synthesis]
  • Caution (evidence, so the direction is not over-believed). Flat-org experiments mostly reverted because removing legible authority just hides it; over-automating judgment (Klarna) had to be reversed. The lesson: devolve the routing, keep accountability legible and human, keep humans where judgment and trust live. FTLAB's advantage is that its human team is small enough to sit where legible coordination reliably works. [Evidence]

3. The seven Domains (each Head is an AI agent)

Each Domain is specified as: what it owns; its human-org analogue (a label only — the seat is an agent); the shape of its agent team; which Triumvirate governance functions apply and who (human) holds them now; a Charter sketch (decides alone / must escalate / may never); and its build/devolve timing. The Charter is the practical meaning of "fully empowered with devolved agency" — the envelope widens on evidence; the "never" line does not move.

D1 — Research & Intelligence

Owns: frontier monitoring, deep investigation, synthesis into frameworks, thesis validation, the research agenda. Analogue: Chief Research Officer (the seat is an agent). Team: a lead research agent orchestrating scanning, investigation, and synthesis sub-agents. Governance: human Intent-Setter sets the agenda and what evidence counts; Architect (human→agent) designs the research system; Guardian = D5. Charter: alone — scan, gather, draft, propose lines of inquiry; escalate — admitting a finding as validated, changing the agenda, anything touching the thesis; never — publish externally without the D5 rigour gate. Timing: already largely agent-run; among the first and furthest devolved. [Framework/Founder]

D2 — Knowledge, Memory & Coordination ⭐ (the keystone — standalone by decision)

Owns: the institutional memory end to end — both its content (taxonomy, framework architecture, glossary, decision logs, provenance, conflict adjudication) and its system (capture pipelines, retrieval, freshness, anti-gaming, access) — and the routine-coordination agent (the integrator) that coordinates the other Domains through that memory. Analogue: Chief Knowledge Officer. Team: memory/curation agents + the integrator + retrieval/quality agents. Governance: human Architect-led (this Domain is the coordination architecture); a named human adjudicator for genuine knowledge conflicts (escalating values/strategy conflicts to the Cortex). Charter: alone — capture, organise, surface, route, sequence, flag conflicts; escalate — resolving conflicts between authoritative documents, any values/strategy conflict, any contested cross-Domain routing decision; never — alter recorded provenance/history, or make a values-level call. Timing: built first, devolved last. It is the substrate everything depends on, so it keeps human oversight longest. Its first real job: resolve FTLAB's own contradictions (the ratio stated three ways; "Five Planes" defined two ways) — if the memory cannot keep the institution's own terms straight, it cannot coordinate anything. [Founder/Synthesis/Evidence]

D3 — Venture Building

Owns: venture hypotheses; plane-by-plane venture design; prototyping/incubation; building and operating ventures through the pipeline (Research Thesis → Prototype → Named → Growth); standing up each venture's own cortex. Analogue: Chief Venture Officer. Team: venture-design agents + builder/operator agents + per-venture incubation cells. Governance: human Intent-Setter (which ventures, on what thesis); Architect designs venture operating models; Guardian (D5) watches stewardship-coherence. Charter: alone — design, prototype within an allocated window and budget; escalate — capital commitment, stage-gate promotion, external partner terms; never — launch a venture that could succeed as a traditional enterprise (off-thesis) or that externalises costs. Timing: mid; this is where the studio meets its ventures (Part 7). [Framework/Synthesis]

D4 — Platform & Agentic Systems (the brief's "Head of Agentic Systems")

Owns: the technical stack (the framework's Five Planes: Data, Model, Agent, Orchestration, Actuation); the agent infrastructure every Domain runs on; security; the platform FTLAB licenses. Analogue: CTO. Team: a lead platform agent orchestrating engineering, infrastructure, and security sub-agents. Governance: human Architect-led. Charter: alone — build, deploy, monitor, maintain within spec; escalate — architectural changes that alter another Domain's authority, security incidents, creating a new agent role; never — deploy an agent without a Charter, or change a values-level constraint. Timing: early-to-mid. This is the brief's example — a lead platform agent orchestrating an engineering swarm, governed by a human Architect. Note: D4 builds the orchestration infrastructure; D2's integrator runs on it. [Synthesis/Framework]

D5 — Quality & Guardianship

Owns: one accountable rigour gate (replacing the seven independent vetoes in the current draft); methodology, critique, citation, empirical and agent-output validation; AI governance, IP, DIFC/DFSA compliance; Alignment-Debt tracking; and the institutional Guardian function. Analogue: Chief Quality & Risk Officer. Team: review, audit, compliance, and validation agents. Governance: this is the Domain the human Guardian governs directly, with halt-rights over any Domain that breaches values — and it must be independent of profit-and-loss (Part 4 on how, at FTLAB's scale). Charter: alone — review, sample, audit, flag, and halt outputs/agents that breach values or quality thresholds; escalate — values conflicts it cannot resolve; never — be overridden on a values breach by a revenue consideration. Timing: among the last to devolve — oversight is the human accountability floor. [Framework/Evidence]

D6 — Communication & Ecosystem

Owns: everything outward — voice, publication, education, distribution, investor/stakeholder translation; and sovereign engagement, institutional and academic partnerships, the intellectual-commons community, and convenings. Analogue: Chief External Officer. Team: editorial/creative/education/distribution agents + relationship and community agents. Governance: human Intent-Setter (what FTLAB says and to whom); Guardian (D5) on claim-accuracy and partner-integrity. Charter: alone — produce, adapt, distribute within voice and thesis guidelines; research relationships; run community and convenings; escalate — external positions on contested matters, entering/shaping a sovereign relationship, partnership terms; never — publish unreviewed claims, breach the open/proprietary IP boundary, subordinate FTLAB's intellectual independence to a partner, or make a binding relational commitment without a human. Timing: publication/education early-to-mid; sovereign relationships devolve last — trust is the most human-anchored function. [Framework/Evidence]

D7 — Capital, People & Operations

Owns: running the house — finance, runway, treasury, resource allocation across horizons (including the non-negotiable "research is always funded"); studio operations; the human team's composition, culture, and well-being; and the apprenticeship pipeline that grows FTLAB's next humans. Analogue: COO. Team: finance/ops agents + people/culture agents + an apprenticeship function. Governance: human Intent-Setter (allocation reflects strategy, not opportunism; cortex composition is a human call). Charter: alone — track, forecast, report, run routine operations within budget; run culture operations and onboarding; escalate — capital allocation, reallocation across horizons, anything affecting runway materially, any change to the human cortex or a person's role; never — starve the research allocation, commit capital off-thesis, or automate away the apprenticeship/cortex-composition function. Timing: finance/ops routine work mid; people/apprenticeship never fully devolves — it produces the humans the model depends on. [Synthesis/Framework/Evidence]

4. The human Cortex — who governs the agents

The Cortex is the only human part of FTLAB. Mapping onto the founders and the business plan's near-term hires (these humans hold governance functions over the agent Domains, not seats inside them):

  • Shayan Kargarian — Managing Partner — lead Intent-Setter. Sets institutional intent and charters across Domains; structured-finance and governance depth (natural for D3 and D7 governance). [Founder]
  • Mehran Granfar — General Partner — Intent-Setter (thesis) + initial Architect. Owns the thesis and the Values the agents are bounded by, and the initial design of D1/D2/D4. [Founder]
  • The human Guardian (governs D5) — held by the founders for now (decided). The Guardian function is held jointly by Shayan and Mehran in the interim: for any given decision, the founder not driving it acts as Guardian over it — a minimal mutual check — under a written conflict-of-interest protocol. This does not fully satisfy the profit-and-loss independence the model ideally wants, so the gap is logged as a known risk and tracked as Alignment Debt, with the planned move — once the team grows — being to externalise the halt-right to an independent director or board-level reviewer with a real veto. Holding it with the founders now is the honest, workable answer at a 3–5-person scale; claiming full independence would not be. [Founder decision]
  • The Architect hire (CTO-analogue human, the business plan's Q2 CTO / Principal Solutions Architect). Governs D4 and the technical design of D2; the human most able to devolve to an agent Architect over time.

That is a 3–5 human Cortex governing seven agent Domains — consistent with the business plan's Year-1/Year-2 numbers, reframed: these people are not a management chain; they hold Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian over the agents.

Concentration risk is the dominant risk of so small a Cortex — losing one Intent-Setter or Architect matters more than losing one of forty employees. Mitigations are design requirements: documented intent and decisions (the D2 memory doubles as succession insurance), more than one human able to hold each function, the D7 apprenticeship pipeline as a succession path, and the founders' stated intent to grow governance capacity (via agents and apprentices), not headcount. [Framework/Evidence]

5. Coordination and governance mechanics

  • The integrator (in D2), chartered tightly. A routine-coordination agent routes work, sequences handoffs, keeps the memory current, and surfaces conflicts. It does not resolve contested cross-Domain or values-level questions — those escalate to the human Guardian. This is how ten-plus agent teams coordinate without a management chain, while staying safe given that the coordinator is itself an agent. [Founder/Synthesis]
  • The memory engineered as a system, not assumed. D2 carries a freshness/provenance/access standard, write-discipline, and anti-gaming — because coordination-through-memory fails silently if the memory is stale, gamed, or contested. [Evidence]
  • Governance by altitude of change. Operational changes: fast, agent-autonomous. Structural changes: more evidence, slower. Foundational changes (thesis, values): most evidence, human-reserved. This decides what an agent may do alone versus what escalates. [Framework]
  • Escalation triggers (any one sends a decision to a human): irreversibility, novelty (outside trained domain), material stakeholder impact, an unresolved values conflict, and regulatory ambiguity (regardless of the agent's confidence). [Direction/Framework]
  • Floor-integrity check. To stop the human floor decaying into rubber-stamping: D5 samples escalations a human "approved" to confirm a human actually judged rather than signed; rising Cognitive Overhead or Alignment Debt triggers a mandatory slowdown of further devolution. [Synthesis/Evidence]
  • The diagnostics (from the framework's own §5.2): Cognitive Overhead (human attention on operations vs judgment — the core devolution KPI; if it rises, FTLAB is drifting back to a conventional structure), Alignment Debt (stated values vs actual agent behaviour — tracked, not pretended to zero), Iteration Half-Life (learning-cycle speed; lengthening signals calcification). [Framework]

6. Direction of travel: how authority devolves

Stated as a trajectory FTLAB is testing, consistent with the framework's allowance that FTLAB could "evolve beyond being an institution into something more like an autonomous methodology or framework." [Direction/Founder]

Mechanism: widen each Domain's Charter — moving decisions from "escalate" toward "alone," on evidence of verified performance, never touching "never." In parallel the human role shifts from doing → directing → only setting intent and judging escalations.

Sequence — by systemic stakes, not per-action stakes (correcting v0.2, which had the keystone devolving first):

  • First / furthest: Research & Intelligence (D1) — already agent-run, high artifact density, low systemic risk.
  • Early–mid: Platform build (D4), publication/education (D6), routine finance/ops (D7).
  • Mid: Venture building (D3).
  • Last / never fully: Knowledge, Memory & Coordination (D2) — everything depends on it; Quality & Guardianship (D5) — the oversight floor; sovereign relationships (D6) — trust; and cortex/apprenticeship (D7) — it makes the humans.

The permanent floor. "Fully autonomous" can become true of doing and coordinating. It cannot become true of accountability — because legitimacy, not capability, is the limit: a regulator, sovereign, or court needs a nameable, answerable human, and values must be authored by humans even when implemented by agents. FTLAB's end-state is therefore the smallest human Cortex that can hold legitimacy, novel/ethical judgment, values authorship, and trust relationships — governing a near-fully-autonomous agent institution. That is the founder-sanctioned "autonomous methodology with human stewardship at its core," made precise. [Direction/Framework]

7. Two tiers: the studio and its ventures

FTLAB is a studio and a portfolio, and venture teams are not FTLAB headcount — each venture has its own small human cortex. The architecture is two tiers built from the same unit (a Domain = a Head agent + agent team + assigned human Triumvirate + Charter):

  1. The studio (FTLAB): the seven Domains above, under the founder Cortex.
  2. Each venture: spun out by D3 with its own small human cortex and its own agent population, sized by sector (tighter human oversight in regulated domains such as Adaptic; larger agent populations in high-autonomy ones).

The studio governs ventures not through a chain but through the shared memory (D2) and one repeated question — who holds Architect, Intent-Setter, and Guardian here, and are they in tension? — applied to each venture as to each studio Domain. Governance scales by the pattern repeating (federating), not the chain lengthening. The reusable venture template is specified in the companion document ftlab-venture-org-template-v0.1.md. [Framework/Synthesis]

8. The ten-Domain end-state (documented for later)

The seven Domains are the structure for now. As the agent workforce matures and the portfolio grows, several merged Domains can federate back into the fuller set — the documented target to grow toward, not to build today:

Now (7)Federates later into (10)
D1 Research & IntelligenceResearch & Intelligence
D2 Knowledge, Memory & CoordinationKnowledge & Institutional Memory (coordination remains here)
D3 Venture BuildingVenture Architecture & Building
D4 Platform & Agentic SystemsPlatform & Agentic Systems
D5 Quality & Guardianship→ splits into Quality & Rigour + Governance, Legal & Compliance
D6 Communication & Ecosystem→ splits into Editorial, Education & Communication + Network & Ecosystem
D7 Capital, People & Operations→ splits into Capital & Operations + People, Culture & Apprenticeship

The trigger to split any Domain is evidence that one human governor can no longer hold its Triumvirate without Cognitive Overhead rising — i.e., the org federates when, and only when, attention load demands it. [Synthesis]

9. Contradictions to resolve, and decisions for the founders

Contradictions for D2's first job to settle (so the org and framework stay coherent): the ratio (stated as 5:100, 1:500, and 1:100–10,000 — recommend retiring any fixed number and sizing each Domain's agent population to its function/sector, holding the human team small); "Five Planes" used two ways (keep it for the technical stack only; the organisational units are Domains); and the phantom "Deal Scout"/"Platform Architect" registry references (reconcile before elevating agents). Titles: recommend Managing/General Partner internally over Co-CEO.

Decisions only the founders can make:

  1. The Guardian's independence (Part 4) — DECIDED: the founders hold the Guardian function jointly for now, under a logged conflict-of-interest protocol, with externalisation to a board/independent reviewer planned as the team grows. (Open sub-decision for later: who the external Guardian becomes.)
  2. Pace of devolution — the risk appetite for widening Charters, especially D3 and D7.
  3. Confirm naming — "Domain," "the integrator," and the seven-Domain set are proposals; cheap to change.

10. Status and next steps

This is v0.3, the decided structure: seven agent Domains under a human Cortex, with the institutional memory as a standalone keystone, an agent integrator with a human Guardian, devolution sequenced by systemic stakes, and the ten-Domain end-state documented for later. The companion documents are the venture-org template (ftlab-venture-org-template-v0.1.md) and the independent pressure-test (ftlab-org-architecture-pressure-test-v0.1.md). Immediate build recommendations: stand up D2's memory substrate first (and use it to resolve the corpus contradictions); consolidate D5's rigour gate and settle Guardian independence; reconcile the contradictions into the next conceptual-framework version so architecture and framework stay coherent.

Held, as the framework asks, with conviction but not rigidity — written to be tested in action and revised by what reality teaches.
