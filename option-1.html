Option 1 · re-run from the work (v0.4)

Named Accountability

Option 1 re-run with the same method as the others — derived from the relevant Book 1 chapters + FTLAB’s actual work. It converges on ~3 value-creating Engines, and shows the department primitive’s tendency to name five is its own flaw.

Option 110 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-organisational-architecture-v0.4.md
In brief

The named-accountability / department model, re-derived. Research + Venture + Consulting collapse to one Venture Engine (“the ventures are the research”); the seven seats reduce to ~3 value-creating Engines — Venture Engine, Platform & Agentic Systems, Knowledge & Ecosystem.

As a pure department model it is forced to name two more — Coordination & Memory and Quality & Guardianship — because it has no engineered Core or separated Edge to hold them. That +2 is the over-construction: those two are exactly what Option 2/3 turn into the Core and the Edge.

Key takeaways
  • Converges on the determination’s ~3 Engines — fixing the “why seven?” over-count.
  • The +2 (Coordination & Memory; Quality & Guardianship) is the diagnostic flaw: a department model must name the Core and the Edge as departments.
  • Guardian carries no P&L (Ch 9): capital and the halt-right must not fuse in the 2-person Edge — externalise the halt-right first.
  • Run honestly, Option 1 is ~3 Engines wearing department labels — the face, not the destination.

0. What changed, and why this re-run exists

v0.3 specified seven Domains. That number was carried over from the framework's 28-agent inventory and never re-derived from FTLAB's actual activities. This re-run fixes that: it keeps Option 1's primitive — a legible model of named accountability, where each function is an AI Head under a single-owner charter, governed by a small human Cortex — but derives the seats from the work rather than from theory.

Run from the work, Option 1 converges on the same ~3 value-creating Heads the determination locks (§10) — the Venture Engine, the Platform, and Knowledge & Ecosystem. As a pure department model it then names two more — Coordination & Memory and Quality & Guardianship — because, lacking an engineered Core and a separated Edge, it has nowhere else to put coordination and oversight. That is five named Heads in total, with Capital & Operations in the founder Edge. The honest finding is that the +2 is the over-construction itself: those two seats are exactly what Option 2/3 turn into the Core and the Edge. Even re-run cleanly, Option 1 demonstrates its own ceiling.

1. Method: chapters, work, framework

Relevant Book 1 chapters, for Option 1's lens (the named-accountability/department model):

  • Ch 6 — Organisational Design as Code. The operative chapter: each function is a VP-Agent under a machine-readable charter with three decision tiers (autonomous / confirmation-required / human-escalation) and a hardConstraints block prior to reward. This is how a "Head" is specified.
  • Ch 9 — The New Triumvirate. The human Cortex that governs the Heads: Architect / Intent-Setter / Guardian, held in tension; the structural-independence prescription for the Guardian.
  • Ch 8 — Production Governance. How the Quality & Guardianship Head and the human Guardian actually run oversight at scale (evaluator-agents, statistical sampling, the weekly random audit).
  • Ch 4 — Anatomy of the AI-Born. Machine Core + Human Cortex; the Cognitive Overhead Index; the Cortex as a single point of failure.
  • Ch 3 — The Second Lineage Break. Why even a department model must stay lean: routing is the job AI dissolves, so named seats must localise accountability, never re-create a routing hierarchy.

Chapters Option 1 deliberately does not implement: Ch 5 (how the Machine Core runs) and Ch 10 (the Mothership). Option 1 carries coordination in a memory department, not an engineered Core, and does not adopt the platform-plus-ventures Mothership shape. That omission is precisely what distinguishes Option 1 (the face) from Option 2/3 (the destination).

The work (business plan + founder-responses-to-open-questions.md): four activities — R&D, venture incubation, transformation consulting, IP licensing — and the founders' stated intent that "the ventures are the research" (Q1, Q13) and "advisory is always tied to venture-building" (Q7).

The framework: the New Triumvirate (§2.3), the VP-Agent Model with Values as encoded constraints (§5.1), and the principle of single-owner charters ("an agent's charter must compile").

Five named Heads in total; the two beyond the three are precisely what Option 2/3 absorb into the Core and the Edge.

3. The five Heads (each an AI agent under a charter)

Each is specified per Book 1 Ch 6: a mandate, a human-org analogue (a label only — the seat is an agent), the agent-team shape, the three-tier charter (decides alone / must escalate / may never), which Triumvirate functions govern it and who holds them now, and build/devolve timing.

1 · The Venture Engine (analogue: Chief Venture Officer) Owns: the whole learning-in-action loop — originate theses, design ventures plane-by-plane, prototype, build, and operate them through the pipeline (Thesis → Prototype → Named → Growth), and run incumbent transformations as co-ventures. Team: research, venture-design, build, and operator sub-agents. Charter: alone — run research, draft theses, prototype, operate within a venture's charter; escalate — capital commitment, stage-gate promotion, external partner/venture terms, anything irreversible; never — commit FTLAB capital or sign a partner without the Edge. Governs: Intent-Setter-led (Shayan). Timing: first and central — it is FTLAB's methodology. Charter note: because one seat spans speculative research → live SPV operation → sovereign co-ventures, it carries life-stage sub-charters (Thesis / Prototype / Named / Growth), each with its own authority envelope, so the single-owner charter still compiles rather than becoming an unbounded grant.

2 · Platform & Agentic Systems (analogue: CTO — the brief's "Head of Agentic Systems") Owns: the AI-Native Enterprise Framework FTLAB licenses (~70% of revenue) and the agent infrastructure every Head runs on — orchestration, memory systems, integration, security. Team: a lead platform agent orchestrating engineering, infrastructure, and security sub-agents. Charter: alone — build, deploy, monitor within spec; escalate — architecture changes that alter another Head's authority, security incidents, creating a new agent role; never — deploy an agent without a charter, or change a values-level constraint. Governs: Architect-led (Mehran). Timing: early.

3 · Knowledge & Ecosystem (analogue: Chief Knowledge & Growth Officer) Owns: publications and executive education (credibility + lead-generation), and the GCC / sovereign / MNC / partner relationships and brand. Team: research-publication, education, content, and partnership sub-agents. Charter: alone — produce and distribute knowledge, run education, maintain relationships within mandate; escalate — public positioning on sensitive matters, partnership terms, sovereign commitments; never — mislead or conceal against a partner's wishes (the founders' non-negotiable, Q7). Governs: Intent-Setter-led, with the Edge holding origination. Timing: early — it is the lead engine for licensing and consulting demand.

4 · Coordination & Memory (analogue: Chief Knowledge Officer — the keystone) Owns: the institutional memory end-to-end (taxonomy, framework architecture, decision logs, provenance) and the routine-coordination agent ("the integrator") that routes work and sequences hand-offs across the other Heads. Charter: alone — capture, organise, surface, route, sequence, flag conflicts; escalate — resolving conflicts between authoritative documents, any values/strategy conflict, any contested cross-Head routing; never — alter recorded provenance, or make a values-level call. Governs: Architect-led. Timing: built first, devolved last — its first job is to resolve FTLAB's own contradictory terms. (This is the seam: in Option 2/3 this Head becomes the engineered Core; here it is a department, which is the honest limit of Option 1.)

5 · Quality & Guardianship (analogue: Chief Quality & Risk Officer) Owns: one accountable rigour gate (replacing scattered vetoes), methodology and output validation, AI governance, DIFC/DFSA compliance, Alignment-Debt tracking, and the institutional Guardian function. Charter: alone — review, sample, audit, flag, and halt outputs/agents that breach values or quality thresholds; escalate — values conflicts it cannot resolve; never — be overridden on a values breach by a revenue consideration. Governs: this is the Head the human Guardian governs directly, with halt-rights; it must be independent of P&L. Timing: among the last to devolve.

4. The human Cortex (the Edge)

The only humans. Per Ch 9 and framework §2.3, they hold three governance functions over the agent Heads — they do not sit inside them:

  • Shayan Kargarian — Managing Partner — lead Intent-Setter, holding Capital & Operations until it earns its own Head. Sets institutional intent and charters; allocates capital; governs the Venture Engine and Knowledge & Ecosystem.
  • Mehran Granfar — General Partner — lead Architect. Sets the research agenda and platform architecture; governs Platform & Agentic Systems and Coordination & Memory.
  • The Guardian carries no P&L — a hard rule (Book 1 Ch 9): the function that allocates capital cannot also be the function that halts on values grounds. So for any decision the Guardian is the founder without the capital/P&L stake in it — a capital decision made by Shayan as Intent-Setter is guarded by Mehran, and vice-versa — under a written conflict-of-interest protocol. Holding Capital & Operations inside a 2-person Edge sharpens this tension rather than relaxing it, so the move to externalise the halt-right to an independent director with a real veto is brought forward, not deferred. This is the inherited Critical (determination §7) — to be solved first, before the Edge holds both capital and the halt.

That is a 2–3 human Cortex governing five agent Heads — well within the business plan's Year-1 numbers.

5. How this differs from v0.3 — and where it stands

  • Seven → ~3 value-creating Engines + 2 departmentalised primitives. Research and Venture merge ("the ventures are the research") and consulting folds into the Venture Engine — that fixes one of the seven's two over-counts. But naming Coordination & Memory and Quality & Guardianship as departments repeats the other: they are the Core and the Edge, not faces. Five named Heads total, of which only three are Engines.
  • It is honestly leaner, matching the business plan's own Year-1 team (founders + engineering + ecosystem/BD + venture) far better than seven departments did.
  • Its standing in the determination is unchanged: Option 1 is the face, not the destination. The re-run makes why explicit. The department model must name Coordination & Memory and Quality & Guardianship as departments, because it has no engineered Core and no separated Edge to hold them. The determined architecture (Option 2/3) turns exactly those two into the Core (coordination/memory/orchestration) and the Edge (Guardianship + capital) — which is why, as the face worn over Option 3's Core, Option 1 reduces further to ~3 Engines (Venture Engine · Platform · Knowledge & Ecosystem). Standalone, the department model needs five seats; mounted on a real Core, three of them are faces and two have become substrate and governance.
  • The build-time ground rule does not apply to Option 1 — it has no Core to build. But that is the point: Option 1 avoids the Core's build by keeping coordination as a human-supervised memory department, which is precisely the limitation that makes it a starting face rather than the destination.

6. Summary

Re-run from the relevant Book 1 chapters (3, 4, 6, 8, 9), FTLAB's actual work, and the framework, Option 1 converges on the same ~3 value-creating Engines as the determination — the Venture Engine, Platform & Agentic Systems, and Knowledge & Ecosystem. As a pure department model it is forced to name two more — Coordination & Memory and Quality & Guardianship — and to hold Capital & Operations in the founder Edge: five named Heads in all. That very over-naming is the finding. The two seats beyond the three are exactly what the AI-born architecture turns into the Core and the Edge; declining to build them is what keeps Option 1 the legible face, not the destination. Run honestly, Option 1 is 3 Engines wearing department labels — and its only real choice is whether to keep coordination human-supervised (Option 1) or engineer it as a Core (Option 3). The re-run is leaner and more honest than the seven-Domain v0.3, and it matches the business plan's own team.
