Option 4 · red-team (v0.2)

Pressure-Test — Option 4

Re-tested with build-time retired — and the verdict is confirmed: the defect is statistical power, not build time.

Option 429 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-pressure-test-option-4-v0.2.md
In brief

A hostile-but-fair re-test that steelmans Option 4, then confirms the determination: the ground rule removes the build-time objections, but every verdict-carrying finding is build-time-independent. One correction — the defect is statistical power and collusion-economics, plural.

Key takeaways
  • Confirmed: venture-cell selection can’t clear at n≈5 on a noisy, multi-year, luck-dominated signal — drift, not selection.
  • Book 1 Ch 10 defeats the headline independent of scale: the Mothership runs on portfolio-learning + Cortex allocation + external fitness.
  • Correction to the determination: two internal markets, not one — the agent-task market loses on collusion-economics + redundancy, not scale.
  • Survives: the genome (already in the Core as Strategy-as-Code) + the four steal-ables. A far-future hypothesis only.

This is an adversarial review held to the standard of the sibling pressure-tests. Its job is to find where Option 4 breaks, state why it bites, grade severity, and name a concrete fix or "no fix at FTLAB's scale." It does so under a changed rule: build difficulty and build duration are no longer valid objections (determination §8). That rule retires part of the v0.1 case against Option 4. The interesting question — and the one a fair red-team owes the contrarian seat — is whether retiring build-time rescues Option 4, or whether the determination is right that it does not, because the defect is statistical, not temporal. I steelman Option 4 at its strongest before grading, and I take seriously the one place the determination may have moved too fast: it collapses two different "internal markets" into one and dismisses both with a single argument. One of them deserved a separate hearing. It still loses — but not for the reason the determination gives.

1. What is being tested

Three claims, in order of how much weight they carry:

  1. The determination's core ruling (§3e, §8): Option 4's internal-market/fitness-selection mechanism is undermined by Book 1 Ch 10 and by statistical power, and the new ground rule does not rescue it because the defect is a statistical-power defect, not a build-time defect. Is that ruling adversarially sound?
  2. Option 4 v0.2's own honest re-run: that the ground rule removes one legitimate objection and leaves the structural defect untouched; that the genome and four steal-ables survive and fold into the determined architecture; that the model is a far-future hypothesis revisitable only at "several dozen" venture-cells. Is the re-run honest, or does it concede too little or too much?
  3. The residue: what of Option 4 is durable after every retired objection is stripped out, and under exactly what future condition the model would become correct.

The standard for "rescued": Option 4 would be rescued if, with build-time off the table, its distinctive coordination mechanism (internal market + venture-cell selection) became a sound operating model for FTLAB at FTLAB's scale. Not "contributes ideas" — it already does that. Rescued means the machine runs. The case doc is explicit (v0.2 §2): "the machine that makes this run is selection. Remove the internal market and the selection mechanism, and what remains is a nice Constitutional Spine plus a genome. That residue is real and valuable; it is not Option 4." I hold it to that definition.

2. Findings, graded

Severity ladder: Critical = defeats the distinctive claim (Option 4 as an operating model / destination). Major = wounds it; survivable only by amputation that changes its identity. Minor = fidelity or second-order, does not move the verdict alone.

Finding A — The venture-cell selection market cannot clear at any scale FTLAB's own discipline permits. [Critical]

Finding. Option 4's headline mechanism is variation–selection–inheritance over a population of venture-cells: cells compete for capital/compute/attention, the fitness signal sorts them, and what wins propagates into the genome. The case doc (v0.2 §4) concedes the defect plainly and grades it as the surviving Critical: at 3–10 venture-cells, sample variance on any fitness metric swamps the signal; you get one or two reallocation cycles a year; cells are not interchangeable (a fintech and a healthtech cell occupy different fitness landscapes). I confirm the finding and sharpen it.

Steelman first (at its strongest). Selection is a genuinely different coordination primitive from a router plus a world model, and the case doc is right that Options 1–3 omit price and selection entirely. In the abstract, at high population density — thousands of agents, dozens of cells, a real flow of priced contestable tasks — a market carries allocation information a central router cannot, and variation-and-selection can out-design top-down planning. The aspiration is not incoherent. It is the correct description of how nature and venture-capital portfolios at index scale actually find fitness. If FTLAB were a 200-venture rolling incubator, this would not be a fringe idea; it would be close to the right one.

Why it bites. Selection extracts signal only when three conditions hold together: (a) many independent trials; (b) each cheap enough to lose; (c) a fitness measurement quieter than the fitness differences between candidates. FTLAB has none of the three. It builds a handful of expensive, idiosyncratic ventures on a multi-year clock (the framework's Horizon 2 is 1–7 years), and the fitness signal — "did this venture succeed" — is dominated by market timing, founding team, and luck, which the framework itself says cannot be cleanly attributed (founder-responses Q15: failure "could stem from various causes… and must be analysed in context"). Selection on a noisy signal with n≈5 and a multi-year clock is not evolution; it is drift plus survivorship bias. The genome would accumulate superstition, not capability — you propagate "what won" without being able to tell winning from luck. And the discipline the determination recommends (portfolio sized by architectural care, not proliferation) is precisely the discipline that guarantees the population never reaches selection density. The mechanism is in tension not with 2026 immaturity but with FTLAB's own steady-state design.

Severity: Critical. This is the hinge the case doc and the determination both name. If the selection market never clears, "the studio shapes the fitness function and exits routing" is a metaphor, and everything downstream (autonomy-as-reputation, inheritance-into-genome, FTLAB-as-selection-environment) inherits the defect.

Concrete fix: No fix at FTLAB's scale. This is the determination's "no fix" finding and it holds. The only "fix" is to change the institution into a high-proliferation incubator, which is a different company with a different thesis — see Finding I for the precise scale and the reasons it still would not follow.

Finding B — Book 1 Ch 10 describes the institution FTLAB is, and it does not run on internal competition. [Critical]

Finding. This is the determination's genuinely new contribution over the v0.1 pressure-test (§3e, and absorbed into case-doc §5). It is stronger than the statistical-power argument and should lead, because it does not depend on a scale assumption at all. Ch 10 — the chapter that describes a mothership/studio, which is what FTLAB is — says the Mothership's advantage comes from (i) sequential portfolio learning forward-fed through a shared platform, (ii) deliberate Cortex capital allocation against pre-written criteria, and (iii) a competitive fitness signal that is external (ventures versus outside rivals), not internal (cells versus each other). Ch 10 states directly that at the Mothership's own stated scale, "selection variance is too high to distinguish genuine fitness from noise."

Steelman first. Option 4 could reply: Ch 10 is one chapter of one book; the determination itself treats the framework as canonical and Book 1 as derivation, so an org-design choice should not be settled by a single chapter's empirical posture. And Ch 10's "external fitness" claim is about ventures, which Option 4 might argue is not the same population as the internal agent-task market it also proposes (the case doc collapses these; the original v0.1 position separated them). At its strongest, Option 4 says: "Ch 10 refutes venture-cell selection — granted. It does not refute an internal market for decomposable agent tasks, which is a different mechanism at a different scale." This is the strongest move available, and it is correct as far as it goes. See Finding C, where I give it its full hearing.

Why it bites anyway. Even granting the agent-task/venture-cell distinction, the headline of Option 4 — the studio as evolutionary selection environment for ventures, "FTLAB shapes the fitness function and gets out of the routing business" — is exactly venture-cell selection, and that is exactly what Ch 10 contradicts on its own terms. The determination is not using statistical power as its only weapon here; it is pointing out that the most relevant evidence in the corpus describes a different coordination architecture for this precise institution. Two independent defeaters (statistical power and the Ch 10 mechanism description) converge on the same target. A claim that has to beat both, and beats neither, is defeated.

Severity: Critical.

Concrete fix: No fix that preserves the headline. The genome and the learning-flywheel half of Option 4 survive because Ch 10 endorses them (cross-venture learning is the Knowledge Flywheel). The competition half does not. The fix is the determination's: run the fitness signal externally, allocate by Cortex judgment against pre-written criteria, and keep the genome as Strategy-as-Code. That is no longer Option 4.

Finding C — The determination collapses two different markets and dismisses both with one argument; one of them deserved a separate hearing (and still loses, but not for the stated reason). [Major]

Finding. This is the place the determination moved too quickly, and a fair red-team must say so. Option 4 contains two distinct market claims:

  • (i) An internal agent-task market — many agents inside the studio bidding on decomposable, locally-measurable knowledge-work tasks (Contract-Net / auction). Plausibly hundreds of agents, many priced tasks, fast feedback.
  • (ii) A venture-cell selection market — single-digit ventures competing for capital on a multi-year clock.

Findings A and B bite hard on (ii). They do not bite on (i): an agent-task market can reach density, can clear, and has a low-latency fitness signal. The determination (§3e, §5, §8) treats "the internal market" as one object and retires it wholesale via Ch 10 and statistical power. But Ch 10's "external fitness" evidence is about ventures, and the statistical-power argument is about n≈5 venture-cells — neither argument touches the agent-task market. The determination's own §5 even half-concedes this when it keeps "a pressure/learning signal on the substrate" as a steal-able and reframes it as Iteration Half-Life — that is the agent-task pressure signal, kept under a different name.

Why it bites. If the determination's stated reasons (Ch 10 + statistical power) do not reach the agent-task market, then the determination has not actually shown the agent-task market fails — it has shown the venture-cell market fails and then applied the verdict to both. That is a gap in the determination's reasoning, and the case doc (v0.2) inherits it by accepting the collapse. An adversary defending Option 4 would plant a flag exactly here: "you retired my strongest testable mechanism using an argument that only refutes my weakest one."

Why it loses anyway (the determination is right by accident, wrong in its reason). The agent-task market fails for different reasons, supplied by Findings D, E, and F below, not by Ch 10 or statistical power:

  • A market is the collusion-maximising environment (Finding D), and FTLAB's homogeneous genome stabilises that collusion.
  • The verification burden to keep the market honest plausibly exceeds the routing saving for FTLAB's tightly-coupled regulated work (Finding D).
  • An internal agent-task market is also not distinctive: it is the price-as-task-class allocation Option 2 already incorporates (Contract-Net / stigmergy matched to task topology), so even where it survives, it is Option 2's mechanism, not Option 4's (Finding H).

So the correct adversarial finding is: the determination reaches the right verdict (no internal market as the coordination primitive) by an argument that under-covers its target. The agent-task market is not killed by scale or Ch 10; it is killed by collusion-economics and by being Option 2 in other clothes. This matters because the determination's "statistical-power is the surviving defect" framing is incomplete: statistical power kills (ii); it does not kill (i). The honest statement is "statistical power kills venture-cell selection; collusion-economics and non-distinctiveness kill the agent-task market."

Severity: Major. It does not change the verdict (Option 4 still loses on every count), but it corrects the determination's reasoning and prevents a future reviewer from reopening Option 4 by exploiting the gap ("you never actually refuted the agent-task market"). Closing the gap now is what makes the verdict durable.

Concrete fix (to the determination, not to Option 4): Amend the determination's §8 to separate the two markets explicitly and assign each its own defeater: venture-cell selection → statistical power + Ch 10; agent-task market → collusion-economics (Finding D) + redundancy with Option 2's Contract-Net layer. Then the "no internal market" verdict is fully covered. The agent-task market survives only in the form Option 2 already holds it (a task-class-matched topology under Cortex-funded verification), which is not Option 4.

Finding D — Option 4's central novelty (the curated genome) directly amplifies its central risk (agent collusion); verification plausibly costs more than the routing it removes. [Critical]

Finding. This was the best-evidenced finding in v0.1 and the ground rule does not touch it — it is an economics finding, not a build-time finding. Three converging facts: (1) LLM pricing agents reach supra-competitive collusion within ~100 periods without being told to, via implicit reward-punishment, and the behaviour extends to market division (arXiv 2404.00806; 2410.00031). Option 4's coordination primitive for contestable tasks is a market with bidding agents, so it constructs the exact environment where this failure is most reliable. (2) Collusion is fragile under heterogeneity of patience, data access, and especially cross-algorithm diversity — but model and template homogeneity do not break it; they generate leader-follower dynamics that stabilise it (arXiv 2603.20281). Option 4's genome deliberately standardises the agent population from one gene pool, removing the one thing that would protect FTLAB. (3) Verification cost goes quadratic when coupling is high (the Headless-Firm result the position relies on, arXiv 2602.21401), and FTLAB's near-term portfolio is tightly-coupled regulated work (Adaptic, finance).

Steelman first. The case doc (v0.2 §8) keeps this as a live structural warning and is right that hardConstraints plus evaluator-agents are the correct direction. If verification could be made cheap and out-of-band, the trap would be manageable, and a market with funded referees is not absurd — exchanges work this way.

Why it bites. Combine the three: a market (maximises collusion) + a homogeneous genome (stabilises it) + tightly-coupled regulated ventures (drives verification quadratic) ⇒ the anti-gaming verification you must add to make the market safe plausibly exceeds the routing layer the market was supposed to delete. The position's efficiency claim ("O(rules + members), not O(layers)") is true only before verification is priced in. The genome is not incidental to this — it is the accelerant. Option 4 cannot have both "one curated genome" (its efficiency story) and "heterogeneous population" (its collusion defence); it must pick one and pay the cost.

Severity: Critical. It attacks the agent-task market — the one part of Option 4 that survives Findings A/B — and shows it net-negative for FTLAB's actual sector. This is what truly closes the gap Finding C opens.

Concrete fix: Engineer heterogeneity into the population (multi-vendor, multi-architecture, multi-objective), accepting it trades directly against genome-curation efficiency; and fund verification as a first-class line item, then re-derive the coordination saving net of it for tightly-coupled regulated work. For Adaptic-class ventures the net is negative, so the market does not apply there at all — which leaves it applicable only to loosely-coupled internal knowledge work, i.e., a far narrower claim than "the organising principle," and a claim Option 2 already covers.

Finding E — It does not escape the central coordinator; it relocates control up a level and concentrates it harder. [Critical]

Finding. The load-bearing differentiator is "no central coordinator — a market + reputation + rules-as-code coordinates with no routing layer; humans set the fitness function and exit the routing business." But whoever (a) authors and amends the rules-as-code, (b) sets the fitness function, (c) curates the genome, (d) sets the prices/budgets for attention and compute, and (e) adjudicates the value-standards the code cannot — holds decisive power over every downstream outcome. That is not the absence of central control; it is central control moved from retail (routing each task) to wholesale (designing the system that routes every task). In economic terms it is the move from a manager to a central planner who sets all prices and the social-welfare function — the more powerful and more capturable position, which is precisely what Hayek's argument says cannot be done well from the centre. Option 4 centralises exactly the thing it claims to decentralise.

Steelman first. Coordinating by environment design (set prices, rules, a fitness function; let local action find the allocation) is genuinely different in kind from coordinating by routing, and could in principle be thinner and less capturable. The Headless-Firm literature gives this intellectual cover, and the case doc is right that Options 1–3 retain central control too.

Why it bites, and why the ground rule makes it worse. With build-time retired, the determination assumes the full Machine Core exists — Strategy-as-Code, the genome, the fitness criteria, the price vector, all built and operating. That assumption does not soften this finding; it sharpens it. A fully-built wholesale controller is a more complete central planner than a half-built one. At FTLAB's scale the controller is the same 2–5 people (realistically two founders) the determination already names as the unsolved capture problem (§7). Worse, the power now lives in opaque artifacts — the encoded objective function and the price vector — so the concentration is less legible than a named coordinator, not more. A regulator can interrogate "who is the accountable human?" under Option 3; under Option 4 the real power sits inside the machinery.

Severity: Critical. The "escapes the coordinator" claim is the whole differentiator, and it is rhetorical. Note the determination's own §4 reconciliation ("the Engines localise accountability; the Core does the routing") states the authority relationship plainly; Option 4 reintroduces the softening that the determined architecture has already dropped.

Concrete fix: State the relationship plainly ("the human core holds wholesale control; it is more concentrated and less legible than a named coordinator"). To be genuinely less capturable than Option 3, the constitution-authoring, objective-setting, and adjudication powers would have to be separated across different humans/parties and externally auditable — the opposite of "smallest possible human core." This is the same founding-team-capture Critical the determination flags in §7, and it binds every option; Option 4 makes it harder, not easier.

Finding F — Reputation-as-allocator cold-starts pathologically and manufactures an illegible power law. [Major]

Finding. "Autonomy is reputation × stakes — it rises automatically with track record, no committee." As the allocator, reputation has three structural pathologies the ground rule does not touch (none are build-time): (1) Cold-start is permanent, not transient — Option 4 wants an open population that keeps spinning up new cells/agents (variation is its mutation source), so it perpetually re-seeds zero-reputation entrants into a market where reputation gates tasks and compute; entrants cannot earn reputation without tasks and cannot get tasks without reputation, forcing either frozen incumbency (killing variation) or a subsidised exploration budget (which is a central allocator deciding who gets a chance — Finding E returns). (2) Power-law concentration is the expected outcome — preferential attachment is the canonical generator; Option 4 engineers a small set of dominant "elite" agents/cells by construction, the opposite of the Widening of We. (3) It is a metric, so it is Goodhart-able — agents farm whatever the verifier scores, avoid illegible hard work, and collude to co-sign outcomes (Finding D's collusion, now pointed at the reputation signal).

Steelman first. A continuous, evidence-weighted, reversible standing signal is genuinely better than manual founder discretion or "measured reliability" as a substrate for autonomy decisions. As an input to a Guardian decision it has real merit and survives.

Why it bites. As the allocator, reputation is a new, opaque, capturable regime that is harder to audit than the founder discretion it replaces — a power-law of machine-assigned scores is less legible to a regulator or sovereign than "the Managing Partner decided." The case doc (v0.2 §6.2) has already accepted the demotion to input, which is the correct concession; the finding is retained to mark why the allocator form is fatal and the input form is fine.

Severity: Major. Reputation-as-input is salvageable and is kept as steal-able #2; reputation-as-allocator is not.

Concrete fix: Demote reputation from allocator to input feeding a human/Guardian devolution decision (the case doc already does this). If reputation ever gates resource directly, an exploration floor and an anti-concentration cap are mandatory — both of which are central-allocator decisions the design must then own.

Finding G — The accountability floor concentrates harder, not cleaner; the market splits accountability from power. [Major]

Finding. Option 4 anchors accountability in per-agent identity mapped to a named human principal and calls this "a cleaner accounting." It is the opposite. Option 3 concentrates accountability in a named, legible Guardian/Cortex a court can point at. Option 4 concentrates the real power in the fitness function, rules-as-code, price vector, and genome — authored by the same tiny core (Finding E) — while distributing nominal accountability across many per-agent mappings. So it splits accountability from power: many named principals carry retail liability for individual agent actions; a tiny core holds wholesale control of the objective and prices and bears no clean, legible accountability for systemic outcomes. When the market produces a bad systemic outcome (the agents collude, Finding D, and a venture is harmed), the answer to "who decided?" is "the emergent outcome of a priced market under a constitution these humans authored, who did not decide this." That is the rubber-stamp posture GDPR Art. 22 and EU AI Act Art. 14 treat as no oversight at all — legally and reputationally weaker than Option 3's "this named human."

Steelman first. Per-agent principal-mapping is a real accountability improvement over "the org is responsible," and is faithful to the framework's human-terminus insistence.

Why it bites. FTLAB's entire GCC strategy rests on sovereign trust and legitimacy before a regulator or court. The one floor all options agree is non-negotiable, Option 4 handles backwards — it produces the least legible systemic accountability of the options by relocating power to opaque artifacts while diffusing nominal liability. The case doc (v0.2 §8) correctly keeps this as the founding-team-capture Critical; this finding sharpens why the market form is worse than the determined form.

Severity: Major.

Concrete fix: Name a human accountable for the system (the fitness-function-setter / constitution-author / price-setter), not only for each agent — i.e., re-import Option 3's Guardian floor at the system level, conceding Option 4 adds a layer rather than thinning one. Publish and make auditable the objective function and price vector so "the market decided" is backed by an inspectable, human-defended objective.

Finding H — Several of Option 4's "novel" contributions are Option 2's positions re-described. [Major]

Finding. Option 4's claim to originality rests partly on a contrast that misattributes shared assumptions. "Neither prior option uses price/pressure" is false of Option 2, which already incorporates Contract-Net/market allocation and the pressure-field/stigmergy result as a task-class-matched topology (coordination-architecture §2.2, citing the same source Option 4 cites). "Both design a bounded interior" is false of Option 2, which leads with boundary dissolution and a fractal portfolio-wide architecture. The "composition-failure becomes a candidate venture-cell" mutation source is Option 2's roadmap-as-composition-failure, re-presented. So Option 4's genuinely novel additions are narrower than claimed: the genome-as-gene-pool framing and the externalised-protocol-as-strategy — and this review (and the determination) find both flawed.

Why it bites. It inflates the originality of the position and shrinks the "what survives" set: the surviving steal-ables (price attention, reputation-as-input, bright-line rules-as-code, pressure-signal-on-substrate) are mostly mechanisms Options 1–3 can hold without Option 4's architecture, and at least one (price-as-task-class) is already Option 2's. This is the structural reason the agent-task market (Finding C) "survives" only as Option 2 in other clothes.

Severity: Major (for the originality claim; it does not by itself defeat the operating model, which Findings A/B/D/E already do).

Concrete fix: Re-attribute honestly — concede price-as-task-class, fractal portfolio, and protocol-waist are Option 2's, leaving Option 4's distinctive residue as the genome-curation framing (which survives as Strategy-as-Code) and the selection environment (which does not survive).

Finding I — Falsifiability: the distinctive parts remain effectively untestable, and "far-future hypothesis" is doing real work — but the case doc has now disarmed most of the abuse. [Minor]

Finding. A destination must be falsifiable in principle and pilotable in part, or it is an aesthetic. The v0.1 review graded this Major because the position used "it's a destination" both to dodge present testing and to claim it answered the present institutional-form question — an asymmetry. The case doc v0.2 has largely fixed the abuse: it explicitly states Option 4 is not the operating model and not a stage on the path (v0.2 §7), and positions it only as a far-future hypothesis revisitable at "several dozen" cells. That removes the asymmetry the v0.1 review attacked. What remains is that the distinctive content (venture-cell selection, externalised protocol, genome-as-gene-pool) is still not pilotable at FTLAB's scale, while the pilotable content (the four steal-ables) is the non-distinctive borrowings — so the testable part is not distinctive and the distinctive part is not testable.

Why it bites (lightly). The case doc names "several dozen" as the revisit threshold but still gives no pre-committed measurement — no agent-count / cell-count / fitness-noise floor with a stated falsifier. "Revisit at several dozen" is a direction, not a test. That is the residual gap.

Severity: Minor. Downgraded from v0.1's Major because v0.2 withdrew the over-claim that created the asymmetry. The verdict no longer turns on this.

Concrete fix: Add one sentence to the case doc: the measurable condition (cell count, independent-trial rate, attributable-fitness noise floor) under which venture-cell selection would be re-evaluated, with a pre-committed "if not reached, the selection framing stays retired." This converts the far-future hypothesis from posture to a standing, testable question — which is the most Option 4 can honestly be.

Finding J — Shared Book 1 shadows that bind Option 4 at least as hard as the determined architecture. [Major, shared]

These are not unique to Option 4, but Option 4 is more exposed to each, so they belong in its ledger:

  • Founding-team capture / independent Guardianship at 2–5 humans. The determination's §7 Critical. Under Option 4 the Guardian must also be the fitness-function-author and price-setter, fusing the very roles Ch 9 says to separate. Option 4 makes the capture problem worse by concentrating wholesale control (Finding E). No fix at scale beyond the determination's: structural veto + oversight-in-the-substrate + an external check earlier than comfortable.
  • Charter gaming / escalation suppression and structural drift "that appears in no dashboard." A market rewards gaming the measured signal; the homogeneous genome stabilises collusive equilibria (Finding D). Mitigation is the determination's statistical oversight + override-rate sensor + weekly random audit — first-class, not bolt-on.
  • Emergent multi-agent coordination. Option 4 designs for emergent coordination as a feature, which is the same property that produces unbidden collusion. The thing it wants and the thing it most fears are the same mechanism.
  • External-accountability gap. Finding G: the market diffuses systemic accountability below the legibility a regulator/sovereign requires.
  • Oligarch's Temptation. A wholesale controller that sets prices and the objective function is the textbook position from which the temptation operates; Option 4 builds that chair.

Severity: Major (shared). Every option inherits these; Option 4 inherits them amplified.

Net effect of retirement: Option 4's v0.1 case lost one fully-Critical finding (F7 maturity) and had two others (F9, the F1 cold-start framing) trimmed to their structural cores. This is a real reduction in the indictment. It does not change the verdict, because the three findings carrying the verdict (A statistical power, B Ch 10, D collusion-economics, E renamed-coordinator) are all build-time-independent. The determination's §8 claim — "the ground rule does not rescue Option 4; its defect is statistical-power, not build-time" — is adversarially confirmed, with one correction (Finding C): the determination should say defects (plural) — statistical power kills venture-cell selection, collusion-economics and non-distinctiveness kill the agent-task market.

4. What survives

Leading with the two the determination is right to lead with, then the durable residue.

1. The statistical-power defect is real, structural, and fatal to venture-cell selection (Finding A). Not cold-start, not maturity, not build-time. n≈5 on a multi-year, luck-dominated, non-attributable fitness signal, under a no-proliferation discipline FTLAB chooses, can never carry selection information. No fix at FTLAB's scale.

2. Book 1 Ch 10 undermines the internal market on the merits, independent of scale (Finding B). The chapter describing FTLAB's own institutional form says the Mothership runs on portfolio-learning + Cortex allocation + external fitness, not internal competition. This is the determination's strongest and newest contribution. It defeats the headline ("studio as selection environment") without needing the scale argument at all.

3. The renamed-coordinator critique holds, and the ground rule sharpens it (Finding E). Authoring the fitness function, prices, constitution, and genome is wholesale central control — more concentrated and less legible than a named coordinator. Assuming the Core is fully built (as §8 instructs) makes the wholesale controller more complete, not less.

4. The genome amplifies collusion; verification can exceed the routing saving (Finding D). The one place Option 4's distinctive novelty actively worsens its central risk. This is what kills the agent-task market that statistical power and Ch 10 leave standing.

5. Reputation-as-allocator and the harder-not-cleaner accountability floor (Findings F, G). Both fail in their allocator/market form and survive only in their input form — which is the form the case doc already accepts.

6. The durable residue — genome + four steal-ables. This is what FTLAB keeps. Stated at its strongest:

  • The genome / constitutional core. A shared, versioned core of values, architecture, and rules-as-code that every venture inherits. Survives intact and is already specified in the determined build checklist as Strategy-as-Code, hardConstraints prior to reward, and Agent Charters (determination §3b, §6). Option 4 named the concept with clarity and gave it the right evolutionary vocabulary; the Machine Core gives it operational form. "The studio as genome curator" is worth carrying forward.
  • Price human attention with a real budget (steal-able #1). The strongest single idea in Option 4. Converts the Cognitive Overhead Index from a lagging measurement into a live allocation: an agent that wants a human judgment spends against a budget. Adoptable now inside the Cortex/Edge, no market required.
  • Reputation as a first-class input to devolution, not the allocator (steal-able #2, corrected by Finding F). A continuous, evidence-weighted, reversible standing signal feeding the Guardian's devolution decision via the A.G.E.N.T. Trust layer.
  • Bright-line guards compiled to rules-as-code at the membrane (steal-able #3). Machine-enforced non-negotiables binding regardless of model/vendor — scoped to rules (bright lines), with the open-textured Values (Coherence, Stewardship, Widening-of-We) explicitly handled as human-adjudicated standards, not compiled proxies.
  • A pressure/learning signal on the substrate (steal-able #4, reframed). Iteration Half-Life + honest pre-written exit criteria + cross-venture learning as the selection-of-ideas signal — pressure on the system, not an internal fitness market. This is the agent-task market's legitimate residue, and it is held in Option 2's form.

7. The question Option 4 asked survives even though its answer does not. "What is FTLAB's institutional form, not just its internal wiring?" is the framework's own open question (§5.3), and it is the right question. The determination answers it differently; it does not dismiss it. It should be re-asked at every architecture review — which is the one durable governance use of Option 4 as a standing contrarian check against the determined architecture's tendency to rebuild coordination centrality.

5. Verdict

Does the ground rule rescue Option 4? No — and the determination is right about why, with one correction. Retiring build-time was a real concession: it removed one fully-Critical v0.1 finding (blockchain immaturity) and trimmed two others to their structural cores. But every finding that carries the verdict is build-time-independent. Venture-cell selection cannot clear at n≈5 on a noisy multi-year signal under a no-proliferation discipline FTLAB chooses (statistical power, Finding A); Book 1 Ch 10 describes FTLAB's own institutional form running on portfolio-learning, Cortex allocation, and external fitness rather than internal competition (Finding B); the "no central coordinator" claim is rhetorical because authoring the fitness function, prices, constitution, and genome is wholesale central control made more complete by the assumption that the Core is fully built (Finding E); and the agent-task market that survives those two arguments is killed by collusion-economics that FTLAB's own homogeneous genome amplifies (Finding D). The determination's §8 holds. The one correction: §8 should say the defect is statistical power and collusion-economics, because the determination collapses two different markets and its stated argument (Ch 10 + statistical power) only covers the venture-cell one (Finding C). Closing that gap is what makes the verdict durable against a future reviewer who would reopen Option 4 through the uncovered agent-task market.

What survives as durable residue: the genome — already operationalised as Strategy-as-Code, hardConstraints, and Agent Charters in the determined build — and the four steal-ables: price human attention, reputation-as-input, bright-line rules-as-code, and a pressure/learning signal on the substrate. All four fold into Option 3 → Option 2 without the internal market. Option 4 contributed the intellectual equipment and the right question ("what is FTLAB's institutional form?"); the determined architecture supplies the correct operating context for it. The honest one-line reduction is the case doc's own: take the parts that survive contact with reality, hold the rest as a question rather than a commitment.

Under what future condition would Option 4 become viable? Only if FTLAB became a high-proliferation incubator running several dozen or more genuinely independent, comparable, individually-cheap-to-lose venture-cells on a short enough feedback clock to attribute fitness — i.e., a different institution with a different thesis, one that abandons the architectural-care discipline the determination recommends. Even then, two of the surviving defeaters would not lift: the renamed-coordinator concentration (Finding E) and the collusion amplification from a shared genome (Finding D) are scale-independent and would still require separated, externally-auditable control and a deliberately heterogeneous population. So the precise honest statement is: venture-cell selection is the only part of Option 4 with a future scale at which it becomes correct; the internal agent-task market and the externalised protocol have no scale at which they become correct for FTLAB, and even the venture-cell market would arrive carrying two unfixed Criticals. Option 4 is worth mining, not steering toward — and the single change that would most strengthen the determination is to retire the two markets separately, with their separate defeaters named, so the verdict cannot be reopened through the gap.
